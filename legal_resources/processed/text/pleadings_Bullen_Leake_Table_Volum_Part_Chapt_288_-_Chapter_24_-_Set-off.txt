Chapter 24 - Set-off
24-01
Set-off is a method of adjusting cross claims to monetary relief. In the absence of lien or some form of real security, an entitlement
to set-off does not give one party the right to detain the property or assets of the other as a “set-off” against the other’s monetary
claims. 1
Where a claimant claims a sum of money against a defendant who has a monetary cross-claim, such that the defendant is entitled,
to the extent of his cross-claim, to be relieved from payment of the claim and to set it up as a defence in the claimant’s action,
the defendant is exercising a right of set-off to the extent of his cross-claim.
The defendant’s claim may be for an unascertained amount. CPR Pt 16.6 does not limit the right of set-off to ascertained sums.
The application is, however, limited to money claims and operates as a defence to the claimant’s action, thereby rendering the
defendant no longer liable to the obligation on the claim. Notwithstanding the broad terms of CPR Pt 16.6, the courts do not
classify a set-off as a monetary cross-claim raised by a defendant to the claimant’s claim, but continue to identify set-offs under
well-established headings such as legal and equitable set-offs. 2
With contracts for the sale of goods and for work done, the Sale of Goods Act 1979 permits a defendant to raise a breach of
warranty as a defence to a claim for the price. In this case the defendant is not strictly setting off his claim at all but exercising
a right to an abatement of the price provided that he establishes that the seller’s breach has caused him damage.
Note that Re Kauping Singer v Friedlander Ltd (In Administration) was overturned by the Supreme Court on the provisions
of insolvency law.
A contractual clause excluding a right of set-off may, in an appropriate case, be avoided under the Unfair Contract Terms Act
1977. 3
In the relatively unlikely event of a set-off being asserted by a trader in a consumer contract or the somewhat more likely event
of a right of set off being excluded by a trader in such a contract, the fairness of the term would now be judged under Pt 2 of
the Consumer Rights Act 2015. It is at least arguable that a clause excluding a set off would fall foul of para.18 of Sch.2 to the
2015 Act—the so-called “Grey List” of terms which are presumed to be unfair until refuted by the trader.
In practice, a defence of set-off will nearly always be accompanied by a counterclaim. The right to set up a counterclaim,
however, is much wider than the right of set-off and many counterclaims will often end the defence section of the defence and
counterclaim with a plea of set-off: if, in the event, no set-off of the counterclaim is possible, it is very unlikely that its inclusion
in the defence will have added to the costs of the action.
