Chapter 18 - Undue Influence
18-01
The equitable doctrine of undue influence is a jurisdiction which extends to gifts, settlements, contracts, guarantees and
securities. It extends to all cases where “influence is acquired and abused, where confidence is reposed and betrayed” 1  at
the time of the impugned transaction. 2  The doctrine “arises whenever one party has acted unconscionably by exploiting the
influence to direct the conduct of another which he has obtained from the relationship between them”. 3  The question which
the court asks is not whether the party influenced knew what he or she was doing, had done or was proposing to do, but how the
intention was produced. 4  The touchstone for whether an intention was produced through an exercise of improper or ‘undue’
influence is whether “the consent thus procured ought not fairly to be treated as the expression of a person’s free will”. 5  Where
the plea of undue influence is made out, a transaction which has been procured by the undue influence of one party over another
is voidable at the instance of the party influenced.
A claim to avoid a transaction on the ground of undue influence constitutes a cause of action in itself and can also be relied
upon as a defence to a claim to enforce the transaction which was so procured.
The court will also refuse to admit to probate a will, the execution of which is proved to have been procured by actual undue
influence. For a full exposition on the law of undue influence see, for example Chitty on Contracts, Vol.1, para.8–058 onwards 6
and Halsbury’s Laws, Vol.22, paras 294 onwards.
The doctrine of undue influence
18-02
The principle laid down in Barclays Bank Plc v O’Brien 7  was clarified and extended by the House of Lords in Royal Bank
of Scotland v Etridge (No.2). 8  The O’Brien principle is that where a lender takes a third party security from the spouse of (or
other co-habitee with the principal debtor), the lender will, unless certain steps are taken, be on notice of the possibility that the
principal debtor used undue influence or misrepresentation to obtain the surety’s consent to giving the security. Therefore, if the
lender fails to take appropriate steps at the time of taking the security, and it later transpires that the spouse (or other surety) was
misled or coerced into providing the security, the security will be voidable at the instance of the surety against the bank or lender.
The O’Brien principle was extended beyond only co-habitees by the decision in Etridge. Lord Nicholls made clear (at [87])
that in the case of suretyship, the lender will be “… ‘put on inquiry’ in every case where the relationship between the surety
and the debtor is non-commercial. The creditor must always take reasonable steps to bring home to the individual guarantor
the risks he is running by standing as surety.”
The steps that should be taken by a lender once it has been “put on inquiry” were set out by Lord Nicholls in Etridge, at [50]–
[57]. This passage should be read in full, as should Lord Scott’s speech at [163]ff. In summary, however, “Ordinarily it will
be reasonable that a bank should be able to rely upon confirmation from a solicitor, acting for a wife, that he has advised the
wife appropriately” (per Lord Nicholls at [56]). 9

The Etridge line of cases involves a three-party situation, the question most commonly being whether a creditor is affected by
undue influence exercised by the principal debtor on the surety. One should not of course lose sight of the simpler two-party
situation, in which a party’s consent to a transaction is procured by undue influence.
A recent banking case in which a two-party situation was alleged is The Libyan Investment Authority v Goldman Sachs
International. 10  The claim made by Libya’s sovereign wealth fund (the LIA) was that Goldman Sachs had used undue influence
to procure its entry into a series of high-value synthetic derivative transactions through January to April 2008, for which the
LIA paid a total premium to Goldman Sachs of about US $1.2 billion. The claim was put on two bases: that Goldman Sachs
had procured the LIA’s consent to the transactions by (a) offering an “improper inducement” to a senior LIA employee, and/or
(b) exploiting a “protected relationship” with the unsophisticated and reliant LIA. Mrs Justice Rose rejected the LIA’s claims
in a judgment that offers a comprehensive summary of the principles of undue influence (see [132] onwards) and an illustrative
consideration of when a relationship of dependence might arise between a bank and its client.
It is also important to remember that actual undue influence can arise even without a pre-existing relationship of trust and
confidence, if threats, menaces or coercion is shown (see Holyoake v Candy, 11  where it was noted that such a plea is likely to
overlap very significantly with an allegation of duress), or where there is evidence of dependence or vulnerability. 12
Classes of undue influence
18-03
It is clear from the judgments in Etridge that the system of classification into “classes” of undue influence should be abandoned:
see, for example, per Lord Clyde at [92]. In its place, the following analysis, derived from Lord Nicholls’ speech in Etridge
at [13]ff. is suggested.
In broad terms, equity identified two forms of unacceptable conduct. The first “… comprises acts of improper pressure or
coercion such as unlawful threats”. The second, “… arises out of a relationship between two persons where one has acquired
over another a measure of influence, or ascendancy, of which the ascendant person then takes unfair advantage.” 13  In both
cases, the touchstone of undue influence was whether “the consent thus procured ought not fairly to be treated as the expression
of a person’s free will”. 14  There is an open question as to whether an “improper inducement” such as a bribe—and as distinct
from improper threats—might amount to “unacceptable means”. This issue was raised but not decided in the case of Libyan
Investment Authority v Goldman Sachs International in which the LIA alleged that Goldman Sachs had procured the LIA’s
consent to a series of derivative trades by offering an improper inducement to a senior LIA employee (the offer of a Goldman
Sachs internship to his younger brother). The court declined to decide the legal point as it found that the internship offer was
not improper, and would not realistically have influenced the LIA’s investment decision in any event. 15
The general rule is that the burden of proving an allegation of undue influence rests upon the person who claims to have
been wronged. Proof that the complainant placed trust and confidence in the other party in relation to the management of the
complainant’s financial affairs coupled with a transaction which calls for an explanation will usually discharge the burden of
proof. In other words there is a rebuttable evidential presumption of undue influence—this is the equitable analogue of the
common law principle of res ipsa loquitur. To raise the rebuttable presumption, the complainant must prove that he placed trust
and confidence 16  in the other party and there must be a transaction which calls for an explanation. 17
The above is to be “distinguished sharply” from a different presumption. This arises where a relationship between A and B is
of a class such that “… the law presumes, irrebuttably, that one party had influence over the other. The complainant need not
prove he actually reposed trust and confidence in the other party. It is sufficient for him to prove the existence of the type of
relationship.” It is well settled that the relationship between spouses does not fall into this category. Relationships to which the
principle does apply (so long as there is or has recently been a relationship conferring a power analogous to parental control)
are those between parent and child 18 ; guardian and ward 19 ; uncle and niece 20 ; stepfather and stepdaughter 21 ; stepmother
and stepdaughter. 22  Obviously, it is highly unlikely that the courts today would apply a different assumption depending on the
sex of either party. Other relationships include solicitor and client 23 ; doctor and patient 24 ; trustee and beneficiary 25 ; religious
adviser and a person to whom he gives his advice. 26

3
Although there is no presumption in the second sense in the case of the relationship between spouses, the court will “… note,
as a matter of fact, the opportunities for abuse which flow from a wife’s confidence in her husband”, and there seems no reason
not to apply the same principle depending on the sex of the spouse(s).
In the case of Hewitt v First Plus Financial Group Plc 27  the concealment of an affair by the husband at the time of proposing
that the wife enter into a risky financial transaction was considered to amount to undue influence, because the wife was entitled
to be apprised of all relevant circumstances. The finding of undue influence did not depend on the conclusion that the wife
had made no decision of her own or that her will and intention was completely overborne. Conscious exercise of will can be
vitiated by the undue influence.
Contract calling for an explanation
18-04
In cases where actual undue influence has been shown, the party influenced is entitled to a remedy 28  unless the right to rescind
has been lost (for example) by affirmation or impossibility of restitution or change of position.
The former requirement that, for either presumption above to apply, there must be manifest disadvantage to the complainant has
now been re-formulated; in particular, the phrase “manifest disadvantage” should not be employed. 29  The proper approach is
“…to adhere more directly to the test outlined by Lindley LJ in Allcard v Skinner, 36 Ch. D. 145, and adopted by Lord Scarman
in National Westminster Bank Plc v Morgan [1985] A.C. 686.” Lord Nicholls quoted from Lindley LJ’s judgment (at 185): “…
if the gift is so large as not to be reasonably accounted for on the ground of friendship, relationship, charity, or other ordinary
motives on which ordinary men act, the burden is upon the donee to support the gift.” And from Lord Scarman’s speech (at 704):
the transaction must have “… constituted an advantage taken of the person subjected to the influence which, failing proof to the
contrary, was explicable only on the basis that undue influence had been exercised to procure it”. The phrase employed by Lord
Nicholls was a transaction which “calls for an explanation”. 30  An example of the Court of Appeal correcting the application
of the former, incorrect test can be found in Macklin v Dowsett. 31
It should be noted that, after Etridge, all non-commercial contracts of suretyship fall into something of a special category, for
in all such cases the creditor “… must always take reasonable steps to bring home to the individual guarantor the risks he is
running by standing as surety.” If the creditor fails to do that, “… it is deemed to have notice of any claim the guarantor may
have that the transaction was procured by undue influence or misrepresentation on the part of the debtor.” 32
The effect of independent legal advice
18-05
In order to rebut the presumption of undue influence, evidence must be adduced to satisfy the court that the donor was acting
independently of any influence from the donee and with a full appreciation of what he or she was doing. 33  The most usual,
though not the only way of rebutting the presumption, is to prove that the complainant had competent and independent advice. 34
The fact that the complainant received independent legal advice does not of itself show that the transaction was free of undue
influence: whether the proper inference is that the advice had an emancipating effect is a question of fact to be decided having
regard to all the evidence: see Etridge, per Lord Nicholls, at [20]. 35
However, it is not invariably necessary for independent legal advice to have been given. 36  For an example of an (unsuccessful)
attempt to argue that the evidential presumption should be rebutted on the basis that the making of the gift was due to the free
exercise of will on the part of the donor, see Hammond v Osborn. 37  See too Goodchild v Bradbury, 38  where the donee was
unable to rebut the evidential presumption notwithstanding the fact that the donor had stated in his witness statement that he
did not think that he had come under any pressure from the donee.

In the surety-wife cases, independent legal advice is critically relevant to whether the lender has constructive notice of any

Position of third parties
18-06
It is essential to bear in mind that the lender (or other third party) will only be affected by the exercise of undue influence over
the complainant if it had actual or constructive notice of that undue influence. In many cases, the lender will have had no actual
notice of the undue influence. In such cases, to make out constructive notice, it is necessary to plead and prove that the lender
was put on inquiry 39  that there may have been some equitable wrong. In Mortgage Agency Services (No.2) Ltd v Chater, 40
Scott Baker LJ put the test this way (at [57]): “… the court is not concerned with what was actually happening between mother
and son but with what the bank knew about it or, by making appropriate inquiries, ought to have known about it. … What is
the information that was available to the respondent? That is the crucial question? Was it sufficient to put it on inquiry as to
some equitable wrong?”
Further, in mortgage cases, it should be noted that even if the charge over the whole property fails due to undue influence exerted
by one party (usually the husband) over the other (usually the wife), the charge may still take effect as an equitable charge over
the husband’s interest: see s.63 of the Law of Property Act 1925. 41
A replacement or substitute mortgage with the same lender would be voidable if it was inseparably connected with an earlier
mortgage that was voidable for undue influence and of which the lender had constructive notice. 42
The peculiar status of contracts of suretyship should be noted: if the creditor fails to take the “reasonable steps” set out in Etridge
(see Lord Nicholls’ speech at [50]ff.), then if the complainant’s consent was procured by the exercise of undue influence or
misrepresentation by the principal debtor, the contract of suretyship will be voidable as against the creditor. 43
In relation to independent legal advice, that the bank knows that a solicitor acts for the spouse is not sufficient to avoid it being
fixed with constructive notice of any undue influence—rather, the bank must have proper grounds for believing that the solicitor
has properly advised the spouse (whether he has or has not, in fact); conversely, absent special circumstances, it is enough that
the bank knows that the spouse’s solicitor has been instructed to give him/her independent legal advice as to the nature and effect
of the transaction and has received confirmation that such advice has been given: see Achampong (above) and Etridge at [79].
The burden of proof
18-07
In the three party-situation, such as the surety spouse situation, it will usually be necessary for a spouse (or other complainant) to
plead and to prove that the bank had constructive notice in O’Brien type cases, rather than for the bank to disprove constructive
notice. 44
However, there is no distinction between facts that are said to put a bank on inquiry in a case of an abuse of a relationship of
trust and confidence which would not equally put it on inquiry in the case of actual undue influence. 45
In the two-party situation, and as set out at para.18-03, there are classes of relationship that give rise to an irrebuttable
presumption of influence at law, such as doctor-patient and parent-child relationships. Outside those specified relationships,
however, a presumption of undue influence is merely a rebuttable evidential presumption. As Lord Scott in Etridge said at [219]:

“It is a presumption which arises if the nature of the relationship of the two parties coupled with the nature of the

sufficient additional evidence to rebut the presumption …. In an appropriate case the presumption may carry the
complainant home.”
Allegations of actual undue influence (such as improper coercion) must simply be made out on the balance of probabilities.
Affirmation
18-08
A transaction entered into as a result of undue influence is voidable and not void. The right to rescind on the ground of undue
influence may be lost either by express affirmation 46  of the transaction by the alleged victim, 47  or by estoppel, or by delay
amounting to proof of acquiescence. 48  Estoppel requires a clear and unequivocal representation that the claimant would not
seek to set the agreement aside which is intended to be acted on and in fact acted on by the other party to his detriment or in
such a way that it would be inequitable to allow the claimant to go back on that representation. 49  The affirmation must in any
event take place after the influence has ceased if it is to be of any value. Lapse of time in itself does not constitute a bar to
relief 50  but it will provide evidence of acquiescence if the victim fails to take any steps to set aside the transaction within a
reasonable time after he is freed from the undue influence. 51
For the possible effects of seeking inconsistent remedies (spouse’s taking of transfer of husband’s share of property coupled with
continuing to advance an undue influence claim), see First National Bank Plc v Walker 52  (though see also Bank of Scotland
v Hussain). 53
Agency
18-09
In Barclays Bank v O’Brien it was established that the doctrine of constructive notice avoids the need for the somewhat strained
approach used in the earlier cases where the creditor was constituted as agent of the wrongdoer in order to get the surety’s
signature on the transaction. Thus there may still be cases in which the creditor is responsible for the husband’s actions because
it can be said on the facts that the husband was acting as agent of the creditor, but such cases will be rare. 54  After Etridge, in
most cases the creditor will be protected if the wife is already dealing with the bank through her solicitor or the bank has either
advised the wife to take independent advice or complied with the principles set out in Etridge (see above).
Etridge made it very clear that in the great majority of cases, the knowledge of the solicitor advising the wife cannot be attributed
to the bank because the wife’s solicitor is not the bank’s agent although the bank may have asked the solicitor to advise the
wife: see per Lord Nicholls, at [77]–[78].
Lord Scott considered the question of s.199(1)(ii)(b) of the Law of Property Act 1925 in Etridge at [176] ff. Where the solicitor
is instructed by both the bank and the wife, a request by the bank to advise the wife is irrelevant, and when advising the wife, the
solicitor is not the bank’s solicitor and so s.199(1)(ii)(b) is of no application. However, if the solicitor never becomes the wife’s
solicitor, the solicitor is acting for the bank alone, and the solicitor’s knowledge that the wife has not been properly advised as
to the security documentation may be attributed to the bank.

Pleading points
18-10
If the allegation is that the conduct complained of amounted to acts of “improper pressure or coercion”, or potentially, to
improper inducement, 55  it is incumbent on the person alleging that actual undue influence to plead and prove it.
This may be done by showing that there was actual coercion by the done in the form of illegitimate pressure of some kind.
It may also be shown by proving that the stronger party exercised such a degree of domination or control over the mind of
the weaker party that the latter’s independence of decision was substantially undermined. 56  The party relying on such a plea
must show that 57 :
 
(1)the other party to the transaction had the capacity to influence the complainant;
 
(2)the influence was exercised;
 
(3)its exercise was undue; and
 
(4)its exercise has “some causal connection between the undue influence alleged and the complainant’s decision to enter
the impugned transaction” (albeit it is not required to be the principal reason or cause). 58  The Court of Appeal has stated
that the causal link to the transaction that is required to make out an allegation of undue influence is that “the equitable
wrong constituted by the abuse of confidence was part of the process by which the victim’s consent to it was obtained. 59
In contrast, in cases where the presumption of undue influence is alleged to arise, the complainant must plead and prove either:
 
(1)the existence of a relationship which, as a matter of law, gives rise to the presumption and a transaction which “calls
for an explanation”; or
 
(2)the facts necessary to establish the relationship of trust and confidence and a transaction which “calls for an explanation”.
As against a third party creditor, rather than as against the wrongdoer, the burden of proof lies on the person alleging constructive
notice on the part of the creditor or bank to plead and prove that the creditor or bank had constructive notice of the undue
influence, rather than for the creditor or bank to prove that it did not. It is for the complainant to show why the bank or creditor
cannot rely upon the transaction/charge or covenant because they were vitiated by the undue influence or misrepresentations
of the wrongdoer, and to plead and prove why this should make the charge/covenant/transaction invalid as against the bank.
The burden of proving notice is upon the person who claims that the vitiating circumstances affect an innocent third party or
party who was not a party to the undue influence or misrepresentation: see Barclays Bank v Boulter, per Lord Hoffmann. The
burden then shifts to the bank to show that it took reasonable steps to satisfy itself that consent was properly obtained. In the
case of Boulter, the House of Lords held that a rule which would put the burden upon the bank to show that it had no notice
of vitiating circumstances could operate very unreasonably.
Defences
18-11
A party claiming undue influence as a defence (for example as a defence to a claim for payment under a guarantee or other
security) makes a claim for rescission of the transaction and pleads the material facts in the same way as if a claim for rescission
was being made by way of particulars of claim. The pleadings set out in the claims can be modified appropriately as defences,
and a counterclaim added for rescission of the transaction.
As a general comment, note that under the CPR Pt 16 r.16.5 60  bare denials are not permissible and where the defendant denies
an allegation, he must state his reasons for doing so and must state his own version of events if he intends to put forward a
different version of events from that given by the claimant. Undue influence failed as a defence in Fladgate Fielder (A Firm)
v Smith, 61  where, on a summary judgment application, the court found that in order to establish a defence based on undue
influence (in the absence of express undue influence), it was necessary to establish two pre-requisites, namely (1) a relationship

The position in a suretyship contract is to be noted. As against the creditor, provided that the surety can make out that: (1) the
relationship between the surety and the principal debtor is non-commercial; (2) undue influence was exercised against her by the
principal debtor (whether by relying on the presumptions set out above or not); and (3) the creditor did not take the “reasonable
steps” identified in Etridge, (s)he is entitled to relief.
