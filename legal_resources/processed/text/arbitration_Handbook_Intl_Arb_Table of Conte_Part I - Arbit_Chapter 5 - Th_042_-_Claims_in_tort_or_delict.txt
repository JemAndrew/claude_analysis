Claims in tort or delict
21-34
The issue of whether a claim in tort or delict is covered by the arbitration agreement is an issue of interpretation of the arbitration
agreement and the law applicable thereto discussed in art.6. The standard ICC arbitration clause covers “all disputes arising out
of or in connection with this contract” and this language is often considered as covering tort or delict claims that are related to
the contract. 134  Assuming that the Tribunal has jurisdiction over a claim in delict or tort, the issue arises as to the law applicable
to the merits of that claim.
21-35
In some instances, in their choice of law provision (or in the arbitration agreement itself), the parties will expressly agree on
the choice of law for tort or delict claims. Such an agreement is covered by the first sentence of art.21(1), although there may
be issues as to the whether this choice of law is effective. 135  In other instances either the parties will not have agreed on the
applicable law for the contract as a whole or it will not be clear whether the applicable law under the contract govern claims in
tort or delict. In such a case, the Tribunal will be called upon to determine the applicable rules of law under the second sentence
of art.21(2). In ICC Case No.16981, the issue was whether the law applicable to a claim for certain termination claims based
on a Greek law was governed by the law of the distribution contract (Finnish law) or the law under which the claims were
made (Greek law). By majority decision, the Tribunal held that Finnish law, the law of the contract, was applicable due to the
nature of the claims. 136  The Tribunal also held that the choice of Finnish law met the requirements of art.14 of the Rome II
Regulation. 137
21-36
The law applicable to the tort or delict may well be different from that applicable to the contract itself. For example, it is
possible that the parties have agreed that a distribution agreement between a French manufacturer and an English distributor
will be governed by French law. However, if a tort occurs, the law governing the tort will generally be, although not necessarily
exclusively, the law of the place where the tort occurs. 138  Therefore, if there is a tort in England, the Tribunal may be called
upon to apply English law to the tort and French law to the contract, if in fact the claims are distinct. In another example, the
law governing a financing was expressed to be English law. However, the lenders asserted tort claims under the law of the
place of incorporation of the borrower and the parties agreed that the applicable law for those claims was the law of the place
of incorporation of the respondent. 139
