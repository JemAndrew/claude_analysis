Commercial law
19-06
The term “Business Law” in this section has not been used as a substitute for “Commercial Law”, a phrase which is undoubtedly
a term of art. Although some of the topics in this section are topics which are considered to come within the term “Commercial
Law”, some are not and there are other topics which would be considered as “commercial” which are found elsewhere in this
work. There is often a fine line to be drawn between disputes properly regarded as “commercial” and thus litigated in the
Commercial Court, or in the Business or Mercantile lists of the County Court, and disputes which are treated as ordinary High
Court or County Court actions (or even, when the subject matter is very technical, as actions assigned to the Technology and
Construction Court).
In a general work intended to cover the entire range of litigation, there would be little point in separate sections for, say, sale
of goods cases which would be litigated in the Commercial Court and sale of goods cases which would not. The principles
governing the contracts are the same, as are the pleading requirements, particularly since the introduction of the CPR. Thus,
with the exception of contracts to sell goods which are to be shipped f.o.b. or c.i.f. (where Commercial Court forms have
been followed), the precedents in this section have been framed as ordinary High Court or County Court actions (now largely
interchangeable, save perhaps where the Consumer Credit Act 1974 is involved).
