Conditional sale
20-07
Conditional sale is an agreement whereby a seller sells goods to a buyer on terms that the purchase price is paid (normally
with interest) and property in the goods does not pass until the final instalment is paid. Though very similar in concept to hire-
purchase, there are two main differences:
 
1.There is no option to purchase: the sale is concluded at the outset and title passes automatically on payment of the
last instalment. This enables the creditor to provide for acceleration of the balance of the purchase price on default with
concomitant passing of title on the buyer’s default. 4
 
2.Being a contract of sale, it was subject to the Sale of Goods Act 1979: in particular, the buyer, being in possession of the
goods with the consent of the owner, can pass a good title to a sub-buyer under s.25(1) of that Act, though this provision
is now confined to conditional sale agreements not regulated by the Consumer Credit Act.
As with hire-purchase, conditional sale frequently involves a tripartite relationship between the creditor, the hirer and the
supplier. Unlike hire-purchase, which is confined to chattels, conditional sale can apply to land and the definition in s.189(1)
encompasses such sales. Conditional sale of land is very rare and is not covered by this section.
Again, as a matter of nomenclature, the terms “creditor” (now “lender”) and “seller” are interchangeable but it is probably better
to use “seller” as this more correctly describes the relationship and reminds one that sale of goods law will apply to the contract.
