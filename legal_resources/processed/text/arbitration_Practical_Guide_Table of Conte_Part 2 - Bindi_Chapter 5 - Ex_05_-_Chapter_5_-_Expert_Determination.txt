Chapter 5 - Expert Determination
Overview
5-001
Expert determination is a binding process involving the resolution of a dispute, or part of a dispute, by using an independent
expert with specialised knowledge in the relevant field. In essence, the parties appoint a neutral third-party expert to assess the
disputed issue and make a binding decision. As disputes can involve a mix of legal and technical issues, expert determination
is most suited to resolving specific technical or specialist matters. An effective approach is to use expert determination for
specific issues and address the remaining aspects of a dispute by employing other dispute resolution methods, e.g. mediation
for non-technical matters.
It is less common to use expert determination to resolve whole disputes involving multiple intertwined legal and factual issues
as the outcome of these types of complex disputes cannot be determined by a decision on an opinion on the merits of just one
element. However, although expert determination is best suited for clearly defined technical questions, it is possible for it to be
used to resolve whole disputes that are entirely focused on highly technical or specialised matters. These include disputes such as
those over the valuation of a business, or about the interpretation of complex technical specifications in a construction contract.
Expert determination offers the advantage of being less adversarial, which can help preserve business relationships. This is
achieved by isolating and addressing technical issues individually.
The expert is ideally selected through a consensual process whereby, having agreed the essential qualifications and experience,
one party provides a list and the other party selects an expert from that list. In the event of issues regarding either identification of
suitable candidates or concerns about their independence, the parties can specify their criteria for the expert and ask an external
body to appoint the expert. It is common for the parties to remain involved whilst a third-party body selects the expert but it is
essential that, after selection, all parties accept that the expert is independent.
Having selected the expert, the process involves providing the expert with the relevant documentation (possibly including
inspection of equipment or sites) and for them to form their determination based on the evidence provided.
An essential element for the determination to be accepted is the definition of what the expert is being asked to determine. One
of the issues that can arise is the potential for the wording of the instructions to contain leading questions that may inadvertently
affect the expert’s opinion.
In its simplest form, an expert determination clause needs to identify the trigger for the use of the process, and include reference
to the use of an independent expert and a clear statement that the determination shall be binding save for manifest error. Almost
by definition, the expert is likely to be an expert in a specific area. As such, it is very important to define or narrow the nature
of the issue or issues that will be resolved using this process.
Depending on the type of contract and the nature of the transaction, this can be as simple as saying:
“In the event of any technical disputes arising from the interpretation of [specific technical aspect], the parties
shall refer the matter to an independent expert agreed upon by both parties. The expert’s determination shall
be final and binding on the parties, except where gross error can be proven.”

Potential benefits
5-002
The primary objective of expert determination is to provide a specialised, efficient and effective resolution mechanism for
disputes that require specific technical or professional expertise. As it produces binding decisions, relying on the appointed
expert’s knowledge and experience in the relevant field, it is particularly advantageous in situations where the parties involved
seek a confidential, less formal resolution process than that offered by traditional litigation or arbitration.
Given that the parties have the flexibility to determine critical elements of the process, apart from potential delays in identifying
a suitable expert and securing their availability, it is designed to be faster and more cost-effective, reducing the parties’ reliance
on court and judge availability.
Expert determination is particularly effective for technical issues beyond the understanding of a general court or tribunal. Indeed,
if such a technical dispute went to litigation or arbitration, an independent expert or experts would most likely be asked to
provide an opinion to court on such issues. The rules about expert evidence in court require an expert to owe a duty to court
and swear that they are independent. 1  However, despite this duty, where there are experts on opposing sides, this will normally
mean that the court hears opposing opinions. In this case, the judge then has to decide how much weight to place on the expert’s
opinion and choose which opinion to accept. As a result, in order to reduce time and cost, as well as to avoid them having to
choose which opinion to accept, judges much prefer a single joint expert.
In contrast, the clear difference in expert determination is that the expert’s opinion is binding and not subject to an assessment by
the judge as to its integrity or validity. This can make such a determination faster than court proceedings for complex technical
matters.
As it is an out of court process, a further benefit arises from the fact that the parties can tailor it to their needs, giving them
flexibility as to what evidence is made available as well as to the timings of the process.
International disputes
5-003
The New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards 1958 (the New York Convention)
cannot be used to enforce agreements arrived at solely through expert determination because such determinations would not be
classified as arbitral awards. This does not stop arbitrators considering expert evidence as part of a matrix of facts, just that a
decision made by an expert in an Expert Determination process cannot rely on the New York Convention.
As such, expert determination in international disputes can involve unique challenges due to differences in legal systems, cultural
expectations, and enforceability issues across jurisdictions. In addition to ensuring that the selected expert has the necessary
legal, technical, or commercial knowledge, additional considerations include selecting an expert with cross-border dispute
resolution experience and understanding the legal frameworks of the relevant jurisdictions. Ensuring the expert’s decision
is enforceable in different countries requires familiarity with local laws regarding expert determination and enforcement of
contracts.
Potential issues: choice of expert

Given the binding nature of this process, the independence of the expert is a critical issue. Whilst both parties will certainly
agree on this, depending on the relative size of the parties and the frequency of such disputes, there can naturally be a tendency
for either party to have a “preferred” expert. The problem is that, for specialist technical disputes, there may be a limited choice
of experts available. The key is for the parties to determine in advance how they are going to select the expert and incorporate
this into the relevant clause.
The total reliance on a third-party expert means that identification and selection of the expert can lead to delays and
disagreements. This is why an effective clause should include a clearly defined process for selection and must have an agreed
backstop if the parties fail to agree or one party opts to request an appropriate body to appoint the expert.
The first step in appointing an expert is for both parties to agree a definition of the scope of expertise that the expert must have.
In order to assist an institution to identify suitable expert candidates, it is essential to identify the required skills, knowledge and
experience. It is also helpful to consider whether there is any expertise that is essential for the expert to be able to resolve the
dispute. The key is to avoid using broad mandates as these can complicate the selection process by making it more difficult to
identify whether an expert has essential expertise. It can be particularly helpful to specify the precise qualifications and expertise
required (degrees, certifications, professional experience).
To avoid using an institution, it is not uncommon for the procedure to specify, for example, that the claimant is to put forward the
names of three experts and the respondent to select one, or for parties to exchange lists of candidates to see if there is common
ground. In proposing, or selecting, a candidate, it is also advisable for parties to verify a candidate’s professional reputation,
experience and relevant track record.
To avoid any future allegations (or perception) of bias, it is important for both parties to agree that no party is to communicate
with an expert unless the communication and response is copied to all parties. This rule is common to other binding dispute
resolution processes.
On the subject of bias and to avoid conflicts, background checks should be run as a preliminary assessment of potential bias
or links that could compromise an expert’s impartiality. Before they are appointed, they should be provided with details of the
nature of the dispute and the parties involved in order for them to sign a declaration of independence stating that there is no
conflict or disclosing anything that could be perceived as conflict.
Once an expert is agreed, there should be a comprehensive written contract providing a clear definition of the expert’s mandate,
procedural aspects (timetable, access to evidence, submission formats), fee structures and payment terms.
Having selected an expert, a significant issue, post appointment, can be that the chosen expert turns out not to possess the
necessary expertise or experience to address the specific dispute. Clearly, if this turns out to be the case, the decision is likely
to be flawed or possibly perceived to be biased. In these circumstances, provided the parties agree, there is nothing stopping
them terminating the agreement with the expert and appointing a new expert.
Whatever the wording of a clause or the final selection process, there is still a risk that the appointed expert may have a bias or
conflict of interest, consciously or unconsciously favouring one party over the other.
It is not uncommon for a “losing” party to blame a decision maker for being biased. In the circumstances of arbitration or
litigation, the existence of internationally agreed frameworks (e.g. UNCITRAL Rules or Model Law) provides some checks
and balances. In the circumstances of expert determination, the absence of an established framework means that allegations of
bias are much more difficult to prevent. This difficulty is compounded if the expert possesses subconscious or unconscious bias.
Whilst this risk cannot be totally eliminated, an agreement that the expert be required to provide detailed written reasons for
their decision should be included in the expert determination clause. The integrity of the written reasons will go a long way to
avoiding a party deciding to raise any issue over perceived bias.
The risk of conflict of interest is real but can readily be addressed by requiring the expert to disclose any conflicts prior to
selection. In the event that an undisclosed conflict of interest is subsequently discovered, this would support a challenge to the
integrity and, therefore, validity of the final decision (see further discussion on Appeals at para.5-007).

Ambiguity in wording
5-005
A poorly drafted or imprecise expert determination clause can lead to issues as to when the clause is activated or, even more
importantly, challenges over the scope and authority of the expert. This can extend to not being clear about the evidence that
should be made available to the expert after appointment.
In the worst case, the consequence of ambiguity can lead to challenges post decision. If this happens, it will inevitably lead to
delays and increased costs including possible satellite litigation with the overall outcome being that all of the intended benefits
of expert determination are lost. As with any dispute resolution clause, this highlights the importance of taking the time to
negotiate a clause that is fit for purpose and carefully considered rather than an afterthought in the closing hours of completion
of a contract.
Express binding nature
5-006
It may arguably go without saying but it is critical to recognise that the opinion of an expert will not be binding unless specifically
expressed and agreed to be so. The absence of specific wording that confirms the parties’ agreement to it being binding would
effectively turn an expert determination clause into a form of non-binding early neutral evaluation clause.
Appeals
5-007
As has been set out, the binding nature of an agreement to submit to expert determination arises from contract and is not derived
from other conventions or treaties. To avoid the potential costs and delays of traditional litigation, expert determination clauses
are typically designed to be final and binding. As the power of the clause comes from contract law, courts are generally reluctant
to overturn an expert’s decision unless there is strong evidence of fraud or bad faith, manifest error or procedural irregularity.
The contract itself may contain provisions for appealing an expert determination including, for example, a review by a different
expert or a limited right to appeal to arbitration or a court.
Whilst the finality of a binding process is generally considered to be a benefit, the limited scope for challenging such a decision
puts the pressure on identifying the correct expert as well as ensuring that the expert has access to all the relevant information
that will support the correct outcome.
In order to avoid appeals on the basis of the expert’s decision being ultra vires, it is important to ensure the expert determination
clause is clear on the scope of the expert’s role, the process to be followed and the binding nature of the decision.
To further limit the scope for appeal, it is normal to include a clause that only allows appeal in the event of manifest or material
error or fraud. It is important that, whilst every effort should be taken to minimise subsequent litigation, all parties understand
this at the outset.
Any reference to manifest or material error always raises the question of what this means and how to recognise such errors if
they exist. There is an important distinction between manifest and material.
A manifest (or gross) error is a clear, obvious and indisputable error that is immediately recognisable without extensive
investigation. Examples of manifest errors would include clear miscalculations (2 + 2 = 5) or typographical errors that change
the fundamental meaning but where it is clear that an error has occurred.

An alternative way of considering this is that a manifest error refers to a mistake that is not just wrong but fundamentally flawed
to such an extent that no expert applying their mind could have reasonably arrived at such a conclusion. This must be more
than a simple error in judgment or an incorrect application of principles. 2  Mr Adrian Beltrami KC reviewed the definitions
and applicability of manifest error within expert determinations. 3  This confirmed the principle that manifest error must be one
that is obvious or easily demonstrable without extensive investigation and that such errors must be more than “just wrong” and
need to be fundamentally and clearly incorrect. However, it is not necessary that the error be identified immediately.
On the other hand, a material error is a significant or substantial error that influences the outcome of a decision or judgment.
It must have a substantive impact on the outcome. An example of this could be a misapplication of the law to the facts. Whilst
manifest errors are blatantly obvious, material errors may require some analysis but still have a clear impact on the case. In
other words, all manifest errors are material, but not all material errors are obvious enough to be considered manifest.
If a determination is unfavourable, a party may be tempted to submit that it is a gross or manifest error but demonstrating such
an error has a high threshold. It is not enough to show that the decision was unfavourable or even that another expert might
have reached a different conclusion, it must be fundamentally wrong such that no reasonable expert could have reached that
conclusion.
Mechanism for addressing errors
5-008
Having allowed for an appeal in the event of manifest or material error, it can help to include provisions for correcting such
errors. For instance, the clause or institution can specify a procedure that allows the parties to request a review if there is a clear
mathematical mistake or a misunderstanding of a fact that could change the outcome.
It is also good practice for the expert to be asked to provide the parties with a draft decision before effectively handing it down.
This allows for parties to provide feedback and ask questions on the expert’s preliminary findings before the final decision
is made. This gives the expert a chance to correct oversights, but it should be made clear that the parties are not allowed to
introduce new evidence after the expert has provided their findings.
By incorporating these elements into the expert determination clause, parties can create a more robust framework that allows
for the efficient resolution of disputes while preserving the finality and integrity of the expert’s decision.
The clause
Simple clause
5-009
Whilst there are some essential common elements, expert determination clauses can vary significantly in wording and scope,
which can affect their usefulness and applicability to different commercial sectors as well as their suitability for different
industries.
An example of a simple clause illustrates the two essential elements being that the dispute is referred to an expert for
determination and that the expert’s decision shall be final and binding:
“Any dispute arising out of or in connection with this contract shall be referred to an expert for
determination in accordance with the rules of the [specific institution or association]. 4  The expert’s
decision shall be final and binding on the parties.”

The simplicity of this standard clause readily illustrates the potential for problems if such a clause were copy/pasted into
a contract. The breadth of the clause (“any dispute”) is not likely to be appropriate for a commercial contract save for
exceptionally narrow circumstances. This is because it captures every possible dispute including, for example, late payment
of an invoice. Further issues arise from the fact that there is no detail of the timing of activation of such a clause. In order to
have beneficial effect, the primary issue here is for the parties to agree what they mean by “any dispute” and, if they leave it as
it is, they do this knowing that a literal interpretation of the word “any” would mean every possible dispute, of whatever type.
Specific clause
5-010
An illustration of a clause that expands the simple clause to make it clear that only specific disputes are covered can be seen as:
“In the event of a dispute related to the [quality or specifications of the construction work], the parties
agree to appoint a [qualified expert structural engineer] to make a determination. The expert’s decision
shall be final and binding.”
Here the expertise is specifically aligned with the core element of the contract (and therefore an area where a dispute is more
likely to occur). The descriptions in the square brackets must be discussed and agreed between the parties to make sure they
are relevant. It is also advisable for the parties to discuss potential disputes that the expert determination is not intended to
cover. This can either be to “stress test” the clause to confirm that such disputes are not covered or any areas that are not
intended to be covered could be specifically excluded as part of the clause.
Hybrid branched clause
5-011
A hybrid clause can also be drafted to create a form of binding “branch” where a dispute could end up moving to arbitration
or, if appropriate, could be determined by the suitable expert. Such a clause could be drafted as:
“Any dispute arising under this contract shall be resolved by arbitration in accordance with the rules
of [specific arbitration institution], except for [disputes regarding vessel performance], which shall be
submitted to an [independent marine expert] for determination. The expert’s decision shall be binding on
the parties.”
The advantage of this type of clause, which can easily be added to a defined arbitration clause, is that it prevents a purely
technical dispute, which would otherwise probably require a finding of fact to be made by an arbitrator or judge, being
handled in proceedings that would inevitably require expert opinion and potentially lengthy and complex cross-examination
of relevant experts.
Creating a comprehensive and up-to-date list of institutions around the world that offer to identify and appoint experts for
disputes resolved through expert determination is challenging due to the sheer number and diversity of such institutions
globally. They range from large, internationally recognised bodies to smaller, specialised organisations. Some of the key
institutions known for their dispute resolution services, including expert determination, are listed in Appendix One, with

Price adjustment/valuation clause
5-012
An expert determination clause can apply both to technical and commercial disputes, for example a price adjustment clause:
“If a dispute arises concerning the adjustment of prices based on market conditions, the parties agree to
appoint a financial expert to determine the fair market value. The expert’s determination shall be final
and binding.”
This is clearly specific to disputes concerning the contractual adjustment of prices but, in drafting this clause, it is notable
that other critical elements have not been included.
In summary, the choice of an expert determination clause in international commercial contracts should align with the specific
needs and complexities of the particular industry or sector and the potential disputes that may arise. It is essential to carefully
draft and tailor such clauses to the contract’s subject matter to ensure effective dispute resolution.
Checklist for drafting expert determination clause
5-013
When drafting a clause, it is essential to define the scope, how the expert is appointed, the subsequent procedure, the binding
nature of the decision and how the fees are paid.
 
• Scope: The disputes or issues that are to be subject to expert determination must be clearly defined. This ensures clarity
on the applicability of the process and reduces the risk of secondary litigation. It is important to avoid broad or unclear
language that could lead to disputes over what is covered by the clause.
 
• Appointing the expert: The qualifications required for the expert need to be specified in detail. This needs to be
considered carefully as, whilst an issue might appear to be about the financial impact of an event, the key issue might be
causation including a technical analysis. As such, the appointment process should allow the parties to select the expert
and, in the absence of agreement, there should be a mechanism for resolving any disputes over expert appointment,
including referral to an appointing authority. This can also include provisions for replacing the expert, if necessary, due
to conflict of interest or incapacity.
 
• Conflict of interest: It can be acknowledged in the clause that the expert should have no financial or personal interest
in the outcome of the dispute and no prior or current relationships with any of the parties involved. This is all that needs
to be said but the parties should be clear that the selection must include checking for any previous consultations or
engagements. It is very important to thoroughly investigate any potential conflicts of interest. These checks can include
current or past business relationships, personal connections, or any other factor that might give rise to a perceived bias
in the expert’s determination. Experts should expressly be asked to disclose any such potential conflicts before their
appointment.
The expert must clearly possess the technical or specialised knowledge relevant to the dispute’s subject matter. This can be
confirmed by evaluating academic qualifications, professional certifications and experience in the specific field related to the
dispute. The importance of selecting an expert with the necessary technical or specialised knowledge cannot be overstated.
It ensures that the expert is capable of understanding the nuances of the dispute, can assess the evidence accurately, and
can provide a well-reasoned and informed determination. This is crucial for maintaining the credibility and integrity of the
expert determination process, ensuring fair outcomes, and minimising the risk of disputes over the expert’s qualifications
or impartiality.

• Procedure: The most effective way to identify a procedure is to make reference to an institution that has a standard
procedure or, ideally, one with a focus on the specific area of dispute. Technical or scientific disputes are often referred
to specialised organisations. To be effective, it is preferable to have a straightforward process that does not turn out to
be more burdensome than alternative dispute resolution methods.
There are many local/national options, but the key is to look for a trusted institution with a track record of handling expert
determination processes. Institutions may charge fees for their services, so it is also a good idea to consider the costs
involved in choosing an institution. Such institutions include The Academy of Experts (TAE); 5  the International Chamber
of Commerce (ICC); 6  the Institute of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales (ICAEW); 7  and Centre for Effective
Dispute Resolution (CEDR). 8
 
• Binding nature: The agreement needs to specify that the expert’s decision is final and binding on the parties, subject
to any agreed-upon exceptions. It is recommended to incorporate a limited appeal mechanism based on a manifest error
threshold.
 
• Fees and costs: The parties need to determine how the expert’s fees and any associated costs are shared. To avoid
subsequent disagreement, it is best to agree in advance how the fees are shared.
Potential issues with expert determination
5-014
For the reasons set out, it is important to select an appropriate expert with relevant expertise and to ensure their independence to
avoid bias. To address possible issues, well-drafted expert determination clauses should also include mechanisms for addressing
issues like expertise mismatch and potential bias.
The possible issues can be illustrated, for example, by considering a construction contract dispute over the quality of work
where an expert in commercial real estate valuation was selected instead of a construction engineering expert. This mismatch
could lead to a determination that overlooked critical technical aspects of the dispute.
If there is a clause in the contract allowing for a review process in cases where the initial expert lacked essential technical
expertise, this would allow the parties to rely on it to agree a secondary review by a qualified construction engineer. In turn, by
relying on the clause, a revised determination could be obtained that accurately addressed the technical issues in dispute.
A further illustration of the circumstances where such a clause could be helpful is where, during a financial dispute over
the valuation of shares in a private company, the expert chosen was subsequently found to have had previous consultancy
engagements with one of the parties. This prior relationship could lead to perceptions of bias in the determination process and,
as a result, lend itself to the aggrieved party challenging the determination. In this case, if there is an appropriate clause including
provisions for challenging an expert based on conflict of interest, a party who raises concerns about bias could more easily
request a new, independent expert with no ties to either party.
Misunderstandings, challenges and opportunities
5-015
The role of the expert is sometimes misconceived by the parties involved, who may expect the expert to provide legal advice
or resolve issues outside their area of expertise. It is critical to establish the expert’s limited scope at the outset, emphasising
their mandate to offer a binding assessment based on the evidence provided. In addition, whilst it may seem obvious, given the
importance of impartiality, the parties need to be clear that they must not make any attempt to influence the expert in any way
and this should include not contacting the expert, unless all parties agree to that.
A common misunderstanding is the enforceability of the expert’s determination. Parties may anticipate that the expert’s decision
is immediately binding and enforceable, overlooking the potential need for additional legal procedures to enforce the decision,

particularly in complex disputes. Ensuring that the scope of the expert determination is comprehensive and well-understood
by all parties is crucial to avoid unforeseen complications and dissatisfaction. Additionally, aligning the expert determination

agreement constitutes a valid contract under the chosen law.
Expert determination is an important tool in the dispute resolution toolbox. Applied correctly, it provides a specialised,
efficient and effective mechanism for resolving technical or expert-level disputes. By leveraging the knowledge of independent
experts, parties can achieve binding decisions that are less formal, more cost-effective and quicker than traditional litigation
or arbitration. The process provides for confidentiality, flexibility and the preservation of business relationships, making it
particularly suitable for disputes involving technical issues or requiring specialised knowledge.
The success of expert determination hinges on clear definitions of the expert’s role, comprehensive drafting of determination
clauses, and the careful selection of impartial experts. Ensuring transparency, maintaining open communication and aligning
the process with the legal frameworks of the relevant jurisdictions is essential to ensure its effectiveness and enforceability.
In summary, expert determination, when appropriately implemented, can resolve disputes in a manner that is both legally valid
and tailored to the specific needs of the parties involved, making it an invaluable component of modern dispute resolution
strategies.
