Article 23(1)(b)
“the addresses to which notifications and communications arising in the course of the arbitration may be made;”
23-21
If the parties are represented by lawyers, the addresses for notification will almost invariably be the addresses of the lawyers and
not those of the parties. Where there is co-counsel, in many instances the co-counsel also receives a copy of the notifications
and filings. In some cases, in-house counsel of one or both parties is added for notification purposes. With the prevailing use
of email communication of Tribunals with parties, adding more than one recipient on each side for notification purposes is not
a problem. However, the Tribunal should be aware that when it is required to send hard copies by mail or courier to several
recipients, having more than one recipient on each side may raise logistical problems.
23-22
When parties actually receive notifications (and how) is, surprisingly, often an issue in international arbitration, especially if
the time frame for responses is rapid. In many instances, parties send letters and briefs by email, followed by copy by courier,
in particular where the briefs comprise exhibits.
