Section 68 Arbitration Act 1996
Introduction
2-11
Section 68 of the 1996 Act provides a further ground on which a party can challenge all or part of an award rendered by an
arbitral tribunal seated in England and Wales. Thus, as with s.67, intervention under s.68 is only permissible after an award
is made. 21  In an appropriate case, it can also be combined with a challenge under s.67. Section 68 entitles a party to arbitral
proceedings to apply to the court for relief on the ground that a “serious irregularity” affecting the tribunal, the proceedings or
the award has occurred. As with s.67, s.68 is a mandatory provision in the 1996 Act and cannot be contracted out of by the
parties. As with an application under s.67, the claimant must first exhaust any available arbitral process of appeal or review
and any available recourse under s.57. 22
Serious irregularity
2-12
The concept of “serious irregularity” was introduced by the 1996 Act and was a new feature of English arbitration law. 23
In order to establish that a “serious irregularity” has occurred, the applicant must demonstrate two things. First, the applicant
must show that one or more of the closed list of irregularities set out in s.68(2) has occurred. 24  Secondly, the applicant must
show that the alleged irregularity has caused or will cause it “substantial injustice”. 25  Since the introduction of the 1996 Act,
a considerable body of law has developed in relation to the categories of irregularity set out in s.68(2)(a) to (i). For detailed
consideration of these categories reference should be made to the relevant textbooks and authorities.
A high threshold
2-13
The courts have repeatedly emphasised that a high threshold must be satisfied before the court will intervene under s.68. 26
Indeed, the present approach is to view that threshold as having been set deliberately high so as to reduce drastically the extent
of court intervention in the arbitral process. Recent authorities suggest that only an “extreme case” will justify the court’s
intervention. 27  It has recently been suggested that fresh evidence might provide grounds for a s.68 application, at least in the
context of an application made on the grounds that the award was contrary to public policy. 28  Further, when considering a
s.68 challenge, the focus of the inquiry is on due process and not on the correctness of the tribunal’s decision and the court
is alive to applications which in truth seek to challenge the merits or the application of the law, rather than complain about a
genuine serious irregularity. 29

Approach to relief under s.68
2-14
An important feature of s.68 is the presumption in s.68(3) that the appropriate relief on a serious irregularity is for the court
to remit the matters in question to the tribunal for reconsideration. For some cases, this will be appropriate. However, if the
applicant wants the award set aside or declared to be of no effect (in whole or in part), it will need to persuade the court that it
is inappropriate to remit the matters in question to the tribunal. For some cases (such as bias) this will be straightforward but
in other cases it will be far less so. The precedent therefore leaves the desired relief sought in brackets. It appears that, if the
claimant seeks removal of an arbitrator, an application must be made under s.24. 30
Timing of challenge
2-15
The challenge must be brought within 28 days of the date of the award, unless there is an available arbitral process of appeal
or review or—provided any corrections are material to the challenge in question—the available recourse under s.57. 31  In that
case, the application must be brought within 28 days of the date when the applicant is notified of the result of that process
of appeal/review or the result of the recourse under s.57. It is possible for an applicant to seek an extension of time to file a
challenge pursuant to s.80(5) of the 1996 Act. 32
Procedure
2-16
The procedure for bringing a s.68 claim is the same as that for a s.67 claim. It requires the claimant to file an Arbitration Claim
Form together with a witness statement which supplies the evidence supporting its claim that the tribunal lacked substantive
jurisdiction.
In a run-of-the mill case, there is no pleadings phase. Instead, the CPR provides a set of automatic directions which apply once
the claimant has filed its Arbitration Claim Form together with its evidence. Thereafter, the defendant is obliged to respond by
way of a witness statement within 21 days of the date on which it was required to acknowledge service or, if no acknowledgement
of service was required, within 21 days from service of the Arbitration Claim Form. The claimant is then provided with only
seven days to respond by way of reply evidence. 33  The claim form should stand as a sufficiently detailed and particularised
statement of case to enable, in the first place, the defendant to the challenge and then, ultimately, the judge dealing with the
matter to see precisely the nature of the challenge, the grounds upon which it is said to arise and, as a result, the particular
questions that will need, or may need, to be dealt with at any hearing. As in all contexts, supporting witness statements should
contain evidence, not comment or argument. 34
If the case is more complicated, and in particular if it raises issues of fact or foreign law which would benefit from the precise
identification of issues through further statements of case, it is possible either (a) for the parties to agree to this approach or
(b) for the claimant (or even the defendant, if it considers it appropriate) to seek non-standard directions from the court. 35  A
suggested formulation for such a case is included in the precedent.

Service
2-17
An important feature of an application using an Arbitration Claim Form is that, unlike a Part 7 Claim Form, the Arbitration
Claim Form must be served on the defendant within one month from the date of issue. 36  Where it is anticipated that service
will take considerably longer (e.g. if the defendant is a sovereign state) it is advisable to apply for an extension of time for
service at the same time as the s.68 application is issued.
Finally, one useful provision to note is para.3.1 of PD 62. Where the defendant to the s.68 application was represented by
English solicitors in the arbitration, it is standard practice for the court to give the claimant permission pursuant to CPR r.6.15
to serve the Arbitration Claim Form on the defendant by way of service on the defendant’s English solicitors.
Early intervention in the Commercial Court
2-18
The Admiralty and Commercial Court Guide contains an important procedural device which is relevant to s.68 applications.
Paragraph O8.5 sets out a procedure by which a respondent to a s.68 application can apply to the court to dismiss the application,
without a hearing, if the “nature of the challenge itself or the evidence filed in support of it leads the court to consider that
the claim has no real prospect of success”. Where the court makes an order dismissing the application without a hearing, the
applicant will have the right to apply to the court to set aside the order and to seek directions for the hearing of the application. 37
If such an application is made and dismissed after a hearing, the court may consider whether it is appropriate to award costs
on an indemnity basis.
Appeals
2-19
The permission of the court is required under s.68(4) for an appeal. As with s.67, a number of Court of Appeal decisions have
made it clear that, subject to one very narrow exception, permission to appeal can only be granted by the first instance judge and
not by the Court of Appeal. 38  In the context of s.67, the Court of Appeal has held that it has no jurisdiction to hear an appeal
on grounds that were not raised in the application to the first-instance judge; the same presumably applies under s.68. 39  If a
judge makes a decision that he or she has no jurisdiction to make and then refuses permission to appeal, the Court of Appeal
has jurisdiction to declare that the judge acted without jurisdiction. 40
