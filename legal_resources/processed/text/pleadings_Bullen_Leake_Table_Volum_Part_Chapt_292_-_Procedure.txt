Chapter 24 - Set-off
Procedure
24-08
CPR r.16.6 provides:
“Where a defendant
 
(a)contends he is entitled to money from the claimant; and
 
(b)relies on this as a defence to the whole or part of the claim,
the contention may be included in the defence and set off against the claim, whether or not it is also a Part 20
claim.”
This rule is permissive in that it allows the defendant to include in his defence a claim to money (whether for specific amounts
or unspecified amounts) but it does not render it obligatory and a defendant is entitled to pursue his cross-claim in a separate
action. The rule does not, however, alter the existing substantive law in relation to what is capable of amounting to a set-off.
There are two areas where it is of practical significance whether a cross-claim can be set off against the claimant’s claim.
Where the claimant is insolvent and the counterclaim lies outside the categories of set-off recognised by s.324 of the Insolvency
Act 1986, the claimant will be able to enforce his claim in full, leaving the defendant to enforce his counterclaim as best he
can by proving in the insolvency of the claimant. Where both claimant and defendant remain solvent, the right to set off the
counterclaim may be relevant as to costs but in practice the wide judicial discretion as to costs normally ensures that a successful
defendant is not penalised in costs merely because his counterclaim cannot technically be set off against the claim.
Secondly, where the claim is admitted and the counterclaim cannot be set off, the claimant may be entitled to summary judgment
on the claim under CPR Pt 24, without a stay of execution. 17
