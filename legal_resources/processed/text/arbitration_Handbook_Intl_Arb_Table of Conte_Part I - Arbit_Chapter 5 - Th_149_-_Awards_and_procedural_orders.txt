Awards and procedural orders
28-65
Article 28(1) leaves open the possibility of use by the Tribunal of a reasoned procedural order or an award for a conservatory or
interim relief. 522  In the final award in ICC Case No.14287, the Tribunal expressed the opinion that, as a general rule, Tribunals
should issue procedural orders and not awards with respect to requests for interim or conservatory measures due the possibility
of prejudgment of issues in the case. 523  The authors agree. One of the major problems that can arise with partial awards is that
they decide issues that affect subsequent awards. Therefore, where, as in art.28, the Tribunal has the option between issuing a
procedural order and an award, except in exceptional circumstances, it should avoid issuing an award.
28-66
The distinction between final and conservatory or interim measures is not always easy to define. For example, an order for the
production of documents may constitute a final or provisional matter. If a party has a contractual right to inspect documents,
then the Tribunal’s decision with respect to that contractual right involves the interpretation and enforcement of that right. 524
If, on the other hand, a party is seeking a procedural order of disclosure that is not based on the contract, it is arguably seeking
an interim measure. Nevertheless, the decision to use a procedural order or an award on provisional measures is left to the
appreciation of the Tribunal subject to the final control on the nature of such decision by the national court. 525
28-67
The distinction is important for two reasons: first, if the decision takes the form of an award, it will have to undergo the scrutiny
and notification process of awards pursuant to arts 34 and 35. Secondly, an award, unlike a procedural order, can be enforced
with the help of the courts, in particular under the New York Convention.
