Section 67 Arbitration Act 1996
Introduction
2-01
Section 67 of the Arbitration Act 1996 Act provides one of the limited grounds on which a party can challenge all or part of
an award 1  rendered by an arbitral tribunal seated in England and Wales. It entitles a party to arbitral proceedings to apply to
the court to set aside an award on the grounds that the tribunal lacked substantive jurisdiction. 2  Section 67 is a mandatory
provision in the 1996 Act and cannot be contracted out of by the parties. 3  Section 70(2) provides that an application under s.67
may not be brought if the claimant has not first exhausted any available arbitral process of appeal or review and any available
recourse under s.57. 4
Substantive jurisdiction
2-02
Section 67 is limited to challenges to an award on the grounds that the tribunal lacked “substantive jurisdiction”. Section
82(1) of the 1996 Act defines “substantive jurisdiction” by reference to the matters specified in s.30 thereof. Section 30
defines substantive jurisdiction to mean: (a) whether there is a valid arbitration agreement; (b) whether the tribunal is properly
constituted and (c) what matters have been submitted to arbitration in accordance with the arbitration agreement. Section 30
supplies an exhaustive definition of jurisdictional matters. 5
Awards on jurisdiction/awards on merits
2-03
A jurisdictional challenge can be brought either against an award that addresses only the question of substantive jurisdiction
(see s.67(1)(a)) or against an award that addresses both substantive jurisdiction and the merits (see s.67(1)(b)). This is partly
a function of the fact that, in the absence of agreement between the parties, the tribunal can determine whether to hear the
jurisdictional objection as a self-standing issue or deal with it in an award on the merits. 6
A rehearing and not a review

It is clear that the court’s approach under s.67 is to conduct a de novo rehearing of the question of jurisdiction, rather than
merely to conduct a review of the tribunal’s findings. In other words, the question for the court is not whether the tribunal was
entitled to reach the decision it reached but whether that decision was correct. As such, the court is not bound by the findings
made by the tribunal and approaches the matter completely afresh. 7
Additional evidence
2-05
One consequence of the fact that the court conducts a rehearing is that, in principle, additional evidence that was not before the
tribunal is admissible before the court, albeit subject to limits delineated in a series of first instance decisions. In particular, the
court has been careful to regulate the timing and manner in which a party may introduce new evidence. 8
However, this is not to say that where a party has participated in the arbitration and challenged the tribunal’s jurisdiction, it will
be entitled to run entirely new grounds of challenge before the court. It is likely that in those circumstances the applicant will
face an objection that it has lost the right to object on the new ground it seeks to introduce, pursuant to s.73 of the 1996 Act. 9
The grounds of a s.67 challenge
2-06
It is important for a party seeking to challenge an award under s.67 to set out (if at all possible) all of its various heads of
challenge in its Arbitration Claim Form and to file its claim within the 28-day period permitted by the 1996 Act (see below).
Otherwise, it runs the risk of not being allowed to rely upon any additional head of challenge which it later seeks to introduce.
The actual grounds for a challenge will of course depend on the circumstances of each individual case. Typically, a challenge
to substantive jurisdiction will involve some form of allegation that the arbitration agreement is invalid or ineffective (at least
vis-à-vis the claimant in question) or that, even if the arbitration agreement is valid and binding, the disputes presented to and
ruled upon by the tribunal fall outside the scope of that arbitration agreement. 10  This is not the place for a detailed discussion
of the approach of the English court to such arguments, which can be found in the detailed works on this topic. 11
However, even if the grounds are made out, the judge has discretion whether to grant any relief and may refuse to do so if there
are good reasons for that refusal. Any submissions about the exercise of that discretion should be made during argument or,
at the latest, immediately following judgment. 12
Timing of challenge
2-07
The challenge must be brought within 28 days of the date of the award, unless there is an available arbitral process of appeal
or review or—provided any corrections are material to the challenge in question—the available recourse under s.57. 13  In that
latter case, the application must be brought within 28 days of the date when the applicant is notified of the result of that process
of appeal/review or the result of the recourse under s.57. It is possible for an applicant to seek an extension of time to file a
challenge pursuant to s.80(5) of the 1996 Act. 14

Procedure
2-08
The procedure for bringing a s.67 claim is fairly straightforward and seeks to promote the general scheme and purpose of the
Arbitration Act 1996, which is to achieve speed and finality. It requires the claimant to file an Arbitration Claim Form together
with a witness statement which supplies the evidence supporting its claim that the tribunal lacked substantive jurisdiction. The
claim is usually commenced in the Commercial Court or in the Technology and Construction Court. 15
In a run-of-the mill case, there is no pleadings phase. Instead, the CPR provides a set of automatic directions which apply once
the claimant has filed its Arbitration Claim Form together with its evidence. Thereafter, the defendant is obliged to respond by
way of a witness statement within 21 days of the date on which it was required to acknowledge service or, if no acknowledgement
of service was required, within 21 days from service of the Arbitration Claim Form. The claimant is then provided with only
seven days to respond by way of reply evidence. 16
If the case is more complicated, and in particular if it raises issues of fact or foreign law which would benefit from the precise
identification of issues through further statements of case, it is possible for the parties either (a) to agree to this approach or
(b) for the claimant (or even the defendant, if it considers it appropriate) to seek non-standard directions from the court. A
suggested formulation for such a case is included in the precedent.
Service
2-09
An important feature of an application using an Arbitration Claim Form is that, unlike a Part 7 Claim Form, the Arbitration
Claim Form must be served on the defendant within one month from the date of issue. 17  In circumstances where it is anticipated
that service will take considerably longer (e.g. if the defendant is a sovereign state) it is advisable to apply for an extension of
time for service at the same time as the s.67 application is issued.
Finally, one useful provision to note is para.3.1 of PD 62. In circumstances where the defendant to the s.67 application was
represented by English solicitors in the arbitration, it is standard practice for the court to give the claimant permission, pursuant
to CPR r.6.15, to serve the Arbitration Claim Form on the defendant by way of service on the defendant’s English solicitors.
Appeals
2-10
The permission of the court is required under s.67(4) for an appeal. A number of Court of Appeal decisions have made it clear
that, subject to one very narrow exception, permission to appeal can only be granted by the first instance judge, and not by
the Court of Appeal. 18
The Court of Appeal has no jurisdiction to hear an appeal on grounds that were not raised in the s.67 application. 19  If a judge
makes a decision that he has no jurisdiction to make and then refuses permission to appeal, the Court of Appeal has jurisdiction
to declare that the judge acted without jurisdiction. 20
