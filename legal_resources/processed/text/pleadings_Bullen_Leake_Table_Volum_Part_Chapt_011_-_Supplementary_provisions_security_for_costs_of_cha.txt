Supplementary provisions: security for costs of challenge or appeal (s.70(6)) and security for the claim (s.70(7))
Security for costs of challenge or appeal (s.70(6) of the 1996 Act)
4-05
Amongst the supplementary provisions relating to challenges or appeals under ss.67, 68 and/or 69 is s.70(6), which provides
that the court may order the applicant to provide security for the costs of the application or appeal and may direct that the
application be dismissed if the order is not complied with. The power to order security for costs shall not be exercised on the
ground that the applicant is: (a) an individual ordinarily resident outside the UK; or (b) a corporation or association incorporated
or formed under the law of a country outside the UK, or whose central management and control is exercised outside the UK.
The approach to considering an application for security for costs is the same as that under CPR r.25.12 and r.25.13, 33  subject
to the express negative limitations based upon the location of the individual or corporation. Section 70(6) was considered in
Azov v Baltic, 34  a case that has been subsequently followed. Longmore J held that, aside from the express limitations, there
was no formal fetter on the discretion of the court in relation to security for costs, however, regard must be had to the factors in
s.1(a) of the 1996 Act. It would be rare to order security for costs if an applicant for relief had sufficient assets to meet any order
for costs and those assets were available for satisfaction of any such order for costs. Excessive weight should not be given to
the fact that the arbitrator had decided the question of jurisdiction against the applicant, although this might tip the scale if the
court could not be certain about the position on assets. The applicant did not have sufficient available assets for the purposes
of enforcing an English costs order. Indeed, there was considerable doubt as to the enforcement of foreign costs orders in the
Ukraine, where the applicant had sufficient assets. The court therefore made an order for security for costs.
In X v Y, 35  X brought challenges against the fourth award under ss.67 and 68 of the 1996 Act and Y sought security for costs
and security for the fourth award under ss.70(6) and 70(7) of the 1996 Act respectively. In relation to the application for security
for costs, following the approach of Longmore J in Azov v Baltic, Teare J found that there was a real risk that the assets of X were
not readily available for the satisfaction of any order for costs and therefore order security be paid. In Moondance Maritime
Enterprises SA v Carbofer Maritime Trading APS (The Moondance II) 36  Field J held that sums awarded in one arbitration did
not constitute an equitable set-off that was relevant to the exercise of the court’s discretion to order security.
In Konkola Copper Mines Plc v U&M Mining Zambia Ltd, 37  KCM brought challenges under ss.67 and 68 of the 1996 Act and
U&M sought security for costs and security for the claim under ss.70(6) and 70(7) of the 1996 Act respectively. Eder J granted
the application for security for costs holding that there was an overwhelming case in favour of such an order. Between [24] to
[32], he held that there was a real risk that the assets of the KCM would not be readily available for the satisfaction of any order
for costs made against them. KCM’s assets were in Zambia and the steps they had taken to resist enforcement of the first award
demonstrated that there was, at the very least, a real risk that they would refuse to pay or delay in paying any costs order. Further,
Eder J considered it fatal to KCM’s position that it had insufficient liquid funds to enable it to pay U&M’s costs in the arbitration.
Security for costs was ordered in Progas Energy Ltd v Pakistan. 38  The claimant’s litigation funders wrote to the defendant
stating that, in the event of an adverse costs order against the claimant, the funders would pay the defendant’s costs up to the
amount of security that had been sought (see [31]). The court held that the letter did not form a contractual commitment nor

an undertaking to the court. The letter did not mean that the claimant had assets available to it. Further, it was no answer to the
application to say that a defendant could apply for a costs order against a non-party litigation funder under s.51 of the Senior
Courts Act 1981 after the costs order had been made. That did not give the defendant any protection before the event and there
was no certainty as to whether such an order would be subsequently made. In any event, if the funder was willing to pay the

(see [31]–[43]).
Security for the claim (s.70(7) of the 1996 Act)
4-06
Section 70(7) of the 1996 Act is a further supplementary provision relating to challenges or appeals under ss.67, 68 and/or
69, which provides that that court may order that any money payable under the award shall be brought into court or otherwise
secured pending the determination of the application or appeal, and may direct that the application or appeal be dismissed if
the order is not complied with.
The DAC Report, [380], states that the purpose of s.70(7) was to “avoid the risk that, while the appeal is pending, the ability
of the losing party to honour the award may (by design or otherwise) be diminished”. In Moondance Maritime Enterprises SA
v Carbofer Maritime Trading APS (The Moondance II) at [9], Field J held that in order to rely upon s.70(7) of the 1996 Act,
there had to be a specific sum of money ordered to be paid under the award appealed or challenged.
At least in the context of an application under s.67 of the 1996 Act, there is normally a threshold requirement that the s.70(7)
applicant must demonstrate that the challenge to the award is flimsy or otherwise lacks substance along with the requirement
that the challenge would prejudice the applicant’s ability to enforce the award. 39
In A v B, 40  Flaux J stated obiter that in considering the effect of the challenge on the ability to enforce the award, regard may be
had to s.103(5) of the 1996 Act 41  and should be related to issues of ease or difficulty of enforcement and diminution of assets.
In X v Y, Teare J regarded X’s s.67 challenge to be flimsy and lacking in substance. However, despite being unsympathetic to
the flimsy challenge, Teare J held that it did not prejudice Y’s ability to enforce the award or diminish X’s ability to honour the
award. Despite the criticism of X v Y in the leading textbook Merkin and Flannery, Arbitration Act 1996, at pp.346–348, 42  in
Konkola Copper Mines Plc v U&M Mining Zambia Ltd at [47] to [48] there was a similar reluctance to order payment in of
the sums awarded. Although there was a real risk of dissipation of assets, there was no evidence that KCM’s challenges would
prejudice enforcement of the arbitral award in Zambia. The challenged award remained valid and binding as a matter of English
law and seemed to be enforceable in Zambia.
4-07
In Peterson Farms Inc v C&M Farming Ltd 43  Tomlinson J stated that he could well understand why it might be thought
appropriate to require security under s.70(7) of the 1996 Act in relation to a challenge made under s.68 of the 1996 Act. In Danko
International v Faucon Investment Co 44  Cooke J considered the “general strength of the claim”. However, he did not refer
expressly to a “threshold requirement” and in Konkola Copper Mines Plc v U&M Mining Zambia Ltd at [44] Eder J expressed
doubts about Danko International v Faucon Investment laying down particular guidelines as to the relevance of the general
strength of the claim on any application under s.70(7) of the 1996 Act.
In Progas Energy Ltd v Pakistan, Picken J followed the approach in Erdenet Mining Corporation LLC v ICBC Standard Bank
Plc, 45  which effectively required evidence of dissipation of assets. There was no such evidence. This position was unchanged
by the fact that the case involved litigation funders. He also proceeded on the basis that there was no “flimsiness” test in relation
to s.68 challenges (see [51]).
In Peterson Farms Inc v C&M Farming Ltd at [27], Tomlinson J stated obiter that it was perhaps unlikely that the court would
be persuaded to impose conditions, such as those under s.70(7) of the 1996 Act, on an applicant bringing an appeal under s.69
of the 1996 Act. This was because the court would have already granted leave to pursue the appeal and it is likely that this
would only have been granted if the court had concluded that the decision of the tribunal is obviously wrong, or at any rate
that it is at least open to serious doubt.

The opinion of the authors of Merkin and Flannery, Arbitration Act 1996 is that a more robust approach should be adopted in
deciding whether to order security under s.70(7) of the 1996 Act. They consider that the flimsiness test should apply to both
ss.67 and 68 of the 1996 Act and that if it is met then there should be a presumption that security is ordered. They also suggest
that s.70(7) allows the court to order the payment in of amounts less than the full amount of the award.
