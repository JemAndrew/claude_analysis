Introductory remarks
22-1
Article 22 sets out the basic principles regarding the conduct of the arbitration. 186  Article 22 should be read in conjunction
in particular with arts 23 (Terms of Reference), 24 (case management conference and Procedural Timetable), 25 (Establishing
the Facts of the Case), 26 (Hearings) and 30 (Expedited Procedure Provisions). In addition, practitioners should keep in mind
the provisions of Part VII D of the 2021 ICC Note on the Expeditious Determination of Manifestly Unmeritorious Claims
or Defenses and the reference, which was added in the 2021 Rules to Appendix IV to the Rules regarding case management
techniques.
22-2
Article 22 sets out two fundamental principles which are sometimes seen as competing in practice: the Tribunal “shall act fairly
and impartially and ensure that each party has a reasonable opportunity to present its case” and the Tribunal shall act in “an
expeditious and cost-effective manner”. Much of the discussion in arts 22–26 involves balancing these respective interests.
22-3
The Tribunal’s duty to act fairly and impartially has not given rise to much substantive discussion, as it is so basic and well-
understood. Respecting that duty is important to ensure that the parties have an enforceable award. 187  The Tribunal’s duty to
be expeditious and cost-effective has been the subject of considerable discussion. 188  In the ICC context, 189  this is reflected in
particular in the report of the ICC Commission on “Techniques for Controlling Time and Costs in Arbitration” initially prepared
in 2007 and revised in 2012. It has also resulted in the 2017 amendments to the Rules to shorten the basic time limit for the
Terms of Reference to 30 days, to provide for the Expedited Procedure Provisions in art.30 and to monitor the arbitrators much
more closely on their availability and the speed with which they carry out their duties, as is reflected in the 2021 ICC Note.
22-4
The 2021 ICC Note includes a series of provisions that are intended to make ICC arbitration more time and cost-effective all
of which are intended to give substance to provisions of the Rules, and in particular art.22. Those provisions of the 2021 ICC
Note include the following:
 
-Part VII B on Expeditious and Efficient Conduct of the Arbitration
 
-Part VII D on Expeditious Determination of Manifestly Unmeritorious Claims and Defenses
 
-Part VIII on the Expedited Procedure Provisions
 
-Part IX on the Efficiency in the Submission of Draft Awards to the ICC
 
-Part XIV D on the Fixing of Fees (taking into account efficiency)
 
-Part XXIV C on Sealed Offers
22-5
With the 2021 ICC Note, the ICC is seeking to prod parties and Tribunals into being more effective. The consequences, as
discussed below are in particular to establish an entirely new and shorter procedure for smaller cases subject to the Expedited
Procedure Provisions discussed in more detail under art.30, to encourage bifurcation of certain issues so that they can be dealt
with at the outset, discussed under art.25, and to expedite drafting of awards as discussed under art.32. Nevertheless, these
measures are not shortcuts. Article 22(4) states that “the arbitral tribunal shall act fairly and impartially and ensure that each

186
The 2021 Rules have left art.22 unchanged from the 2017 Rules except for the addition of the last sentence in art.22(2)
and a drafting change in art.22(2) of the 2021 Rules.
187
In the Queen Mary University/White & Case 2018 International Arbitration Survey the four most valuable
characteristics of international arbitration were viewed by those surveyed as “enforceability of awards”, “avoiding
specific legal systems/national courts”, “flexibility” and “ability of parties to select arbitrators”. See http://
www.arbitration.qmul.ac.uk/research/2018/ [accessed 4 August 2021].
188
In the Queen Mary University/White & Case 2015 International Arbitration Survey the two worst characteristics of
international arbitration were viewed by those surveyed as “cost” and “lack of effective sanctions during the arbitral
process”. See http://www.arbitration.qmul.ac.uk/docs/164761.pdf, p.7.
189
The concerns about the delays and costs in international arbitration are not new, as can be seen from the fact that even
legislators have identified the problem and sought to address it. Thus, as discussed below, as early as 1996, the English
Arbitration Act included at s.33(b) explicit provisions towards increasing efficiency and speedy resolution of arbitral
disputes. More recently, s.1464(3) of the French Decree of 13 January 2011 included a provision expressly requiring
that “[b]oth parties and arbitrators shall act diligently and in good faith in the conduct of the proceedings”. (Translation
by Paris, The Home of International Arbitration, The 13 January 2011 Decree. The New French Arbitration Law, p.33.)
