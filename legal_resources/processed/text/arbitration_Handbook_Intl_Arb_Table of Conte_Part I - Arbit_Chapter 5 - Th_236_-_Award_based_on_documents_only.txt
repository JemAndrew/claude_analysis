Under art.3(5) of App.VI, the Tribunal is expressly allowed, after consulting with the parties, to decide the dispute “solely on
the basis of the documents submitted by the parties, with no hearing and no examination of witnesses or experts”. In accordance
with art.26(1), when a hearing is to be held, the Tribunal may conduct it “by videoconference, telephone or other appropriate
means of communication”. This provision differs from art.25(6) of the ICC Rules which allows the Tribunal to decide the case
solely on documents “unless any of the parties request a hearing”. Indeed, under the Expedited Procedure Provisions, a party’s
request for a hearing may no longer be sufficient to obtain an oral hearing. 671
30-53
Where the parties agree that the case should be decided on documents only, this provision will not create any issue. Where in
turn both parties request a hearing, even when acting under the Expedited Procedure Provisions, the Tribunal will have to very
carefully assess whether it should deny such a request. Admittedly, as per art.22(4) of the ICC Rules, the Tribunal must ensure
that each party has a reasonable opportunity to present its case. However, a party’s entitlement to a reasonable opportunity to
present its case does not necessarily oblige the Tribunal to hold a hearing, for instance to hear witnesses and experts. Indeed,
in many jurisdictions, a party’s entitlement to a reasonable opportunity to present its case does not oblige a Tribunal to hear
witnesses and experts, nor to hold a hearing even if a party requires so. 672
30-54
Whenever written witness statements or expert reports are on the record, and a party requests the possibility to cross-examine the
witness or the expert, it may be necessary to hold a hearing, unless the Tribunal determines that the witness or expert testimony
is not relevant. That would be the case if the dispute turns on facts that are sufficiently proven by documentary evidence. For the
Tribunal to come to that conclusion, it would however need to conduct a very careful analysis of the entire case at the time of
making the decision as to whether to allow or disallow a hearing. It would be unacceptable if a Tribunal subsequently dismissed
a claim for lack of evidence, if the very evidence was based on witness or expert testimony.
30-55
If a party is not provided with the opportunity to cross examine a witness which it seeks to cross examine, in the authors’
opinion, then it should be on the basis that the witness evidence is not material to the outcome of the case. To rely on such
witness evidence after denying the possibility of cross examination would be a breach of due process.
30-56
Hearings under the Expedited Procedure Provisions remain possible, but are likely to be shorter than in the normal case. For
instance, the Tribunal may forego opening statements, or limit them to a strict minimum, and may fix short time-limits for cross
examination, or may even want to proceed itself with the examination of witnesses. To be in a position to do so, it will have
to be very well prepared. In deciding whether to hold a hearing or not, the Tribunal will also have to consider the complexity
of the case. For an arbitrator, having a hearing is often a unique and sole opportunity to ask questions and thus to test whether
he or she has truly understood what the dispute is about. This opportunity has a price, and should not be too easily dismissed
in favour of the time factor.
30-57
