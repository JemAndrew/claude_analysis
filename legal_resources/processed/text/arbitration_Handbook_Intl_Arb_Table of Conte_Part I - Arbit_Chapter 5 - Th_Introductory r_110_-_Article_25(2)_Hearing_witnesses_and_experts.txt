Article 25(2): Hearing witnesses and experts
“The arbitral tribunal may decide to hear witnesses, experts appointed by the parties or any other person, in the presence
of the parties, or in their absence provided they have been duly summoned.”
25-21
Article 25(2) is permissive. The Tribunal may decide whether or not to hear witnesses, experts or more generally any other
person. However, a Tribunal must meet the requirements of the law of the place of arbitration discussed under art.19 and
art.22(4) and should meet the due process requirements of the place of probable enforcement if that is ascertainable. Therefore,
the permissive nature of art.25(2) is subject to limitations on due process, but also to the parties’ agreement. If both parties
request that the Tribunal hear witnesses, it will usually be obliged to do so and in light of the consensual nature of arbitration,
it will normally be well advised to do so. 355  The key factor is that a Tribunal must balance its discretion under art.25(2) with
the general requirement that it respect the parties’ agreement as to the procedure.
25-22
The ICC Rules do not give a definition of the term “witness” and do not state whether any person can be a witness or not. The
current practice, as reflected in art.4(2) of the IBA Rules on Evidence is that “any person may present evidence as a witness,
including a Party or a Party’s officer, employee or other representative”. 356  Indeed, in an ICC arbitration, anyone capable of
describing facts based on his own perception may be a witness, irrespective of his status as a party, employee, agent or the like.
This is only subject to the lex arbitri, and more precisely a mandatory rule of law applicable at the place of arbitration. In the
major places of arbitration, no such restrictions exist, although the local court practice may be quite different in that respect.
In France, testimonial evidence is permitted in commercial disputes, although in practice live witness testimony virtually never
happens. Even when a party testifies as the principle, “nul ne peut se constituer preuve soi-même” (“nobody can constitute
evidence for himself”) does not apply to commercial disputes, as has been recently confirmed by the French Supreme Court. 357
For an ICC Tribunal, the status and quality of a fact witness may have an impact when assessing the credibility and the weight
of the testimony, but it would normally not be a basis to exclude or ignore such evidence.
25-23
The ICC Rules do not give a definition either of the term “expert”, and art.25(2) does not distinguish between experts appointed
by the Tribunal and those by the parties, although the IBA Rules on Evidence do draw a distinction (in arts 5 and 6). The
categories of expert evidence are very broad.
25-24
The Tribunal has a discretion to decide on its own initiative to hear a person if it believes that it is important for the outcome of
the case. In such as case, the Tribunal will need, however, to seek the assistance of national courts having no coercive power or
imperium vis-à-vis the third parties who refuse to appear before it. Although the situation is not clear in various circuit courts
in the United States, it may be possible in some US states to use s.28 U.S.C. s.1782 to require a person to provide oral evidence
(as well as documentary evidence as discussed below) in support of foreign arbitration. 358

If a Tribunal considers that certain evidence is not necessary to decide the dispute, then the refusal to hear the witness or expert
should be based on relevance. 359  If the Tribunal decides that a witness statement or expert report is relevant, and therefore
intends to rely on it, the decision not to hear the witness or expert would raise serious procedural issues. As a general rule, a
factual or expert witness should be made available for cross examination unless the opposing party waives the right to cross-
examine the witness.
25-26
Cross examination or questioning of witnesses is viewed as an important element in determining whether evidence is reliable
or not. Cross examination originated with the common law systems and is seen as an essential element of due process in those
systems. However, the importance of questioning of witnesses by counsel is now a widely accepted practice in international
arbitration, although the method of questioning of witnesses and, above all, the time allowed for that questioning varies with
the Tribunal.
25-27
In civil law countries, it is a basic principle that the due process (contradictoire) must be respected. Therefore, the concept that
one party could work with a witness or expert to prepare a statement or report and that the other party would not have the
opportunity of questioning the witness or expert appears problematic, if much weight is to be given to such testimony.
25-28
The better view is that art.25(2) is permissive in the sense that it permits a Tribunal to rule that certain witness or expert testimony
is not required for deciding the case. 360  However, art.25(2) does not permit the Tribunal to take written evidence of witnesses or
experts into account and then refuse to hear the witness or expert, unless, of course, both parties would have waived such hearing.
25-29
Article 25(2) provides that the Tribunal can hear witnesses and experts in the absence of one of the parties if the party has been
duly summoned. This provision is intended to preclude a defaulting party from blocking the procedure. If one party defaults,
the Tribunal must, however, hear the relevant evidence, reach a decision and reflect that decision in a reasoned award. In ICC
arbitration, there is no possibility of “default award”, as there is in national court proceedings.
25-30
Article 25(2) does not set out any details as to how witness evidence should be presented. This is in part because the manner
of presenting evidence will vary from case to case. However, the IBA Rules on Evidence provide an outline as to how witness
evidence is often taken and the rules that Tribunals often apply in international arbitration. As noted above, the IBA Rules on
Evidence provide useful guidelines, particularly so that parties receive equal treatment. 361
25-31
On November 2020, the ICC Commission on Arbitration and ADR issued a Report on “The Accuracy of Fact Witness Memory in
International Arbitration: Current Issues and Possible Solutions”. 362  The Report considers existing research on witness memory
and discusses the importance of the accuracy of witness memory in Arbitration. The Report shows that witness memory can be
easily altered or contaminated and examines ways in which the participants in the arbitral process can reduce such distortions.
In particular, recommended steps for counsel focus on the conduct of interviews (e.g., keeping records, conducting interviews
witness by witness, asking open-ended, unbiased and non-leading questions). Recommendations for arbitrators include requiring
witness statements to contain information as to how they were prepared and reminding witnesses that they should only testify
on their own knowledge of events.
25-32
For many practitioners, the main point is that witness evidence is mainly helpful to the extent that it is based upon and explains
the contemporaneous documentary record. The documentary record is usually reasonably objective. A witness’s explanation
of why he or she wrote an email or drafted a document in a certain manner may bring the email or other document to life.
Where the witness evidence is not tied to the contemporaneous documentary record, it is much more likely that the recollection
will be subjective.
