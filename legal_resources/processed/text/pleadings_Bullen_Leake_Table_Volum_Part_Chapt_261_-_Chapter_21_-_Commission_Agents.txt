Chapter 21 - Commission Agents
21-01
As a result of the obligation to implement EU Council Directive 86/653, 1  the Commercial Agents (Council Directive)
Regulations 1993 (SI 1993/3053) were made by the Secretary of State for Trade and Industry. The 1993 Regulations came into
force on 1 January 1994 and apply to all contracts entered into under the agency agreement after that date, and whether or not
the agency agreement itself pre-dates the commencement date. The Regulations define a commercial agent as a self-employed
intermediary with continuing authority to negotiate the sale or purchase of goods on behalf of his principal or to negotiate and
conclude the sale or purchase of goods on behalf of and in the name of that principal. There must be a contractual relationship of
principal and agent between the parties so, for example, a sub-agent who had not directly contracted with the principal cannot
invoke the Regulations. 2  The Regulations are concerned with the sale and purchase of goods and not of services. Officers of
a company or association (or partners in the case of a partnership) are excluded from the definition, but there are few other
exceptions. The primary ones are where the commercial agent’s activities are secondary to his principal commercial agency
activities, 3  or are unpaid, or where he is operating on a commodity exchange or in the commodity market.
In performing his activities, a commercial agent must look after the interests of his principal and act dutifully and in good faith
(reg.3). He has three specific obligations: (a) the duty to make proper efforts to negotiate and where appropriate conclude the
transactions he is instructed to take care of; (b) the duty to communicate to his principal all necessary information available to
him; and (c) the duty to comply with his principal’s reasonable instructions.
The principal has a similar duty to act dutifully and in good faith (reg.4). This duty includes the obligation to provide all
necessary documentation relating to the goods concerned as well as information necessary for the performance of the agency
contract. In particular, where the principal knows that the volume of commercial transactions are likely to decrease to less than
normally expected, he must tell his agent.
The parties are not free to contract out of these duties. Furthermore, the 1993 Regulations apply where an agent’s operations take
effect within the EU on behalf of a principal situated outside the EU. This cannot be overridden by a choice of law purporting
to choose the law of a non-EU jurisdiction. 4
21-02
Part III of the regulations deals with remuneration. In the absence of agreed terms as to remuneration, the agent is entitled to
remuneration which commercial agents appointed for the goods forming the subject of the agency are customarily allowed, in
the place where the agent carries on his activities. In the absence of such a custom, it is then that he is entitled to reasonable
remuneration taking into account all the aspects of the transaction.
Part IV of the 1993 Regulations deals with the conclusion and termination of the agency contract including a statutory right
to compensation contained in reg.17. There is also a specific limitation on the right to impose restraint of trade clauses. For
the purposes of reg.17 the “agency contract” is to be widely construed as meaning the agency itself rather than the particular
contract in force at the date of termination. 5
The guidelines on the calculation of indemnity are usefully summarised in AMB Imballaggi Plastici Srl v Pacflex Ltd. 6
The compensation provisions on termination operate when the contract expires by effluxion of time. 7

The 1993 Regulations were amended by the Commercial Agents (Council Directive) (Amendment) Regulations 1998 (SI
1998/2868) which came into force on 16 December 1998. Regulation 2 amends the 1993 Regulations to allow a court or tribunal
to apply the law of another Member State where the parties have agreed that the agency contract is to be governed by the law
of that Member State.
In Sagal (trading as Bunz UK) v Atelier Bunz GmbH, 8  the Court of Appeal decided that the definition of ‘commercial agent’
in the 1993 Regulations connoted an agent who had to obtain the authority of his principal to enter into a contract whereupon
the contract would be made in the principal’s name and not that of the agent. While an agent can, in English law, be authorised
to contract in his own name, such an agent falls outside the Regulations.
In Software Incubator Ltd v Computer Associates UK Ltd, 9  the Queen’s Bench Mercantile Court (HH Judge Waksman QC)
held that the sale or supply of software promoted by the claimant company counted as the “sale of goods” for the purpose of
the definition of “commercial agent” in reg.2(1) of the 1993 Regulations.
21-03
In Lonsdale (trading as Lonsdale Agencies) v Howard & Hallam Ltd, 10  the House of Lords held that, for the purposes of
compensation, reference to the value of the agency was based on the assumption that it continued, as being the amount which
an agent could reasonably expect to receive for the right to stand in his shoes, continue to perform the duties of the agency and
receive the commission which he would have received. The agent was entitled to be compensated for the damage he suffered as
a result of the termination of his relations with the principal; he was treated as having lost something of value as a result of the
termination and was entitled to compensation for the loss. The value of the agency relationship lay in the prospect of earning
commission and what had to be valued was the income stream which the agency would have generated. English courts were
entitled to apply English rules for assessing this compensation and were not bound by the practice in other EU jurisdictions
(notably France) of assessing compensation by applying a fixed multiplier to the lost annual income.
In Semen v Deutsche Tamoil GmbH 11  the First Chamber of the ECJ decided two important points about indemnity of
commercial agents under the Directive. First, it is not possible automatically to limit the amount of indemnity to which a
commercial agent is entitled in respect of loss of commission where his actual loss would exceed that limit. Secondly, if an agent
is employed by one of a group of companies, benefits accruing to other companies of that group are not, in principle, deemed
to be benefits accruing to the principal and, consequently, do not necessarily have to be taken into account for the purposes of
calculating the amount of indemnity to which a commercial agent is entitled.
Although not strictly arising out of the 1993 Regulations, the Supreme Court’s decision in Re D&D Wines International Ltd (In
Liquidation); Bailey v Angove’s Pty Ltd 12  should be noted. Though the general rule of law is that authority of the agent may
be revoked by the principal at any time, even if expressed to be irrevocable in the agency agreement, such authority may be
treated as irrevocable if two conditions are met. First, the agency agreement must expressly make it irrevocable and, secondly,
the authority had to secure an interest of the agent, being either a proprietary interest or a liability owed to him personally. In
those cases, the agent’s authority was irrevocable while the interest subsisted.
