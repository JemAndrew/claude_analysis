Construction and formalities
The Statute of Frauds 1677
27-07
The distinction between a guarantee and an indemnity is vital. Under the Statute of Frauds 1677 s.4, a guarantee must be made
in writing or evidenced by a note or memorandum in writing, in either case signed by or on behalf of the guarantor. Unlike
other requirements of the Statute of Frauds, equity did not mitigate this rule and it remains in full vigour. It should be noted in
this context, however, that, if the surety’s obligation arises in respect of an agreement regulated by the Consumer Credit Act
1974 (CCA), then it is irrelevant whether the contract of surety is on its true construction a guarantee or an indemnity because
all surety contracts are required by the CCA to comply with the requirements of the Act and its dependent regulations as to the
negotiation, form, content and enforcement of surety contracts (see para.27-09).
If the document signed by the guarantor refers to some other document or transaction, external evidence is admissible to explain
the reference or to identify the document, and the document signed by the guarantor can be read together with another document
to constitute an enforceable guarantee. 9
The consideration for a guarantee need not, however, be in writing (Mercantile Law Amendment Act 1856 s.3). On the other
hand no formalities are required by law for an indemnity.
27-08
Outside the confines of the CCA, therefore, the requirements of the Statute of Frauds make it imperative to ascertain into which
category of surety an agreement falls and, indeed, whether an agreement is truly a surety agreement. As with all areas of contract
where categorisation is relevant, a court will have regard to the substance of the contract and not necessarily to its wording.
The acid test is whether the obligation of the surety is collateral to and dependent on the obligations of the principal debtor, in
which case it is a guarantee and the statute applies or whether the surety’s obligations are primary obligations, in which case
it is an indemnity and the formalities are unnecessary. 10
It is thus a commonplace of litigation where there has been an informal assumption by one person of another person’s obligations,
for the beneficiary (the creditor) to argue that the assumption constituted an enforceable indemnity and for the apparent surety
to argue that it constituted a guarantee rendered unenforceable for want of the essential formalities.
A second area of dispute arises when a creditor seeks to rely on an undertaking by the apparent surety which does not, on
analysis, amount to a contract of surety. Thus a promise made only to the debtor to put him in funds to meet his liabilities to the
creditor creates no obligation to the creditor to guarantee those liabilities. 11  Similarly the “letter of comfort” once so fashionable
in commercial circles whereby a person indicated in general terms that he would give financial support to a third party was held
not to be intended to have legal effect and to create no obligation to anyone who dealt with the third party on the strength of it. 12

The Consumer Credit Act 1974
27-09
Surety, in respect of contracts governed by the Consumer Credit Act 1974, is covered by the elaborate code laid down by that
Act. The Act and its regulations prescribe the form and content of surety agreements and the formalities required to activate the
contract of surety and to enforce it against the surety. Special procedural rules apply to consumer credit cases and the subject
is dealt with in more detail in Section 20: Credit, Finance and Leasing to which the reader is referred. On 6 April 2008, all the
pre-existing financial ceilings for regulation were removed and thus all credit or hiring agreements to which an individual (or
some groups of individuals) is a party as debtor or hirer became regulated by the Consumer Credit Act 1974, unless expressly
exempted from regulation (e.g. some business agreements, some mortgages and some utilities agreements). Consequently the
defences available to a surety under that legislation became a great deal more important after 6 April 2008 and the practitioner
is advised when dealing with any surety agreement entered into after that date to check whether the principal agreement was
regulated by the CCA 1974 and, if so, whether the formalities of that Act have been complied with.
The new provisions relating to the supply of information during the currency of the agreement and on termination have greatly
added to the range of circumstances in which a regulated agreement becomes unenforceable against the debtor or hirer and
hence against any surety. See para.20-40.
27-10
The implementation of the EU Consumer Credit Directive (2008/48/EC) 13  on 1 February 2011 by a series of changes to the
CCA 1974 and to its dependent regulations greatly complicated the law relating to regulated agreements. In some areas there are
three parallel systems for documenting agreements (dependent on date or function of the agreement) and the formalities both
before making the agreement and when drafting the agreement itself have been made much more complex. In all cases where
the CCA applies therefore, the first task of the lawyer representing the surety is to examine the circumstances surrounding the
making and documenting of the principal regulated agreement to ascertain whether it is enforceable against the principal debtor
and the second task is to enquire into the workings of the agreement to see whether by failing to serve information statements,
default notices and the like or by failing to ensure that these documents were in the prescribed form, the creditor has rendered
the agreement either permanently or temporarily unenforceable against debtor and surety.
Where rights are rendered temporarily unenforceable, they continue to exist and, once the circumstances arise which render
them enforceable again, enforcement proceedings can be commenced against debtor and surety. 14
One of the key points to watch about the amended CCA is that, since 1 February 2011 most regulated credit agreements have
been subject to a right of withdrawal within 14 days of the making of the agreement. Withdrawal by the principal debtor ought,
of course, to discharge any surety and it is likely that the courts will so hold but it should be realised that, unlike the position
with cancellation of a regulated agreement, the CCA does not legislate expressly for the discharge of sureties on withdrawal.
27-11
A further tier of complication was added in 2014. Parliament decided to incorporate consumer credit and consumer hire into
the system of regulation of financial services created by the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA). The regulation
of consumer credit was removed from the Office of Fair Trading (which was abolished) and handed over to the new Financial
Conduct Agency (FCA). Considerable areas of the CCA were repealed, to be replaced by provisions of FSMA or its dependant
statutory instruments. The exemptions from CCA regulation, for example, are now to be found in the Financial Services and
Markets Act 2000 (Regulated Activities) Order 2001 (SI 2001/544) (the RAO).
This area is made more difficult by the practice of the FCA (and its predecessor, the Financial Services Authority) to issue
handbooks and sourcebooks, containing rules and guidance for the industries regulated. The principal sourcebook for the
consumer credit industry, CONC, contains detailed rules governing every aspect of the relationship between the lender, the
borrower and the surety.
These rules have two consequences. First FSMA itself, in s.138D (formerly s.150) confers a right of action on any private
person who suffers loss as a result of a contravention of a rule made by the FCA. This would include a surety who had been
induced to guarantee a debt by conduct on the part of the lender which contravened CONC. For a precedent see 20-F30.

Secondly, a surety has a like right to the borrower to seek to have any credit agreement made with an individual (whether
or not such an agreement would be ordinarily regulated by the CCA) reopened under CCA ss.140A–140C on the ground of
unfair relationships. The scope of such a claim has been widened by the introduction of FSA/FCA handbooks because, quite
independently of FSMA s.138D, breach of the appropriate handbook is likely to be taken as constituting unfairness while the
Supreme Court has decided that compliance with the handbook does not necessarily negative unfairness. 15
Thirdly it should be noted that even in circumstances where the CCA does not apply, a guarantor of the obligations of a debtor
(particularly a corporate debtor) may be treated as a consumer for the purposes of the Unfair Terms in Consumer Contracts
Regulations 1999 (SI 1999/2083) or, now, Pt 2 of the Consumer Rights Act 2015—see the ECJ case of Dumitra v BRD Groupe
Societe Generale (Case 534/15). 16
