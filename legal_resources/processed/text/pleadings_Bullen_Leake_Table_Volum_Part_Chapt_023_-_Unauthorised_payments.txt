Breach of mandate
9-09
The primary duty of a paying bank is to honour its customer’s instructions and make the payment 36  in accordance with the
mandate. 37  The payment instruction can take a number of forms, including a cheque, an order to pay by direct debit, a standard
order, or an electronic funds transfer instruction 38  (e.g. transfers effected by online banking, or withdrawals via an ATM). In
all such cases, however, the fundamental principle is the same: where a bank makes a payment in the absence of authority from
its customer, it has no right of indemnity against its customer, and hence no entitlement to debit the customer’s account with
the amount of the payment. 39
However, a bank is only obliged to honour a cheque or other payment order if there are sufficient funds in the customer’s account
to meet the cheque or order, or if the bank has agreed to provide the customer with overdraft facilities to meet the cheque or
order. In drawing a cheque or otherwise requesting a payment against inadequate funds, a customer impliedly requests the bank
to grant an overdraft (or an increased overdraft facility, as the case may be). The bank then has an option whether or not to accede
to that request and hence honour the cheque or payment order. 40  If the bank elects to honour the cheque, bringing the account
into overdraft, it does so (in the absence of an agreement to the contrary) upon its standard terms as to interest and charges. 41
Where the payment instructions received by the bank are ambiguous, the bank must at least adopt a reasonable interpretation
of its instructions, although the prudent course is for the bank to seek clarification from its customer. 42  If it fails to do so,
the bank may find that it has acted in the absence of, or contrary, to its instructions (as properly interpreted), and hence face
a claim for breach of mandate. 43
A sum standing to the credit of a joint account is a debt owed by the bank to the account holders jointly. Where the mandate in
respect of the joint account requires that the paying bank will only make payments out of the account on the joint signatures of
both of the account holders, there is a separate agreement between the bank and each of the account holders that the bank will
not honour drawings unless both account holders have signed for them. In an action for breach of mandate where the bank has
wrongfully honoured an instruction by a single account holder it is not necessary to join all the account holders as parties to
the proceedings. 44  The innocent joint account holder must be able to establish that the funds wrongfully transferred out of the
account were his property. Where such facts cannot be proved, the innocent holder is entitled only to recover a moiety of the
amounts paid out. 45  Where a bank is found liable in these circumstances it may be able to claim contribution from the recipient
of the funds wrongfully paid out for moneys had and received and/or for breach of warranty of authority.
A particular form of breach of mandate arises where a paying bank purports to honour a cheque or other bill of exchange bearing
a forged signature. In such circumstances, the payment is, by s.24 of the Bills of Exchange Act 1882, a nullity, so the bank is
not entitled to debit the customer’s account.

Defences
9-10
The principal defences available to a paying bank which has made a payment in breach of mandate are as follows:
 
(1)The loss was caused by the customer’s breach of its duty to the bank to refrain from drawing the cheque in such a
manner as to facilitate fraud or forgery (the “Macmillan duty”). 46  The paradigm example is where the customer signs a
blank cheque. Beyond that, whether a failure by the customer to take ordinary precautions amounts to a breach of this
duty is a question of degree. 47
 
(2)The customer is estopped from asserting the forgery by reason of the breach of its duty to the bank in failing to inform
the bank of any forgery on the account as soon as the customer became aware of it (the “Greenwood duty”). 48
 
(3)The customer is estopped from asserting the forgery by reason of an express representation that the cheque or payment
order was genuine. 49
 
(4)The bank is in any event entitled to be indemnified since the payment discharged a debt owed by the customer to the
beneficiary of the payment (the “Liggett defence”). 50  However, the “mere fact” of the payment, if unauthorised, will not
usually have the effect of discharging the debt. 51  Generally, the debt will only be discharged if the payment is authorised
or subsequently ratified. 52  There may, however, be other circumstances not amounting to ratification in which allowing
the customer to recover for breach of mandate would give rise to an unjust enrichment, in which case the defence would
operate. 53
 
(5)The payment was ratified. For ratification to apply, the customer must have (a) expressly or impliedly manifested an
unequivocal intention to adopt the unauthorised payment, and (b) done so in full knowledge that the payment was without
authority. 54  Ratification will only be implied where one cannot logically analyse the act of the customer without imputing
his approval of the payment. 55  This defence is not available where the bank has paid a cheque bearing a forged signature. 56
There is, however, no general defence of contributory negligence available to the paying bank. In Tai Hing Cotton Mill Ltd v
Liu Cong Hing Bank Ltd it was argued that the relationship of banker and customer gives rise to a duty owed by the customer
to the bank to exercise such precautions as a reasonable customer in his position would take to prevent forged cheques being
presented to the bank, or at the very least to check his monthly statements so as to be able to notify the bank of any items which
were not, or indeed not have been, authorised by him. However, this argument was rejected by the Privy Council, which held
that the implied obligations upon the customer were limited to the Macmillan and Greenwood duties referred to above.
Payments where the bank is on notice of fraud
9-11
Where a bank is on notice that an agent acting on behalf of a customer is misusing his authority in order to misappropriate his
principal’s money, the bank faces two conflicting obligations. On the one hand, a bank is under a duty to its customer to honour
payment instructions given in accordance with the customer’s mandate, whilst on the other hand there is an implied duty in the
contract between bank and customer that the bank will exercise reasonable care in executing its customer’s orders. 57  Hence, a
bank may incur liability in honouring its customer’s cheques even if such cheques are drawn in accordance with the mandate.
The balance between these conflicting duties was considered in Barclays Bank Plc v Quincecare and Lipkin Gorman v Karpnale
Ltd. In Quincecare, Steyn J held that the fair balance was that a banker must refrain from executing an order if and for so
long as he is put on inquiry in the sense that he has reasonable grounds (although not necessarily proof) for believing that the
order is an attempt to misappropriate the funds of the company. In Lipkin Gorman, May LJ said 58  that “it is in my opinion
only when the circumstances are such that any reasonable cashier would hesitate to pay a cheque at once and refer it to his

or her superior, and when any reasonable superior would hesitate to authorise payment without enquiry, that a cheque should
not be paid immediately upon presentation and such enquiry made”. May LJ emphasised that this would only arise in rare
circumstances. The standard of care was formulated by Parker LJ as follows 59 :
“The question must be whether if a reasonable and honest banker knew of the relevant facts he would have
considered that there was a serious or real possibility albeit not amounting to a probability that his customer might
be being defrauded.”
Quincecare was applied in Singularis Holdings Ltd v Daiwa Capital Markets Europe Ltd, 60  the first case in which the court has
found that a financial institution acted in breach of the Quincecare duty. At first instance, Rose J held that the Quincecare duty
“require[s] a bank to do something more than accept at face value whatever strange documents and implausible explanations are
proffered by the officers of a company facing serious financial difficulties” and found on the facts that there had been “obvious”
and “glaring” signs that the director was perpetrating a fraud on the company. 61
A refusal to make a payment may be justified under money laundering or related legislation. Section 238 of the Proceeds
of Crime Act 2002 creates an offence of becoming concerned in an arrangement which the defendant “knows or suspects”
facilitates (by whatever means) the acquisition, retention, use or control of criminal property by or on behalf of another person.
Where a bank suspects that a money laundering offence may be committed by complying with a payment instruction it must,
in order to avoid committing an offence itself, make an authorised disclosure under s.338 and seek consent under s.335 from
the National Crime Agency. That obligation would appear to conflict with the bank’s obligation to comply with instructions
in compliance with its mandate. The solution is found in Shah v HSBC Private Bank (UK) Ltd, 62  where it was held that there
is an implied term in the contract between the bank and the customer such that the bank is permitted to refuse to execute a
payment instruction where it suspected that the transaction constituted money laundering and was awaiting consent under s.335
to perform the transaction.
