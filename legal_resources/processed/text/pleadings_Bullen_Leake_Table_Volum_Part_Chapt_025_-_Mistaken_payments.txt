Mistaken payments
9-13
From time to time, a bank may make a payment out of an account by mistake, for example by honouring the same instruction
twice, by overlooking a countermand, or by mistakenly honouring the instructions of one of two account holders under a
joint account which requires the signatures of both. Where the bank makes such a payment then, unless there is subsequent
ratification from the customer, the payment is authorised, and hence the bank cannot claim an indemnity from the customer. In
these circumstances it must look to the payee for recovery, and the legal basis is restitution for mistake.
It is important to note, first, that the restitutionary claim is personal in nature, i.e. it is not necessary to demonstrate that the
bank has retained title to the money paid over. 72  Second, the fact that the bank may have acted negligently in making the
mistake does not defeat the claim in restitution. 73  Third, the quantum of the claim is measured by the enrichment received
by the recipient, rather than the loss suffered by the payor (since a claim in restitution is not a claim for compensation). 74
Fourth, following Kleinwort Benson v Lincoln City Council it is no longer necessary to establish that the bank’s mistake was
one of fact rather than law.
Conditions for recovery
9-14
The principles of restitution for mistake were summarised in Barclays Bank Ltd v WJ Simms, Son & Cooke (Southern) Ltd 75 :
 
(1)If a person pays money to another under a mistake 76  which causes him to make the payment, he is prima facie entitled
to recover it.
 
(2)His claim may however fail if:
 
(a)the payer intends that the payee shall have the money at all events, whether the fact be true or false, or is deemed
in law so to intend; or
 
(b)the payment is made for good consideration, in particular if the money is paid to discharge, and does discharge, a
debt owed to the payee (or a principal on whose behalf he is authorised to receive the payment) by the payer or by a
third party by whom he is authorised to discharge the debt; or
 
(c)the payee has changed his position in good faith, or is deemed in law to have done so.
However, the bank has no restitutionary right against the payee on the basis that it held the mistaken belief that there were
sufficient funds or facilities to meet the cheque or transfer, since in those circumstances the payment is within the mandate and
the bank can have recourse to its customer. 77

Defences
9-15
A recipient of a mistaken payment has a defence in so far as he has changed his position in paying away all or part of the
payment in circumstances where it would be inequitable in all the circumstances to require him to make restitution. 78  The
defence operates pro tanto. It is not necessary for the recipient to have changed its position in reliance upon the validity of
the payment but there must be a causative link between the change of position and the receipt of the mistaken payment. 79
Hence, the defence will only operate where the recipient can identify some form of detriment, and the mere spending of the
money will not of itself normally suffice; rather, the recipient must have incurred “extraordinary expenditure”, i.e. he must have
entered into a transaction which he would not have entered into had he not received the payment. 80  The defence is not limited
to detriments incurred after the mistaken payment: a defendant who has changed his position in anticipation of the future receipt
of a payment can equally rely on the defence of change of position. 81  The defence is not available, however, to a recipient who
has changed his position in bad faith (for example where he has spent the money knowing that he was not entitled to it) 82  or
to a recipient who has acted illegally. 83
Estoppel is also an established defence to a claim for restitution. 84  The essential elements of the estoppel are: (a) a representation
of fact by the payer leading the payee reasonably to believe that he was entitled to treat the money as his own; (b) detrimental
reliance upon the representation by the payee (usually equating to the change of position); and (c) the payment must not
have been primarily caused by the fault of the payee. 85  Although a representation by conduct may in some circumstances be
sufficient, the mistaken payment itself is not a representation which can give rise to an estoppel; there must normally be some
further indication of the payee’s supposed title to the payment. 86  According to the orthodox approach, estoppel operates as a
complete defence to a restitutionary claim. 87  However, the courts have recognised that this could lead to injustice. In National
Westminster Bank Plc v Somer, 88  the Court of Appeal held that there was a recognised exception, namely that the estoppel
should not operate in full where it would be clearly inequitable or unconscionable for the defendant to retain a balance of the
mistaken payment in his hands. Clark LJ noted the tension between the rule and the exception, and recognised that in practice
it would very often be unconscionable for a recipient to retain the whole of the sums paid to him. The alternative approach,
applied by the Court of Appeal in Scottish Equitable Plc v Derby, 89  is to allow a defence of change of position to “pre-empt”
the defence of estoppel, again so as to achieve a pro tanto result.
Where the bank has received money paid by mistake, it may itself face a restitutionary claim. If it has received the money on
behalf of a customer, the defence of “ministerial receipt” may be available (see the section on defences to constructive trust
claims, para.9-E13). In some circumstances, a bank which receives money paid by mistake may hold the funds on trust to repay
the paying party: this is most likely to arise if the payment was made at its request, the fund is identifiable, and the recipient
bank has notice of the claim. 90
