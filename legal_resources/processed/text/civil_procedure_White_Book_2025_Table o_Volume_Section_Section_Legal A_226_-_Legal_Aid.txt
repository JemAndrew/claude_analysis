General Introduction
7D-1+
On 1 May 2012 the Legal Aid Sentencing and Punishment of Offenders Act 2012 (“LASPO”) received royal assent. Part 1
of LASPO is concerned with legal aid. The Legal Aid, Sentencing and Punishment of Offenders Act 2012 (Commencement
No.6) Order 2013/453 brought the bulk of Pt 1 into force as from 1 April 2013 as well as Sch.1 (“Civil Legal Services”).
The Access to Justice Act 1999 set up the scheme for the grant of public funding which was operated by the Legal Services
Commission. Section 38, LASPO abolished the Commission and replaced it with a Director of Legal Aid Casework to apply
directions and guidance to be issued by the Lord Chancellor, acting independently of him (s.4, LASPO). The Lord Chancellor
has the duty to make legal aid available for the purposes of civil and criminal law and therefore to exercise the functions
granted to him by statute (see s.1). The extent to which he is entitled to make legal aid available are heavily circumscribed
by Sch.1 to LASPO and its amendments.
Under LASPO new statutory provisions take the place of exceptional funding directions. Section 10 enables additional
proceedings to be brought within the scope of legal aid where to fail to do so would constitute a breach of “(i) the individual’s
Convention rights (within the meaning of the Human Rights Act 1998), or (ii) any rights of the individual to the provision
of legal services that are enforceable EU rights” (see s.10(3)(a)). Where the Director has made a wider public interest
determination, exceptional case funding for coroners inquests may be available.
It is not the intention of this part of The White Book Service to consider the circumstances in which legal aid may be granted
under the legal aid regime, or to set out the rates of remuneration which may be payable. The statutory framework of the
equivalent part of LASPO itself is far too detailed to be able to provide any meaningful summary assistance even by the
selection of key extracts. The system of providing funding services subject to exclusions, now governed by s.9 of LASPO
and Pts 1–4 of Sch.1, will be familiar to practitioners in the area.
For the larger part the 2013 and previous editions included the following relevant to civil (non-family) proceedings arising
out of the 1999 Act scheme: the statutory charge and the right of set-off under a Lockley order (s.10(7), Access to Justice
Act 1999 and Pt III, the Community Legal Service (Financial) Regulations 2000 (SI 2000/516)); security for costs ( reg.6,
the Community Legal Service (Costs) Regulations 2000 (SI 2000/441)); costs protection and orders for costs against funded
clients and the LSC (s.11, Access to Justice Act 1999 and Pt II, the Community Legal Service (Costs) Regulations 2000
(SI 2000/441)) and the Community Legal Service (Cost Protection) Regulations 2000 (SI 2000/824). These regulations are
preserved in the 2013 edition.
Sections 25 and 26, LASPO, are the statutory provisions which allow similar or equivalent regulations to the above to be made
in respect of legal aid, and the final parts of this section contain the further regulations in these areas now made under LASPO.
Under the LASPO regime, the detailed provisions as to the statutory charge are set out in the Civil Legal Aid (Statutory
Charge) Regulations 2013 (SI 2013/503). Provisions concerning costs protection are contained in Pts 2 and 3 of the Civil
Legal Aid (Costs) Regulations 2013 SI 2013/611) of which reg.12 sets out the legislative rules as to security for costs. These
are included in full in this section below. In some circumstances cases funded under the 1999 Act regime will fall under the
LASPO scheme for the purposes of costs protection and the statutory charge by virtue of transitional provisions.

Whilst practitioners working on legally aided cases are entitled only to prescribed rates and fixed fees, in the event of a
costs order being made in the client’s favour “the amount of the costs to be paid under a client’s costs order or client’s costs
agreement shall … be determined on the same basis as it would be if the costs were to be paid to a person who had not
received funded services”: reg.21(1), the Civil Legal Aid (Costs) Regulations 2013. Regulation 21(3) contains the necessary
