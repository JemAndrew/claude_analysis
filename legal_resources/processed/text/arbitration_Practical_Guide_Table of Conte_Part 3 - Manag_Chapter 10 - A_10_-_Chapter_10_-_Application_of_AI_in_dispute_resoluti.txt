Chapter 10 - Application of AI in dispute resolution
Overview
10-001
The emergence and rapid development of artificial intelligence (AI) in dispute resolution sets the scene for a transformative
shift in how legal disputes are managed and resolved. This evolution is being driven by the integration of sophisticated AI
technologies into the legal field, aiming to enhance efficiency, reduce costs and make dispute resolution more accessible.
The primary objective of this chapter is to provide a backdrop to the anticipated changes and to prompt the reader to think about
how this may affect their role and impact on strategies for dispute resolution.
A significant challenge in writing this chapter is that, as it is an attempt to look into a crystal ball and predict the future, it is
certain that some of the comments, amounting to forecasts, will turn out to be wrong. Nevertheless, having considered things
carefully, the hope is that this section will still be helpful and, on balance, more right than wrong.
AI technologies in dispute resolution
Generative AI
10-002
For the purposes of this section, the acronym “AI” is used to denote “generative AI” rather than, for example, the use of a
standard search engine. Whilst a deep technical understanding of how AI works is not necessary, in order to properly assess
its impact, it is still important to have an awareness of how generative AI works and why it is fundamentally different to
a simple search engine.
A conventional search engine finds existing information that matches your search query. This matching takes place by
crawling and indexing the web (or specific datasets) and using algorithms to match a query to relevant existing content. This
is the equivalent of a librarian who helps you find a specific book on a shelf. In contrast, generative AI creates entirely new
content, including, for example, legal advice, based on the information that it has been trained on. This training includes
learning patterns and relationships contained within its data.
Comparing the outputs, generative AI creates original text. This contrasts with a search engine that finds existing things and
delivers pre-existing content. Stepping back, generative AI adapts to different prompts and styles whilst search engines rely
on specific key words or combinations of key words. 1
AI technologies

In considering AI technologies, it helps to understand the difference between an AI platform and an AI model. An AI platform
is a comprehensive environment that provides the tools and technologies for developing, training and deploying AI models.
In this sense, it includes hardware, software, and services to handle data preprocessing and the deployment of the model.
By way of illustration, GPT4o is a model, not a platform and runs using OpenAI's infrastructure with interfaces through
partnering with Microsoft's Azure OpenAI Service.
In considering the evolution of AI in legal dispute resolution services, without making any recommendations of which is
better, it is worth briefly considering examples of the current leading platforms. Examples of broad-based AI platforms
from leading providers include Google AI Platform, Microsoft Azure AI, Amazon Sagemaker/AI Services, H2O.ai and IBM
Watson Studio. This is not a recommendation of any of those platforms but, by considering the companies behind them, it
is easy to identify possible differences emerging. For example, Google can draw on the strengths of its vast Google Cloud
Ecosystem, whereas Microsoft Azure AI has a tighter integration with other Azure services and is already offering tools
integrated with the Microsoft Office suite.
Whilst sharing technologies, a platform can also deliberately focus on supporting specific needs. For example, H2O.ai
supports a wide range of programming languages and is noted to be strong in predictive analytics suitable for business
modelling. In contrast, IBM Watson Studio has an emphasis on enterprise collaboration. There are also some very specific
platforms, for example, focused on graphics acceleration or sales activities.
In contrast, an AI model is a specific algorithm that has been trained on data to make predictions, decisions or perform tasks.
It represents the core intelligence of an AI solution, encapsulating the learned patterns from data. The platform supports
the infrastructure and tools needed for AI development. The model is the outcome of the AI development process. In this
context, most users considering AI legal services will encounter the leading models that include OpenAI ChatGPT (providing
versatile text generation in different styles), Google Gemini (based on Google’s factual database with a focus on factual
accuracy), Microsoft CoPilot (focused on document creation, analysis and visualisation, and coding with integration into the
Microsoft Office ecosystem), and LaMDA (using Google AI focusing on dialogue and informational responses). These are
not recommendations but examples.
An AI model user interface can appear to be as simple as entering a question/request into a box, possibly having uploaded
some documents. Behind the scenes, there are some very different technologies at work. These include natural language
processing (NLP) 2  (not to be confused with neuro-linguistic programming), machine learning (ML), 3  large language models
(LLM), 4  neural networks, 5  specific generative models 6  and optimisation algorithms.
The flexible way that these elements can be coupled together on different AI platforms supports the conclusion that, ultimately,
although AI’s use in dispute resolution is inevitable, the path to an industry standard is likely to be akin to the rise and fall
of VHS, Betamax and Philips V2000 video players where VHS won, despite not being the absolute best. 7  A modern-day
equivalent is how streaming apps for watching movies have developed, eventually converging on Netflix, Amazon Prime
and Apple TV as parallel standards (with users of other less well-known options very happily committed to their choice).
In the context of legal disputes, recognising that providers of legal services can be classified broadly into defined practice
areas and functions, the future will probably not end up with one super-AI tool for legal services based only on one platform.
Instead, it is likely that different tools for different situations will evolve and, in the end, probably consolidate under a few
dominant brands/user-interfaces.
In the forthcoming transition, it is worth remembering that “perfect is the enemy of good” and, in such a rapidly developing
area, early adopters are likely to gain an advantage on those who wait for the perfect solution to arrive.
Despite the different technologies, there are some common issues and concerns that apply to all applications of AI in the
legal services/dispute resolution sector.
Natural language processing (NLP)
10-004
NLP enables computers to understand, interpret and generate human language. The core capabilities of NLP are the ability to
sort text into categories, break down sentences into parts of speech and identify the relationships between words. In dispute

resolution, NLP can be used to analyse legal documents, contracts and communications between parties to identify relevant
facts, patterns, and, most importantly, the legal issues that arise. This capability can significantly reduce the amount of time
spent, and skill required, for document review. In addition, a significant benefit is that AI can “read” thousands of pages
in seconds and provide an analysis of the content ranging from a high-level summary of the potential issues through to
identifying strengths, weaknesses of the case, and missing documents.
LLMs work under the umbrella of NLP (having identified the text and what each word means) and are neural network-based
models that learn to predict the next word or sequence of words given a previous sequence. In this sense, LLM’s form the
fundamental building blocks of generative AI with their ability to produce human-like original text, summarise topics and
answer questions. The potential to increase efficiency and accuracy, at the same time as substantially reducing costs, means
that the early adoption of AI will be in analysing large volumes of documentation. Although not directly relevant to dispute
resolution, it is clear that work currently done by solicitors or barristers on issues such as disclosures has the potential to
become fully automated.
Machine learning (ML)
10-005
ML, a subset of AI, involves the development of algorithms that can learn from and make predictions or decisions based on
the information that is made available for analysis and the questions asked. In the context of dispute resolution, it is possible
for ML algorithms to analyse historical dispute resolution outcomes to assess the strengths and weaknesses of both parties’
positions and, therefore, influence the expectations and strategy for settlement of a dispute. 8  If both sides had access to the
same level of AI resource, and if the chances of success were analysed to be particularly high/low, the logical outcome is
that proceedings would never start and one or the other party would make an offer. This may seem like a good thing but, as
discussed in more detail in para.10-030, there are potential unintended consequences that might end up with injustice being
created if one party is AI enabled and the other is not.
Automated decision systems
10-006
Automated decision systems use AI to facilitate or make decisions in dispute resolution processes. In the short term, it is highly
unlikely that such decisions will have any legal power unless, as with an arbitration clause, the parties have unequivocally
agreed to be bound. It is much more likely that these systems will start by offering an indication of the strengths and
weaknesses of both sides and, with reference to particular statute or case law, provide an indication of the likely outcome and
probability of success/failure. This should be considered as the AI equivalent of early neutral evaluation.
Whilst this might seem far-fetched, at the time of writing, there are already AI-enabled applications operating in the US
market 9  which provide a simple “judgment” based on analysing the documents and submissions received from the parties.
AI technology–application
Application of AI: prediction of outcome of dispute
10-007
An early study of the ability of AI to predict outcomes was carried out in 2017 using data from 240,000 justice votes and
28,000 case outcomes from US Supreme Court rulings over the period 1816–2015. 10  This assessment used a traditional
structured statistical approach based on a classification algorithm to predict whether the outcome was that the appeal was

“affirmed”, “reversed” or “other”. Comparing the predicted outcomes with the case outcomes, the accuracy of the model
developed was 71.9%.
In contrast to the types of claims arising from commercial disputes, there are clearly substantial differences between cases
that come before a Supreme Court, as well as the way that the US Supreme Court is appointed but, in view of developments in
generative AI, the potential to assist parties assess the probable outcomes was clear prior to the application of generative AI.
A key difference between methodologies used in statistical models and AI is that the statistical model uses a structured
approach whereas AI leverages unstructured data (e.g. case texts, legal briefs) and learns to generate outcomes without
needing explicit feature engineering. AI analyses vast datasets of historical cases to identify patterns and correlations between
case features and outcomes and provides the potential ability for it to take into account multiple elements, including how a
specific judge has previously behaved, from each case in order to develop a forecast. 11
This does not get away from the fact that the output is only a prediction and, as a result, it remains important that it is
treated cautiously when considering a negotiation strategy for dispute resolution. Behind this predictive analysis, the best
applications will also implement NLP with reference to legal principles, case law and judgments to work out how strong the
case is 12  and to get a view of the best alternative to no agreement (BATNA). 13
Application of AI: early neutral evaluation
10-008
Once an issue has been identified as suitable for early neutral evaluation (ENE), the application of AI can move from a
higher-level probabilistic evaluation of the success of a claim to a more focused assessment of the facts behind a specific
issue in dispute. In this application, the characteristic benefits of AI being able to analyse vast amounts of technical and legal
documents can be used to assist an evaluator, allowing for a human overview to produce the final assessment. Balancing the
obvious efficiency, overreliance on AI for an initial assessment also runs the risk of leading the expert to overlooking crucial
aspects of the case that require human expertise. However, recognising the risks, for narrower issues, AI has the potential to
provide a definitive evaluation of the issue as well as providing a thorough explanation of the reasons behind the evaluation.
The value of using AI in ENE is further enhanced when considering that traditional ENE relies on the neutral evaluator’s
experience but also runs the risk of introducing unconscious bias. The development of AI algorithms to be objective and
data-driven has the benefit of potentially reducing bias in the initial assessment. However, acknowledging that AI models
are only as good as the data they are trained on, errors or biases embedded in the training data can also lead to inaccurate
assessments, potentially harming one of the parties. As a result, for the successful use of AI in ENE, it is crucial to ensure
that the AI tool is transparent and explains the reasons for its decision.
In its current state, AI cannot replace the legal expertise required to fully understand the nuances of complex commercial
disputes. However, having helped an evaluator rapidly produce a reasoned assessment, AI can still help the evaluator highlight
potential risks associated with continued litigation for each side. In so doing, this can further encourage pragmatic settlement
discussions between the parties.
Overall, AI has the potential to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of ENE in commercial disputes. However, addressing
the risks and barriers through robust development, transparent algorithms and human oversight is crucial for effective
implementation.
Application of AI: mediation
10-009
If an AI, machine-learning enabled ENE stage has not resulted in a negotiated settlement, a further AI enabled mediation can
take place. There are already AI tools to assist in mediation. 14 AI learning and analysis is broadly based on evaluating the
probability of things happening and, with the ability of AI to recognise human emotions based on cues in written language,
this capacity is improving rapidly. It is very easy to dismiss AI as lacking empathy or emotion. This is true. However, in
the same way that humans vary widely in their ability to read a situation or detect when someone is upset, AI has a rapidly

developing capacity to analyse emotions and adjust its visible behaviour based on literally millions of observations of humans
interacting. From this, it seems highly likely that AI tools will develop that can respond and adjust the way they communicate
to take into account the personality and emotions of the parties.
At the moment, AI’s role in mediation is to help streamline the process by assessing the statements from both sides including
offers. It then considers these alongside precedents from similar disputes and proposes options for settling that it considers fair.
Apart from reduced cost and increased speed, the benefits include a lack of perceived bias and emotion in making proposals.
Some tools describe themselves as AI mediation tools but are in effect a form of auction tool where blind settlement offers
are made and the AI tool indicates whether a side is within reach of a settlement without telling them what the other side
has offered. These tools can work for very simple disputes involving “single number” issues but cannot cope with resolution
of more complex disputes. 15
Application of AI: dispute resolution
10-010
The following sections include reviews of applications of AI to legal services and dispute resolution that exist at the time
of writing. Given the pace of change, it is recognised that the status and existence of such applications is likely to evolve
quickly. As such, reference to specific apps by name may become redundant but the issues and opportunities that have been
identified will still be relevant.
Efficiency and cost reduction
10-011
AI can handle large volumes of data at speeds unattainable by humans, significantly reducing the time and cost associated
with dispute resolution processes. By automating document analysis and utilising predictive analytics, AI technologies can
streamline pre-trial preparation by summarising and highlighting relevant documentation. The ability to generate specific and
unique advice by predicting possible and probable outcomes, as well as highlighting strengths and weaknesses of a potential
litigant’s position, has the potential to reduce the need for lengthy and expensive litigation or arbitration.
Legal assistance
10-012
The ability to analyse contracts can also be applied at the front-end of a contractual relationship. Arguably, the best form of
dispute resolution is dispute elimination or dispute prevention. With this in mind, there is benefit in considering the application
of AI to reviewing contracts when they are being negotiated rather than when they are broken.
An example of this are AI applications that claim to offer AI tools to review contracts and highlight unacceptable or missing
clauses. In addition, some claim to “understand” the contractual context and the users’ position and to make proposals for
negotiation with the counterparty. The real effectiveness of this will no doubt be tested in the near future and, if proven to be
reliable, will change how lawyers are used to review draft contracts that may be produced during settlement discussions. 16
There are an increasing number of tools of different levels of complexity and cost that are primarily designed to provide an
understanding of legal documents. For any defendant/respondent in a commercial dispute, one of the first requirements when
receiving a claim, or letter before claim, is to understand the basis of the case and the strength of their defence. At a very
basic level, an AI tool can provide rapid, cost-effective insight without the need to pay for legal advice. The impact of this
on the dispute resolution process is likely to be to speed up the response and give the respondent a low-cost opportunity to
determine their strategy for resolving the dispute.
Some AI tools offer to provide legal assistance to parties seeking legal help. The general principle is that users upload all
their legal documents and communicate with the AI tool through a chat interface. This allows users to ask questions and

Once such a tool is set up, in principle, it would have the capacity to review an unlimited number of documents and, if the tool
is trained using specialist textbooks or articles, it is highly likely to provide useful legal advice for resolution of commercial
disputes. The current sense is that the cost of doing this is likely to be thousands of times cheaper than using a reputable
law firm. A challenge is then whether such a tool is used in house by legal advisers (to reduce their own costs and increase
service) or by the lay client (to reduce the role of lawyers early in the dispute).
At the other end of the spectrum, there are already generative AI tools designed specifically for large law firms. 18  These
tools, based on OpenAI’s platform, are specific to the legal services sector and use what they call “legal language models”.
By focusing these tools on the work carried out by legal firms, they are designed to automate and enhance various aspects,
including contract analysis, due diligence, litigation and regulatory compliance. At this stage of the evolution of AI, given the
lack of confidence that the outputs will be correct and free from hallucinations, the output still needs to be carefully reviewed
by a lawyer. Arguably, this could be considered to be similar to the role of a partner or senior member reviewing the work
of a junior before it is goes out. 19
Even removing the hype, the established application of AI supports the view that legal professionals will benefit from
increased efficiency and accuracy in their work. This is likely to have a significant impact on the time and resource necessary
to deal with complex disputes. The unintended consequence of this efficiency is what most lawyers would describe as the giant
elephant in the room, being the question of what this will do for the employment of lawyers and the fees that they can charge.
From the point of view of a top law firm, they are likely to see it as enhancing their service, but they will certainly have one
eye over their shoulder looking at the power such a tool would give to a small commercial law firm who would conventionally
have been excluded from working on large corporate commercial dispute resolution issues.
At the sharp end of the legal services supply chain, the question of the impact of AI in dispute resolution quickly moves to
what the role of barristers in any commercial chambers will look like in five years’ time. If a law firm can get a legal opinion
from an AI tool that has effectively digested every statute in place and every case that has ever been reported on and is able
to provide comprehensive advice and legal opinions, this also indicates that, in the long term, as the quality and reliability of
legal services AI tools improves, the role of a barrister in giving an initial opinion/advice will change. At one end, the law
firm (or in-house legal department of a lay client) can use AI to get that advice. At the other end, a commercial chambers
could invest in or, at least, embrace the technology to speed up this preliminary phase whilst providing the client with the
confidence that such AI output has been checked and signed off by a barrister with the appropriate level of experience.
With an eye on access to justice for non-legal professionals in companies or as individuals, Layman Law is a legal AI tool
that claims to help professionals understand complex legal language quickly and easily. It provides a facility to upload legal
documents and describes its objectives as “making it easier for users to understand the meaning of the documents without the
need for expensive legal fees”. 20  As with all current applications, these claims need to be proven and customer confidence
gained before such tools will be relied on.
An interesting potential impact of this near-free early advice in the context of a dispute is that it will reduce the barrier that can
quickly appear when a non-lawyer receives a legal document. It is common for a lay reader to react by thinking that speaking
to a lawyer will cost money. As a result, they do not contact a lawyer but, instead, they search on the internet for articles with
the end result that they still may not understand the issue. If they get quick cheap advice and discover that the claim they are
facing has some legal merit, they could be less likely to dismiss or ignore it and, as a result, could contact a law firm earlier.
This impact is likely to be greater on individuals or small companies. The difference with a larger or more sophisticated
company is that there is more chance it will have an in-house team who could have enough experience to use such a tool to get
advice. This team may not include lawyers but will have some awareness of the nature of contractual disputes. In this case,
there could be a greater chance that they will turn to an AI tool for that initial advice. As per the other examples above, the
impact on a commercial dispute resolution lawyer is likely to be for the first conference with a client to be more productive
and to offer greater added value as, by definition, the lawyer will be addressing issues that the client cannot obtain answers
from AI or lacks confidence in these answers.
By way of illustration, apps already exist that apply a similar model aimed at law firms and corporate legal departments. 21
These include tools for legal document analysis that utilise AI and LLM to provide insights into contracts and legal documents.

Application of AI: enhanced decision making
10-013
AI’s ability to analyse vast amounts of data can lead to more informed decision-making in dispute resolution. By leveraging
the knowledge that is embedded, but sometimes hidden, in historical data, parties can make decisions that are grounded in a
broader understanding of similar disputes and their outcomes. Considering the potential application inside a corporation or
large law firm (solicitors or barrister’s chambers), the capability of enhanced decision making could arguably reach super-
power levels when compared to reliance on an OpenAI tool that is based on publicly available documents.
The mainstream information providers and legal research platforms such as Thomson Reuters 22  and LexisNexis 23  have
already launched enhanced AI services. Compared to OpenAI-type tools that are based on publicly available information,
whilst certainly being more expensive, the power that these tools will have is that they can draw on the strengths of the
enormous legal literature database already available to these platforms, including books and journals that are typically behind
paywalls. In addition, as access to one or more of these platforms is already considered to be essential for any dispute
resolution lawyer to be able to provide advice and prepare cases, the market development is expected to be rapid.
The pace of development and innovation will also be increased from challenges and opportunities that are now driving new
AI-enabled legal information/research providers such as Ross Intelligence 24  and LegalZoom. 25  These are currently niche
tools focused on legal research and drafting.
It is important to realise that failures in drafting, including ambiguity and omissions, can lead to disputes regarding
interpretation and scope of an agreement. In this context, generative AI can also assist in drafting to avoid historical problems.
This would include drafting to include dispute resolution clauses that are tailored to suit the nature of the parties’ contractual
relationship as well as the type of transaction that is covered by the contract.
Superpowered decision making
10-014
In the UK, the legal market is based on common law with a clear distinction between the roles of solicitors and barristers/
advocates. For larger commercial disputes, there is a tendency for the work to go to the chambers who are perceived to be in
the premier league (sometimes referred to as “magic circle” firms). In considering how AI can develop, recognising that there
may be confidentiality issues to overcome, it is not inconceivable that a large, successful commercial chambers could deploy
an in-house AI tool that would surpass that of anything available even to Thomson Reuters or LexisNexis. The reason for
this is that the conventional “public” database of legal precedents is derived from published judgments, books and journals
that, after payment of a subscription, are available to anyone.
What is missing is the information from the 80% of commercial cases that are settled out of court. In addition, although
most commercial cases are open to the public, the established platforms do not have access to the extensive bundles, running
to thousands of pages; the full claim and defence documents; the skeleton arguments submitted by barristers; the detailed
submissions made in court; the appeal submissions; and any settlement offers/mediation records along the way.
Given how the capacity of generative AI to develop accurate and effective responses is dependent on the material that it
has “read”, there would be a substantial leap in knowledge, and therefore predictive capacity, if AI tools learned from all

It would be easy to dismiss this concept based on an instinctive reaction to potential breaches of the embedded duty of

in principle, will contain the medical records of everyone in the UK health system. 26  This is planned to be made available
for research and development of treatment and, in this plan, it is anticipated that all the data will be anonymised to protect
the identification of any individual.
Arguably, there is no reason why such an approach could not be taken with data related to commercial disputes. This would
require stripping out personal and company data and may require changes to regulations (in particular to UK GDPR 27 ) but,
behind this, the vast majority of civil proceedings are open to the public who can hear evidence and listen to submissions.
The real issue would relate to documents covered by legal or litigation privilege and out of court offers to settle.
There is no doubt that this area raises many tricky issues but, given that the learning contained within such a tool will be
in-house, if a chambers (or firm) was big enough, the value of overcoming the issues would be significant and is likely to
justify the effort/cost required to solve.
Enhanced accessibility
10-015
AI-driven online dispute resolution (ODR) tools can make dispute resolution more accessible to individuals and businesses,
especially those with limited resources. By way of illustration (rather than recommendation) one consumer-facing AI tool,
CyberSettle, already promotes itself as “using artificial intelligence to help you fight big corporations, protect your privacy,
find hidden money, and beat bureaucracy”. 28  This tool appears to provide a “freemium” service whereby users can access
free basic features like generating demand letters or a monthly-fee subscription. At this time, the benefits of this are largely
unproven. The illustration is included to highlight the potential for AI to provide tools that can be accessed from anywhere
at any time. This can make it easier for consumers or businesses to resolve disputes without the need for expensive legal
representation.
It is also possible for AI to be used as part of a hybrid-service combining AI with human expertise. One business model
describes its consumer-focused mission as to “level the playing field between everyday people and big companies”. 29  Such
services can be provided based on an upfront flat-fee that varies depending on the complexity of a case and can cover initial
consultation, negotiation and document preparation in anticipation of consumer-focused arbitration proceedings. In some
cases, a contingency-based fee structure can also be offered, taking a percentage of the recovered amount.
It is not surprising for an application that was focused on consumer action against larger finance, telecoms and transport
companies, to meet resistance and the target defendants have tried to change their contracts to prevent this sort of action.
It is anticipated that, as the cost of legal action falls, there could be a similar attempt by larger companies to regain their
traditional position of strength against smaller companies that is derived from having deeper pockets. This would most likely
show up in small claims cases where the cost of litigation can easily exceed the amount being claimed.
Governments around the world are already embracing AI to facilitate access to justice and to encourage out of court resolution,
including mediation. 30  This requires consideration of the ethical impact to ensure that increasing access and efficiency does
not come at the expense of justice itself. 31  The belief that it will be possible to address these issues is reflected in a cross-
cultural review of 500 professors in law departments suggests that legal scholars believe that, on average, almost 30% of
judicial decision-making will be carried out by AI in only 25 years’ time. 32

Application of AI: online dispute resolution platforms
Negotiation-focused platforms: Smartsettle
10-016
There are a number of platforms that can be used to make dispute resolution more effective. This review is not a
recommendation but should only be seen as an illustration of what is available and what sort of claims are being made by
such platforms.
Smartsettle describes itself as a “collaborative negotiation tool” with the objective of “resolving conflict in a more peaceful,
collaborative & intelligent way throughout the world”. 33  It provides a family of AI enabled products including Smartsettle
Infinity for complex negotiations and Smartsettle One for resolving single-value monetary disputes.
Behind Smartsettle is an AI-powered platform that specialises in negotiation and mediation. It is described as using algorithms
to analyse the preferences and priorities of the parties involved in a dispute and aims to suggest solutions that are acceptable to
all. The platform’s goals are described as facilitating a more efficient negotiation process by focusing on optimising outcomes
based on the parties’ input, potentially leading to win-win solutions.
The primary application of AI here is about eliciting the preferences and objectives of the parties and generating potential
agreements based on expressed preferences. This contrasts with a more evaluative/generative AI where the emphasis is on
evaluating potential outcomes and tactics if the parties were to seek to resolve their disputes by litigation or arbitration.
The process
10-017
The process that is likely to be used by such platforms is not likely to differ from that as explained by Smartsettle. This would
follow a logical flow in, first, setting up the case. Here, the parties involved in a dispute would create an account and provide
details about their case. This would include their interests and desired outcomes. Each party would then submit their offers
and counteroffers confidentially through the platform.
The set-up stage does not really use AI but, having done that, the next stage is for AI enabled analysis. Here, AI analyses
the data submitted by both parties, including their offers, counteroffers and any supporting documents. AI can then identify
potential areas of agreement and disagreement, as well as potential opportunities for compromise. Having done that, AI can
facilitate the generation of suggestions for negotiation strategies and compromises that are tailored to the specific case. A
suitably configured platform can also then provide prompts and questions to help parties communicate more effectively and
explore different options.
If the parties do not fully engage, this would thwart any benefit offered by AI in the same way as it would with a human
mediator. However, assuming the parties are willing to change their positions, they could continue to negotiate through the
platform, using AI’s suggestions and prompts to guide them. If an agreement is reached, the platform can then generate a
legally binding settlement document that both parties can sign electronically.
Bidding
10-018
As an added tool, AI can also provide for multivariate visual blind bidding and other negotiation processes. This sounds
complex but at its heart, this is a process where parties submit settlement proposals (bids) without knowing each other’s
offers. This can reduce the potential for emotional bias and encourage a more strategic approach with a focus on reaching a

resolution. The “multi-variate” functionality allows the parties to consider factors other than price which, in turn, can facilitate
settlements that are in the long-term interests and mutual benefit of the parties. The use of visual interfaces to represent bids
and outcomes can help understanding and analysis. These platforms have the potential to allow the parties to participate in a
dispute resolution process without the need for representation or much advice from a lawyer.
In this context, the role for generative AI is limited as it is not strictly about generating new content but is closer to conventional

Process-focused platforms
10-019
In contrast to the consumer-focused models, an example of an early mover in business focused services providing access to
ODR platforms, sold on a software as a service (SaaS) basis is Modria. 34  This was set up in 2015 before the accelerating
wave of GPT models was developed and describes its service as a “fairness engine” and its goal to be “the operating system
for online dispute resolution. So, any kind of dispute, no matter how complicated or how simple, how high volume or low
volume, we can use these building blocks … to build an appropriate resolution path for that dispute”. 35
The value proposition here is based on efficiency, cost-effectiveness, scalability and, ultimately, improved customer
satisfaction by providing a faster and more transparent dispute resolution process.
The building blocks illustrate the main stages that any equivalent platform or tool is likely to use, i.e. problem diagnosis,
technology facilitated negotiation, mediation and evaluation. What is highlighted here is that the low-cost enabling nature of
AI is likely to lead to disputes being more thoroughly evaluated earlier in the dispute resolution process than would otherwise
happen in conventional arbitration or litigation proceedings. As costs quickly escalate the closer a dispute moves towards a
final binding stage; this subtle difference illustrates the potential for AI to make alternative dispute resolution more efficient
and effective.
A further illustration of the relative power of such a platform approach can be seen with reference to the aspiration of one
of Modria’s founders:
“… we want to build an online multidoor courthouse, and we want there to be not just several doors, not five
or ten or a dozen, we want there to be thousands of doors. I would like to build a custom door for every single
dispute. When someone comes in and tells me ‘This is the kind of problem that we have’, we can dynamically
build a resolution process.” 36
The white-label 37  platform-based approach of Modria has led to it being adopted for use in state courts, city/council
enforcement departments, mortgage providers, utility companies and automotive producers.
Stepping back and considering the true implementation of generative AI, Modria appears to be an example of a service that
might declare itself (or appear to be) AI but is more about automation, streamlining and assisting human experts than being
a fully AI-driven resolution. It is anticipated that, with the rapid evolution of AI in the application to commercial dispute
resolution, Modria should find it relatively easy to add deeper AI functionality across all the building blocks resulting in a
particularly valuable enhancement to the problem diagnosis and final evaluation stage.
Blockchain-based platforms
10-020
At the outer edge of new approaches to resolve disputes lays Kleros, 38  a blockchain-based tool for dispute resolution. This
offers a unique approach compared to platforms like Cybersettle and Modria which is decentralised and gamified. Kleros

Chapter 10 - Application of AI in dispute resolution, UKBC-EFFDIS 549291550 (2025)
© 2025 Thomson Reuters.
11
describes itself as an “an open-source online dispute resolution protocol which uses blockchain and crowdsourcing to fairly
adjudicate disputes”. 39
The objectives of Kleros are set out as:
“Kleros is a decentralised decision protocol for use on smart contract platforms, which has been implemented
on Ethereum. It acts as a decentralised third party capable of providing decisions on the correct result when
applying a set of rules to questions ranging from simple to highly complex. This is achieved by using game
theoretic incentives to have crowdsourced jurors analyse and rule on cases correctly. Hence, Kleros provides
judgments in an inexpensive, reliable, typically fast, and decentralised way. Of particular relevance is the use
of this protocol to dispute resolution, creating for a form of decentralised justice.” 40
Given its technology base, Kleros operates primarily in resolving disputes with a cryptocurrency element and it leverages
blockchain technology to operate as a decentralised court system, meaning no single entity controls the process. The disputes
are resolved by jurors, who are selected and incentivised by staking tokens to vote on cases fairly and consistently. In theory,
jurors select cases based on their expertise and the potential reward they can earn.
This is a very unusual almost populist approach to resolving disputes. It is not really enabled by generative AI but does raise
the question whether there is any scope for resolving more mainstream commercial disputes this way.
One of the issues with blockchain platforms such as Kleros is that, to gain the benefit of the United Nations Convention on the
Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards (1958) (New York Convention) for enforcement, an arbitral award
needs to be made in writing and signed by the arbitrator(s). The Kleros “jury” approach does not produce any identifiable
signatories or provide any explanation and, as it is decentralised, there is no identifiable “place” of arbitration. As such, if
viewed in isolation, using Kleros would not meet the requirements for enforcement under the New York Convention.
However, when considered as a tool for arriving at a decision, it is possible for blockchain platforms to be used in a hybrid
process alongside arbitration proceedings. In a groundbreaking development, on 28 May 2021, a Mexican court enforced a
traditional arbitral award that incorporated a decision reached using a blockchain arbitration protocol (Kleros system). This
enforcement was obtained by the parties expressly instructing and providing detailed procedures for their arbitrator to use
the Kleros system to govern the arbitral decision. A critical part of the instruction to the arbitrator was:
“The arbitrator will receive the decision from ‘Kleros’ and incorporate it into his arbitral award to govern the
substance of the ruling, issuing it in writing and indicating the date, place, name, and signature of the arbitrator.
The award will constitute the definitive resolution of the controversy, without further instance, parties expressly
renouncing to any recourse to revoke or modify the decision, as well as renouncing to the deadlines that the
law establishes for filing such recourse. The award will be electronically sent to the parties, and they will be
entitled to receive a physical copy of it.” 41
Kleros ran the dispute through its protocol and reached a decision where the jurors had all concluded that the defendant failed
to fulfil the obligation to pay the rent. On 27 November 2020, the arbitrator rendered the arbitral award and subsequently
filed a motion in the Mexican courts pursuing the recognition and enforcement of the award. On 28 May 2021, the court
recognised the arbitral proceeding, giving notice to the defendant to comply with the award within a five business days
deadline, after which the award would be executed through public force. This marked the first time a court has acknowledged
a “Blockchain Arbitral Award”.
It is anticipated that, given the need for certainty when considering contractual disputes, it is not likely to be attractive to
many parties to put the fate of their dispute in the hands of a jury who have no real knowledge of the dispute and, although are
incentivised by an algorithm to provide fairness and consistency, by its own definition, may consider the process to be a game.
Having said that, a lot of small-company litigants who end up in proceedings without being able to afford representation
come out of the process disillusioned and of the view that the process was stacked against them, and they were effectively
playing a game of chance. If you are a litigant with that frame of mind, Kleros might start to look a bit more attractive.

Barriers to development and adoption
Trust and reliability
10-021
Despite the obvious benefits, there are a number of barriers that may affect the development and broader adoption of AI in
commercial dispute resolution.
The lack of trust and reliability is a significant potential barrier with both legal users and lay clients, in the absence of
established confidence, doubting the reliability and fairness of AI-driven resolutions. Behind this fear is a perception of the
risk of bias in algorithms and/or oversimplification of complex legal issues. It does not resolve the problem but, if clients were
to step back, they could also consider the potential for unconscious and conscious bias and prejudice that already exists within
the judicial system. Given that any generative response is drawn from having absorbed historical judgments, the bigger issue
is how AI can avoid repeating prejudice and bias that may be contained within these judgments. This challenge is considered
further in this chapter in para.10-031.
Transparency
10-022
A further barrier arises from the appearance of AI as a form of black box (a bit like the oracle of Delphi) where the decision-
making process or basis for the advice given is not transparent. This can make it difficult for users to understand how
conclusions are reached. Given the capacity of AI to respond to any questions asked, it is perfectly possible (and logical)
that, where an explanation is needed, AI can be asked to provide an explanation of how it reached its decision. This is little
different to reading a judgment issued by a very senior and experienced judge and asking them to provide further explanation
of their reasons. However, the current issue with AI is that, whilst an explanation may sound plausible, there is no certainty
that the explanation itself is correct.
Regulatory and ethical concerns
10-023
Regulatory and ethical concerns also exist about privacy, data protection and the ethical use of AI. This is particularly with
regard to automated decision-making that might affect legal rights and obligations. If the AI is trained on publicly available
documents (e.g. books, journals and judgments) then, in principle, there should be no problem. The bigger issue is if AI is
used to assess a bundle of confidential commercial documents that is uploaded for the purpose of a hearing but never intended
or expected to be made public. This will need more careful consideration and regulatory bodies are already considering how
to address this. Formative illustrations of the approach needed can be seen in the guidance given by the Solicitors Regulation
Authority, 42  Bar Council 43  and The Law Society. 44
By way of illustration, the areas that lend themselves to regulatory control include copyright, data protection, confidentiality,
cybersecurity, equality, bias and discrimination. Aspects such as the integrity of the output and the consequence of negligent
use are important but are likely to be caught in the existing framework of regulations and codes that govern the activities of
solicitors and barristers. Having said that, in the transition stages where confidence needs to be gained, it is also possible to
see how a regulation might be put in place requiring the court (or a client) to be informed if AI has been used in formulating
the advice or submissions made to court.

Technical limitations
10-024
Considering historical step-changes in technology such as the steam engine, electricity, the internet and mobile phones, and
the evolution of subsequent enhancements, AI is a technology that has only really been accessible to the public since around
2018. Applying the principle that the best predictor of future behaviour is previous behaviour, and considering Moore’s
Law, 45  there is a strong probability that the current issues facing the use of AI in dispute resolution that are perceived to be
based on technical limitations will disappear within five years.
Any restraints arising from current limitations should be considered in the context of the speed of development of generative
AI. It would be very easy to frame a view of the potential for the use of AI in dispute resolution based on its current capabilities.
This would be a mistake as the potential capability would certainly be underestimated.
To put this statement in context, recognising that this book is about legal dispute resolution, a short review of the history
of the development of generative AI may be helpful in understanding how AI is likely to develop and the likely speed of
this development.
The first applications of generative models based on NLP and ML can be traced back to the 1980s and 1990s when speech
recognition tools were developed and started to become widely used. The next step was the introduction of neural network-
based language models as deployed in a neural probabilistic language model in 2003. 46  This used a feedforward neural
network to learn word representations and generate text.
The next leap forward was the development in around 2014 of what is called Sequence to Sequence Models 47  (Seq2Seq)
which use recurrent neural networks that learned to map an input sequence of tokens (such as words, characters or symbols)
to an output sequence of tokens, using an encoder-decoder architecture. This was then used in various NLP tasks such as
machine translation and speech recognition.
Around about the same time as the development of the Seq2Seq models, Generative Adversarial Networks (GANS) were
developed. 48  This has led to the ongoing development to text to image synthesis and supports the robustness of text
generation. The use of GANS involves a generator that tries to create fake data, and a discriminator that tries to distinguish
between real and fake data. The generator and the discriminator compete with each other in a form of game, where the
generator tries to fool the discriminator, and the discriminator tries to catch the generator.
The watershed moment for generative AI in NLP came with the introduction of Transformers and Large Language Models
(LLMs) in 2017. 49  These were the basis for the development of models like GPT-1 (Generative Pre-Trained Transformer),
first released by OpenAI in 2018.
In 2018, GPT-1 was introduced, having learned from reading books, capable of reading comprehension, commonsense
reasoning and sentiment analysis, and had 117 million parameters. A “parameter” refers to the elements of the model that
are learned from the training data. Parameters are essentially the internal variables of the model that are adjusted through the
training process to minimise the difference between the predicted output and the actual output in the training data. The more
parameters a model has, the more complex relationships it can capture.
Less than a year later, in February 2019, OpenAI introduced GPT-2 with 1.5 billion parameters, having been trained with
information from the internet. The following year, GPT-3 was introduced, having 175 billion parameters with an enhanced
user interface and capable of being fine-tuned. GPT-4 was introduced in March 2023 with an unconfirmed estimate of between
240 billion and 1 trillion parameters. 50  GPT4o was introduced in May 2024 and is reported to have a capacity of 1.8 trillion
parameters.
Apart from the number of parameters (an indication of speed of processing and the capacity to handle complex information),
the biggest difference was that GPT-4 underwent a process known as reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF)
to improve the model by learning from both automated data and real human interactions. The result was that GPT-4 provided
more precise and relevant answers. GPT-4 could also see and describe images in great detail. The relevance of this to its
use in dispute resolution is that the ability to analyse images is likely to be critical in detecting emotion in the context of

Chapter 10 - Application of AI in dispute resolution, UKBC-EFFDIS 549291550 (2025)
© 2025 Thomson Reuters.
14
mediation and court settings. This feature was not present in GPT-3. In addition, GPT-4 had the capacity to respond to voice
control on its mobile app.
GPT-4o further enhanced its language skills and, through improved multi-modal (text, vision, and audio) capacity is able to
work with languages that don't use a Western alphabet, such as Hindi, Chinese and Korean. The significance of this capacity
in dispute resolution can be seen in the potential for use of AI as a resource when parties involved in a dispute do not have
the same level of understanding of English.
As a result of its increased speed and capacity, and its hybrid training model, GPT-4o has an improved capacity for avoiding
inappropriate, disallowed and sensitive requests. This is a further step towards more effectively being able to empathise and
handle emotions which, in turn, is highly relevant to dispute resolution.
The reason for focusing on OpenAI’s model is because it is currently one of the most well-known models. When considering
the other growing models such as Google Gemini, Claude, and Llama, the different approach they are taking shines a light
on the existence of technical competition which will only serve to reduce or eliminate the current technical barriers. Whilst
based on similar architecture, Google’s model has been designed with a greater emphasis on understanding the context in
search queries. Similarly, whilst OpenAI have focused on the creative generative capacity, Google has focused on better
understanding the query itself.
The reason for providing the preceding, admittedly heavy, tangential technical description of the evolution of AI technology
was to give some context to the current perception that technical barriers will limit the use of AI in dispute resolution and to
support the view that, on balance, it is likely that, in future, the application of AI will not be limited by technical capabilities.
Cost of implementation
10-025
Developing and implementing sophisticated AI solutions can be costly, but once developed, the potential for improvements
in efficiency and reduction of cost is obvious. The challenge is that if cost is a barrier to the successful deployment of AI,
this will end up with AI enhancing the position of the firms that are already successful.
Having said that, looking at the current cost of accessing advice from a top firm or chambers, on balance, it is much more
likely that smaller firms (typically representing smaller clients) will be able to boost their capability by effectively drawing
on all the data that would be available if the experience of every partner or senior counsel were available in one box.
Overcoming barriers and building confidence
10-026
Of relevance to the application of AI in legal services, the continued work on developing AI includes the efficiency of modelling,
the issue of hallucination and bias, human values and empathy, and trust and confidence.
In particular, the work on efficiency includes improving the language modelling to reduce the amount of data needed for a
reliable model; improvements in understanding of cause and effect to deliver more coherent answers based on reasoning rather
than patterns.
There have been some high-profile examples of errors resulting from the AI tool hallucinating and/or appearing biased. In order
for AI to be successfully used in dispute resolution, this particular issue needs resolving in a way that gives users near-100%
confidence in the results. This can include fact checking which will address the issues of factual accuracy and bias by ensuring
that models can verify information and anchor their responses in up-to-date real world knowledge. Similarly, work on bias
mitigation is being carried out by training data and model design to prevent discriminatory or harmful language generation.
For application in legal services in particular, it is essential that trust and confidence—from both professionals and lay clients—is
improved. The development work that is going on includes developing “explainable AI” with a focus on methods to understand
how models arrive at their outputs and to communicate this to improve trust and confidence in decisions or advice.

There is already a difference in how some of the main AI tools (Open AI – GPT4, Google Gemini and Microsoft CoPilot) work.
This is likely to evolve but, for example, CoPilot uses GPT coupled with Microsoft Azure technology to bring AI to Office365
tools. As a result, CoPilot already has the ability for a legal services user to ask for authorities and it then uses Bing search to

In contrast to other tools, at the time of writing, ChatGPT 4o differentiates itself in allowing a user to upload up to 20 documents

to breach confidentiality rules as well as highlighting specific risks including anthropomorphism, hallucinations, information
disorder, bias in data training, and mistakes and confidential data training.
Another critical area of development that is relevant to using AI in legal services, is that of human values and empathy. The
work being done here is to try and align the AI tools with human values to ensure they incorporate an ethical element with
an enhanced understanding of empathy and emotion. It is clear that an enhancing model with the ability to detect underlying
human emotions and reactions to information would allow the model to adjust its tone and response to suit individual needs
and, in so doing, assist in dispute resolution.
Considering the application of AI in commercial dispute resolution, it can be seen that improvements and development in most
of these areas would significantly enhance the potential for AI to help the effectiveness of dispute resolution whether as part
of a non-binding or binding process.
Specifically focusing on the issue of the confidence of potential users and overcoming barriers to development, there are a
number of areas of development that could have a big impact on the use of AI in dispute resolution. The first is the question
of enhancing transparency and explainability. The Microsoft Copilot interface provides for a distinctive response with clear
explanations for its analysis. This helps build trust.
However, it is still possible to ask a model for an explanation of AI hallucination and to get a comprehensive description of the
issue that a model, based on LLM and ML, predicts the next word or phrase without any intrinsic ability to detect truthfulness.
As such, having been trained on data using real cases such as Thaler v Comptroller-General of Patents, Designs and Trade
Marks, 52  it can accurately determine what follows the name Thaler, but all its LLM knows, based on probability, is that this is
followed by [date] and then one of a number of options (for example, UKSC, EWCA, EWHC). Based largely on probability,
it could determine that the most likely phrase to follow would then be [EWHC]. Having done that, it is likely that this will be
followed by a number of digits and then brackets (QB) or (Ch) as these are the most common divisions.
The disconcerting result is that the AI generated case may look absolutely correct in its structure but not exist. In carrying out
research for this book, a tool was asked about hallucinations and then asked for citations to cases involving AI hallucination.
The explanation for hallucinations was accurate, the example case that the AI tool produced was a hallucination.
The issue of bias and ensuring fairness is more difficult to deal with than hallucinations in that a hallucination is, by definition,
wrong and verifiable. In contrast, bias and fairness can be much more subtle and a user’s own unconscious bias might cause
them not to see or appreciate bias for what it is. This is not such a problem for using AI in assisting dispute resolution but
could strike at the heart of any attempts to use AI in the role of a judge or arbitrator. The work done on resolving this issue
involves implementing rigorous testing and validation processes to identify and mitigate biases in AI algorithms. The use of
diverse datasets and ongoing monitoring can help mitigate the problem, but it remains a challenge to ensure that AI systems
make fair and unbiased decisions when their training model could be based on hundreds of years of history where societal and
institutional bias was systemic.
There is no doubt that the application of AI in legal services to assist in dispute resolution will continue to accelerate. However,
there are a number of hurdles that need to be overcome if the full potential for AI is to be delivered. To be acceptable it is essential
for AI to be successful and, as such, the successful development of solutions will have to include not only the AI technical
experts but engagement with all participants in the legal services supply chain including industry regulators. If successful,
the final outcome will be tools that allow the users to understand the reasons for their advice or decisions, are perceived to
be reliable, accurate, ethical, and fair and, ultimately, take into account that the users are fallible emotional humans who can
respond differently to the same circumstances.

Accessibility and scalability
10-027
The advent of AI in dispute resolution platforms presents a transformative shift towards greater accessibility and scalability
in legal processes. This shift has the potential to democratise access to justice by managing a larger volume of disputes more
efficiently and with a lower skill base required to access.
However, a re-evaluation will be needed of the traditional roles played by legal professionals, such as barristers and solicitors,
and raises questions about the potential impact on the power dynamics between larger and smaller companies engaged in
disputes.
Accessibility to justice
10-028
As already set out, AI-driven ODR platforms and other AI legal assistance tools can significantly lower the barriers to accessing
legal services. By automating parts of the dispute resolution process, such as document analysis, evidence gathering, and even
some aspects of legal reasoning, AI can reduce the need for extensive legal representation in certain cases. This not only makes
legal processes more affordable but also more accessible to individuals and small businesses who may lack the resources to
engage in traditional, often costly, legal battles.
It is clear that this will be appealing to smaller firms but not to the larger firms or companies who have deeper pockets. Human
nature is that efforts will be made by them to try and fight this. The big question is how but, in any event, this is likely to lead
to a transition period of turbulence in the availability of tools.
One other effect is that large companies are likely to consider using AI technology to bring everything in-house. In the extreme
case, they will no longer need to rely on the resources of a multi-skilled large law firm but, instead, could rely on their own in-
house legal team, perhaps headed by a general counsel with access to an in-house barrister or consultant who, with the help of
AI, could carry out everything that external service providers used to provide. This is likely to lead to changes in the business
models of established large commercial/corporate focused solicitors and chambers.
Within this framework, the roles of barristers and solicitors are likely to evolve with the increasing integration of AI in dispute
resolution. Whilst there is the obvious threat that AI can than replace these professionals, if embraced, AI can also significantly
augment their capabilities, allowing them to focus on higher-value tasks that require human insight, empathy and strategic
judgment. The role of barristers is likely to move away from providing advice, drafting pleadings and opinions, and towards
engaging more in cases that require complex advocacy and legal reasoning.
If they are able to deploy their own in-house tools, solicitors could also see their roles shift towards advisory services, leveraging
AI tools for document management, case prediction and procedural guidance, and away from pure drafting or transactional
facilitation. This shift could enhance the value provided by legal professionals by enabling them to offer more strategic, informed
advice to their clients. If they do not do this, there is a good chance that the work done by solicitors will reduce and, instead,
be carried out by a client’s in-house team with the assistance of AI.
Scalability of dispute resolution
10-029
AI tools based on a suitable platform can handle a significantly larger volume of disputes simultaneously, thanks to their ability
to process and analyse data at a scale unattainable by human legal practitioners alone. This scalability is particularly beneficial

Chapter 10 - Application of AI in dispute resolution, UKBC-EFFDIS 549291550 (2025)
© 2025 Thomson Reuters.
17
for handling cases like small claims, consumer disputes, and other high-volume, low-value disputes that often do not get pursued
due to the disproportionate costs of traditional legal processes.
By offering a more efficient and cost-effective avenue for these disputes, AI platforms can help ensure that more individuals
and small businesses can seek redress and enforce their rights. This scalability without much incremental cost means that the
dispute resolution tools that are publicly available can be relatively easily acquired by companies and individuals alike.
The general impact of AI to replace repetitive tasks and enhanced analysis will put further pressure on the services currently
carried out by trainees, paralegals, associates and junior barristers. Equipped by AI, an in-house legal team will certainly be
able to identify an affordable tool that allows them to bring this in-house. The delay in doing this will most likely be dependent
on the issues of confidence and reliability of the outputs from AI.
Given the challenges with AI’s emulation of empathy, disputes that are still likely to go to court and not be resolved through AI-
enhanced ADR are those with an emotional component (see Chapter Eleven). Equally, disputes with a significant component of
empathy and emotions might be better suited to traditional forms of ADR. This is especially true for mediation where the human
mediator can work through the emotional complexity that may be contained in the dispute. For other disputes, especially those
which have no real disputes of fact, these would lend themselves to enhanced AI assessment whatever the size of the dispute.
Power dynamics between larger and smaller companies
10-030
There is the potential for unintended consequences in considering the power dynamics that are not based on economic power/
size, but power conveyed by virtue of the outputs of an AI tool. This could arguably be a bit like the influence of the oracles at
Delphi 53  where the oracles were given by a high priestess called the Pythia, who entered a trance-like state and uttered cryptic
verses that were interpreted by priests. The priests then delivered these divine messages from the god Apollo to the waiting
kings, generals, lawmakers and ordinary people for guidance on various matters, such as war, politics, colonisation, religion and
personal affairs. The impact of the oracles was such that, when acted on, they could become self-fulfilling prophecies in that,
if death and destruction were forecast, then by the actions of those who received the forecasts, death and destruction ensued.
Let’s consider a scenario where, in the absence of AI, both parties were getting human advice on the pros and cons of their
case. It is not unknown for a legal service provider to amplify their clients’ views and be hesitant to tell them if their case
is completely hopeless. Rightly or wrongly, the “billable hours” measure of success in a lot of legal firms can drive aspiring
lawyers to carry out work that could turn out to be unnecessary. Nevertheless, when parties go into a negotiation, they are likely
to have a view of their strengths and weaknesses that is affected by the phenomenon of confirmation bias. 54
How could this change as a result of the impact of each side carrying out an AI evaluation of their prospects for success? If
either party perceives that AI is telling them that they are highly likely to win in litigation or arbitration, this could result in
their willingness to enter into ADR discussions decreasing and effectively encouraged by AI, they could be more likely to adopt
a “take it or leave it” approach to negotiation. Similarly, if AI informs a party that their case is weak, they may effectively
perceive this advice as being a statement of certainty that they will definitely lose. In this case, they are likely to be reluctant
to fight and may concede.
These polarised outcomes might be considered to be naturally fair but, given the potential for AI to inadvertently polarise when
viewed through a confirmation bias lens, the actual effect could be to go against the spirit of a mediated/negotiated approach
where the ideal objective is to search for a settlement that supports long-term mutual cooperation.
Having said that, it is relatively rare for disputes to have no chance of success so, in general, the value of AI remains in giving
both parties an independent balanced view of the merits/weaknesses of their case and encouraging them to consider settlement
options. In future, it is not inconceivable that AI could be trained to identify the benefits of continued long-term cooperation
and the true cost of termination of an established relationship. Clearly not all disputes are in regard to contractual relationships
that are of mutual benefit, and, in that case, an emotionally aware AI could detect whether there is any real merit in facilitating
a settlement that maintains a relationship when, effectively, the marriage is already dead.

Overall, it needs to be recognised that the impact of AI on the power dynamics between larger and smaller companies in disputes
is multifaceted. On the one hand, AI levels the playing field by making legal processes more accessible to smaller entities,
thereby reducing the advantage larger companies might have in terms of resources for legal battles. Smaller companies and
individuals can benefit from AI’s ability to reduce costs and speed up resolution, potentially making it more feasible to pursue
claims and defend against larger entities.
On the other hand, the development and deployment of sophisticated AI tools require significant investment, which larger
companies are better positioned to make. This could lead to a scenario where larger companies leverage more advanced AI
capabilities to manage disputes more effectively, potentially reinforcing existing power imbalances.
However, the proliferation of third-party AI dispute resolution platforms and services may mitigate this risk by providing both
small and large entities with access to advanced AI tools without the need for significant upfront investment. Furthermore,
regulatory oversight and the development of open-source AI dispute resolution tools could help ensure equitable access to these
technologies.
Objectivity and consistency
10-031
Given that an AI model starts off with intrinsic neutrality and statistical objectivity, this should enhance the consistency in advice
given in relation to dispute resolution. When using AI to assess the legal strengths and weaknesses of a claim, it is critical that
the user perceives the advice to be reliable and consistent. Questions can still be asked along the lines of “tell me the strongest/
weakest points in my claim” and ask how to present a case in the best way but, in principle, a user should prefer an objective
answer to an arguably biased question. For AI to become established in dispute resolution, demonstrating this objectivity and
consistency is essential.
By analysing data and applying predefined algorithms, AI can potentially reduce human biases that affect decisions. However,

eliminated.

In the immediate term, integrating human oversight into an AI-driven dispute resolution processes ensures that AI decisions
can be reviewed and, if necessary, overridden by human experts. This hybrid approach allows for the benefits of AI’s speed
and efficiency while retaining human judgment to address nuances and equity issues that the AI might not fully capture. In the
context of dispute resolution, this requirement for human oversight is likely to remain for a long period of time. In this sense,
as well as enhancing the potential for ADR solutions, the major impact of AI will be to reduce the cost and time taken to go

The question of whether AI could ever fully replace a judge in court touches on fundamental characteristics of the legal system
and human judgment. While AI can assist with certain aspects of the legal process, such as evidence review, case law research
and procedural tasks, apart from cases where the entire judgment is based on agreed facts and is largely formulaic, AI is unlikely
to fully replace human judges.
Legal reasoning involves not just the application of law but also the interpretation of statutes and precedents, consideration of
fairness and moral judgments. AI currently lacks the capacity for ethical reasoning, empathy, and the nuanced understanding
of justice that human judges bring to their decisions. As moral and ethical judgments are deeply rooted in societal values and
human experiences and can be very subjective, the challenges facing AI judges are substantial.
There is a lot of research currently being carried out into if/how to incorporate ethics into a generative AI model but until this is
done and, more importantly, proven to the satisfaction of litigants and legislators, it is unlikely that AI will be the final arbiter
in any but simple cases.
A further challenge is that law evolves in response to changes in society, technology and values. The common law system in
particular lends itself to the hierarchy of statutes and courts/precedents. The problem with AI based on LLMs is that, almost
by definition, its response is determined by a probabilistic assessment of what has been decided before. This indicates that AI
models have an inbuilt inertia based on historical precedent and, for it to adjust, in some cases, there would need to be some
way of signalling a requirement to forget historical precedent and replace this with changes in statute or regulations, as well
as giving priority to judgments from higher courts.
In contrast, human judges should be able to interpret the law in light of new circumstances and emerging social norms. It is
unlikely that AI will be able to easily match this. One way of considering this is akin to the deployment of video assistant
referees (VAR) in English football/soccer. Ultimately, a decision is still made by a human referee, but they can draw on the
VAR if appropriate.
Ultimately, a legal system’s legitimacy is partly derived from its administration by humans, who are seen as capable of empathy,
fairness and moral reasoning. Replacing judges with AI could undermine public trust in the justice system. However, as referred
previously, as there is already a body of thought that considers the established judiciary to be conservative and inherently biased,
for some, the use of AI could be considered to be a preferable alternative.
Sir Geoffrey Vos, Master of the Rolls (at the time of writing), summarised the challenges facing the use of AI judges observing
that in many areas of professional expertise, AI is already more reliable than human advisors. In transition, if AI lays claims to
being able to predict outcomes, it is inevitable that clients will want to know what it thinks, even if, ultimately, they will want
to know what a real-life lawyer thinks of the predictions. A critical limitation is that “legal decision-making involves a range
of factors beyond just predicting the outcome of a case, including strategic and ethical considerations and client goals”. 56

Preventing AI “hallucinations”
10-034
The phenomenon of “hallucination” in the context of AI language models refers to instances where the model generates
information or data that is inaccurate, misleading or entirely fabricated. This can happen for several reasons, including but not
limited to the model’s training data, and the way it generates responses based on patterns rather than factual understanding. One
of the main problems is that the output of an AI model can give the sense that the model has a real understanding of the world.
It needs to be remembered that, as sophisticated as they appear to be, AI language models do not “understand” anything and,
at their most basic, they are currently only sophisticated pattern recognition tools.
An early example of this was the case in Manhattan where a lawyer representing a claimant suing China Southern Airlines for
an injury on a flight had relied on ChatGPT-generated research and cited the fictional cases “Martinex v Delta Air Lines” and
“Zicherman v Korean Airlines”. These cases had been entirely made up by ChatGPT and both lawyers were fined US$4,600 for
misconduct. 57  Following this, a Texas judge issued an order banning the use of generative AI without additional fact-checking
conducted by an actual person. 58
These examples should serve as a warning for any lawyer tempted to rely on AI to form arguments or submissions without
checking all sources.
How hallucinations occur
10-035
AI models like ChatGPT are trained on vast datasets collected from the internet, books, articles and other sources. These datasets
contain a mixture of accurate information, misinformation, and everything in between. The model learns to mimic patterns in
this data, meaning it can also learn and reproduce inaccuracies or biases present in its training material. Despite their complexity,
GPT models lack true comprehension and, as a result of training data limitations coupled with pattern overgeneralisation and lack
of grounded reasoning, it is conceptually easy for a model to generate a plausible sounding but entirely fabricated statements.
The way a question or prompt is phrased can significantly influence the model’s output. Vague or ambiguous prompts can lead
to responses that are more likely to contain hallucinated information. This is because the model attempts to fill in gaps with
what it predicts is most likely, based on the patterns it has learned.
AI models can also overgeneralise from their training data, making broad statements that seem logical but are inaccurate in
specific contexts.

AI output and interactions become increasingly “human”, this problem has the potential to increase. As a lawyer, there are some
fairly obvious strategies that can be used. These might go without saying but are included here to highlight the fact that, despite
these simple and obvious checks, it is still possible, e.g. for a junior lawyer under pressure, not to do these things.

Technical developments
10-037
Recognising the fundamental importance of building user confidence in AI, the developers know that they must resolve the issue
of hallucination. As a result, increasingly rigorous training and validation processes are being deployed to ensure AI models
accurately reflect reality and are less prone to generating false information. Focusing just on the application of AI in the legal
sector, the best tools will ultimately be trained on high-quality, verified datasets, including existing legal research platforms.
Even if the dataset is perfect, given the predictive nature of current algorithms, additional error detection algorithms are
being developed. These are specifically designed to detect and correct errors or anomalies in AI outputs, including checks for
consistency with known legal facts and precedents.
Developments also include incorporating mechanisms for cross-referencing AI-generated outputs with authoritative legal
databases and fact-checking tools to verify the accuracy of references and citations.

are aware of the potential for AI to generate incorrect or misleading information. As with the early US cases, it is anticipated
that this is likely to lead to guidelines that are either approved by the court or the relevant regulatory body.

(iii) fairness; (iv) accountability and governance; and (v) contestability and redress.
At a practical level, the guidelines boil down to reminding practitioners that they should take care to check the integrity of outputs
of AI, to consider the “black-box” characteristic of AI’s responses, be aware of potential bias in the outputs, and to ensure that
confidentiality and intellectual property obligations are not breached. The guidance ends with a reminder that irresponsible use
of LLMs can lead to harsh and embarrassing consequences, including claims for professional negligence, breach of contract,
breach of confidence, defamation, data protection infringements, infringement of IP rights (including passing off claims) and
damage to reputation; as well as breaches of professional rules and duties, leading to disciplinary action and sanctions.

The role of AI as an arbitrator
10-049
Whilst it may not be just around the corner, there is also a lot of discussion about the use of AI to carry out the role of an
arbitrator. There is arguably a fine line between a tool that is heavily relied on by an arbitrator to assess a case, analyse evidence
and assist in the production of an award, and a tool that carries out the entire function of a sole arbitrator.
As a binding procedure, using AI as an arbitrator is different from using it as a tool. Particular care would have to be taken
to ensure that any use of AI does not inadvertently reduce the capacity to enforce an arbitral award through the New York
Convention. 74  If such a challenge came, it would most likely be the award conforming to the due process requirements of art.V
including complying with national arbitration laws.
One of the primary reasons why AI tools are not likely to become arbitrators sitting on international arbitration tribunals in
the short term is the problems that can arise from national arbitration laws specifying that only natural persons can act as
arbitrators. 75  This is likely to prevent AI tools acting as arbitrators without human involvement until there is a change to the
relevant national laws. However, a review carried out of the US Federal Arbitration Act (FAA) concluded that the FAA allows
for non-lawyer arbitrators and drew the tentative conclusion that AI platforms could potentially serve in this capacity if the
parties agreed. 76  As such, it follows that, whilst an award derived from an AI platform might face a challenge and the courts
would have the capacity to annul or modify it, they would be slow to do so in the absence of manifest error or fraud.
However, even if machines were allowed to be arbitrators, concerns would still remain about impartiality, independence and
fairness, and the inertia that permeates the legal system would remain. These fundamental questions would need more than a
technical answer and concerns are likely to remain unless there was some education and/or incentivisation to encourage the
use of AI tools.
In order to consider further what areas of AI may need to be regulated or controlled in arbitration, it is worth recapping on the
potential use of AI to assist arbitrators (as opposed to being the arbitrator itself). As has been set out, AI has the potential to
improve arbitration proceedings by assisting with document review and analysis to pinpoint key information, highlight trends or
patterns, and detect inconsistencies. AI also has the potential to assist with assessment of evidence including analysing witness
statements and uncovering potential contradictions.
Noting the indication in the EU AI Act of the areas that are likely to be regulated to the point of licencing or controlling their
use, it is more likely that such controls/pressure will be placed on the providers of the tools themselves rather than the users
of AI for commercial legal services.
However, the bigger challenge comes if AI were used in drafting elements of awards. This use could range from writing
procedural summaries to streamline the process through to determining or advising on the award itself.
The risks and challenges of using AI in arbitration that are worth considering for regulation include the potential for bias (or
perpetuating unfairness) and the difficulties in providing a reliable explanation for any decisions. Extensive use of AI might also
lead to the unintended consequence of arbitrators becoming deskilled and losing (or diluting) their ability to carry out critical
judgment. This is already a risk with junior lawyers and there is no real difference in arbitration. Finally, although there is no
real indication of how effective this would be, there is a need to be aware of the potential for adversarial manipulation where
parties could attempt to manipulate the input data to “game” an AI system, aiming to produce favourable outcomes.
Unlike lawyers, accountants and doctors, although there is important institutional certification, being an arbitrator is not a
regulated profession and there is no equivalent of striking-off an arbitrator who has behaved unethically. As such, the concern
over use of AI arguably needs to be considered alongside the question of how a party knows that a human arbitrator is impartial,
independent and fair. The answer to this is that they can’t but that their confidence lays at the feet of the reputation and
certification of the chosen arbitrators.
It is arguable how much benefit there will be gained from regulating an AI tool when there is no regulation of the user of the tool.
This would be the equivalent of developing an advanced surgical robot that has a rigorous design, testing and safety certificate

and then allowing anyone off the street to use it to carry out brain surgery. In that sense, the regulation of the tool would serve
no useful purpose. Pushing the analogy, if the regulatory authorities were concerned about the risks to society of bias or fairness
arising in arbitral awards, they would regulate the arbitrators and not AI for use in arbitration.
However, for the consistent use of AI by arbitrators, for example as an assistant to analyse evidence or to write awards, an
obvious step would be for institutions either to certify any AI tool that meets certain requirements or to develop their own tools
for use by arbitrators acting in proceedings being run under their rules. These certification requirements would need careful
thought but would be likely to involve some statistical proof of anti-bias filters, an ability to give full reasons (with reference
to evidence and/or exhibits) and some test of legal ability (a bit like passing the bar exams) with reference to some standard
applications or theoretical cases.
Given the essential need for the parties mutually to agree all key elements of proceedings, it would also be an essential step
for institutions to require parties to consent to use of AI by an arbitrator. If this was not done, the scope for appeal at the point
of enforcement would greatly increase.
Possible regulatory measures
10-050
In summary, if regulation were considered, it should be done with a clear eye on managing the risks whilst still providing
access to the benefits of using AI. Given that the capacities of AI are evolving almost exponentially, any regulatory framework
should be flexible enough to accommodate the rapid development. In addition, the regulations should be developed through a
collaborative approach between all parties including arbitration institutions, legal experts, arbitrators and AI developers. The
reason for giving some focus to institutions is that, for AI to be accepted in use by arbitrators, any regulation or certification
of the use of AI would need to be a near-global standard. If this was not achieved, there could be challenges in choosing a
seat, or constituting a tribunal, if the arbitrators were all concerned about regulations in their home nation that may differ from
other members of the tribunal.
Accepting the need to try and get international consensus, possible regulatory measures could include ethical guidelines based
on an obligation for transparency about how AI is used in each arbitration. Consistent with this objective of openness, there could
also be a requirement to declare the type of AI model used and how its output feeds into the arbitral decision-making process.
Although it would be difficult given the complex nature of AI reasoning, guidelines should be provided to address the issue of
accountability and who is responsible for AI-related errors. As a starting point, it would follow that as AI is being used to assist
the tribunal, the accountability should start with the tribunal, as this is where the accountability for manifest errors currently lays.
If there was institutional involvement, the evolution of regulations relating to the use of AI in arbitral proceedings would benefit
from the institutions developing rules that could be updated to require arbitrators to disclose their intended use of AI and the
specific tools involved. Institutions could also put in place procedures to enable parties to challenge or dispute the outputs of
AI systems where concerns exist about unfairness or errors.
A critical element of the regulatory approach would also have to focus on AI literacy with arbitrators and practitioners requiring
upskilling on AI’s functions, strengths and limitations so they can use it responsibly and critically.
Potential AI certification
10-051
While there is currently no formal, widely recognised certification scheme for AI tools in arbitration, there are strong signs
indicating a move towards it. Part of the drive to certification is the growing awareness of its need with increasing discussions
and initiatives highlighting the need for regulation and transparency around AI in arbitration. These discussions are not fully
coordinated but are likely to pave the way for potential standards and certification processes.

Organisations like the Silicon Valley Arbitration and Mediation Center (SVAMC) have released draft guidelines on AI in
arbitration, which have the potential to become the foundation for future certification frameworks. 77  In addition, major
organisations, and institutions like the ICC, UNCITRAL, and CIArb, are actively considering questions around AI’s role in
arbitration. Ideally, given the global nature of international arbitration, these efforts should be coordinated but after the first
authoritative launch, there is likely to be a rapid adoption and, ultimately, a convergence of certification standards.
The practical steps for a hypothetical AI certification scheme for arbitration would be likely to involve setting standards covering
transparency, capability, and elimination or mitigation of bias with benchmarks or test protocols to validate the AI tool’s ability
to minimise potential biases. There is also likely to be a need to establish the standards for data used to train and validate any AI
tool. These standards would include providing reassurance of relevance, reliability and accuracy. The certification would also
include determining how errors are identified and managed as well as who takes responsibility for this. An effective certification
programme would also include beta-testing involving a human reviewer testing the system with the ability to feedback and
provide potential human overrides at any stage of proceedings.
Apart from the equivalent of bug fixes, given the speed of evolution of the capacity of AI platforms, there would need to be a
regular review process including upgrades and corresponding certification. In the event that a tool was found to be failing post-
certification, there would need to be a process for de-certifying and informing users that the use was no longer certified.
Whilst certification would provide a critical level of reassurance, it wouldn’t eliminate risk entirely. However, if developed with
joined-up thinking, it would raise the bar for AI tools used in arbitration, promoting transparency and accountability.
Vision for the future
10-052
It is almost inevitable that the power of AI in transactional law will continue to grow in the areas of drafting, reviewing and
assessing legal documents. Focusing on dispute resolution, the capacity of generative AI to produce new ideas also lends itself to
more proactively considering alternative dispute resolution outcomes that are focused on mutual benefits going forward rather
than just resolving the dispute.
It is equally inevitable that AI tools will reduce the costs and time involved in resolving disputes, as well as enhance the quality
and consistency of legal analysis supporting decisions.
A key question is if/how the use of AI will transition from being used (or seen) as a form of data-cruncher to being able to
provide reliable advice/analysis akin to that provided by a qualified lawyer. In April 2023, a study was carried out to assess
GPT4’s ability to pass the US Uniform Bar Exam (UBE). 78  This showed GPT4 passing the UBE scoring an average of 75.7%
against an average law student scoring 68%. The pass mark is 60%. The study provided the results for the different modules,
and this identified GPT4’s strongest areas as contracts (88%), evidence (85.2%) and criminal law and procedure (81.1%). Its
weakest performance was in civil procedure (61.1%), but it beat the human students in every area apart from tort (GPT4 =
64.9% v Human 71%). 79
The real significance of this detailed study is that in the 12 months between GPT3 and GPT4, the improvements in AI have
led to GPT4 passing the bar exam. The tool that passed the UBE did not have any specific legal training or special algorithms.
Instead, the gains were driven by the scale of the underlying models more than any fine-tuning for law.
Loosely applying Moore’s Law (as explained in para.10-024), the trajectory indicates that, in the next 12 months, GPT4 is
likely to have the capacity not only to pass the bar exam but to become the top student. Will that make it a “good” lawyer? Not
necessarily. This is because the human element, the ability to empathise, the ability to “think” strategically, and, ultimately, the
ability to persuade judges, still needs to be developed and proven.
Turning back to dispute resolution, generative AI could certainly enable new forms of dispute resolution that are more
interactive, collaborative and personalised. For example, parties could use conversational agents that can generate natural
language responses and guide them through a structured dialogue to identify their interests, preferences and options. These
agents could also provide feedback, suggestions and explanations based on relevant laws, precedents and best practice.

Alternatively, parties could use immersive technologies, such as virtual or augmented reality, which can generate realistic
scenarios and simulations to help them explore different perspectives, empathise with each other and test potential solutions.
To balance the vision set out above, although AI has the potential to smooth the path of dispute resolution, in order to fully explore
this potential, it is necessary to consider the source of the parties’ disagreements and whether the disagreements are over the
facts, the law, or how the law applies to the facts. The source of the parties’ disagreements matters as it may determine the extent
to which AI can help resolve their disputes. AI may be helpful in clarifying the parties’ misunderstanding over how well-defined
questions of law apply to their facts but may be less helpful when parties disagree on questions of fact where the prevailing
facts dictate the legal outcome. 80  A further problem arises from the fact that, currently, AI’s strength lies in application of
computational power to publicly available data but, behind the public data there is a mountain of private information and micro-
agreements and disagreements. As a result, although AI might provide clarity over the application of legal precedent, it may
still struggle to provide clarity over the outcome of a legal dispute.
As carefully set out by Niblett and Yoon, concerns over the application of AI for dispute resolution include general concerns
about the limitation of automation 81  with specific concerns already highlighted including algorithmic bias, 82  lack of
transparency, 83  failure to provide adequate explanations, 84  and whether parties will trust the decisions of an algorithm. There is
also the risk that using algorithms based on previous decisions, if used categorically, could calcify the common law, potentially
limiting its usefulness in new or rapidly evolving legal areas. 85
It follows that the future of AI in dispute resolution does not necessarily imply a replacement of human lawyers or adjudicators,
but rather a redefinition of their roles and skills. Sir Geoffrey Vos has referred to a digital justice system in terms of a funnel
comprising three tiers, with the first being where the problem is identified by systems utilising AI that serves to direct a would-
be litigant to the best online pre-action site for their problem or to online legal advice, whether that be AI or human. 86  This
vision includes a second tier comprised of a suite of ODR portals and only if this second tier fails is the potential litigant directed
to an online digital court process.
This is in line with a future dispute resolution world where human lawyers focus more on complex, creative and strategic
tasks, such as advising, counselling, advocating and designing solutions, while delegating more routine, repetitive and
standardised tasks to AI systems. Adjudicators could also benefit from AI assistance in analysing evidence, identifying issues
and applying rules, while retaining the final authority and discretion over the decision-making process. AI could also enhance
the transparency, accountability and explainability of dispute resolution, by providing traceable, verifiable, and interpretable
data and reasoning.
The future of AI in dispute resolution may have different implications for different types of legal practitioners. This will depend
on their functions, jurisdictions, and markets. For instance, barristers and court advocates, who typically specialise in advocacy
and representation in courts or tribunals, may face significant competition from AI systems that can generate persuasive written
arguments and submissions. If the AI system also has the capacity to simulate the behaviour and preferences of judges and
arbitrators, it is easy to see the tension this could create in the market for providing written advice and opinions. However, there
could be increased opportunities for counsel to use their oral advocacy skills and skills of persuasion or to act as advisers for
businesses trying to develop a legal strategy to avoid litigation or arbitration.
Solicitors, who generally provide a broader range of legal services, such as drafting contracts, conducting due diligence or
facilitating transactions, may have more opportunities to collaborate with AI systems that can automate and optimise these
tasks, as well as generate new insights and solutions.
In any event, these distinctions are not fixed, or universal, as different legal systems and cultures may have different definitions
and regulations of the roles and responsibilities of legal practitioners. AI may ultimately serve to bring the roles together.
