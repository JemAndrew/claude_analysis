Rule 44_4
44.4— Basis of assessment 1
7B-13+
(1) Where the court is to assess the amounts of costs (whether by summary or detailed assessment) it will assess
those costs—
(a) on the standard basis; or
(b) on the indemnity basis,
but the court will not in either case allow costs which have been unreasonably incurred or are unreasonable in amount.
(Rule 48.3 sets out how the court decides the amount of costs payable under a contract.)
(2) Where the amount of costs is to be assessed on the standard basis, the court will—
(a) only allow costs which are proportionate to the matters in issue; and
(b) resolve any doubt which it may have as to whether costs were reasonably incurred or reasonable and
proportionate in amount in favour of the paying party.
(Factors which the court may take into account are set out in rule 44.5.)
(3) Where the amount of costs is to be assessed on the indemnity basis, the court will resolve any doubt which it may
have as to whether costs were reasonably incurred or were reasonable in amount in favour of the receiving party.
(4) Where—
(a) the court makes an order about costs without indicating the basis on which the costs are to be assessed; or
(b) the court makes an order for costs to be assessed on a basis other than the standard basis or the indemnity
basis, the costs will be assessed on the standard basis.
(5) [Omitted]
(6) Where the amount of a solicitor’s remuneration in respect of non-contentious business is regulated by any general
orders made under the Solicitors Act 1974, the amount of the costs to be allowed in respect of any such business
which falls to be assessed by the court will be decided in accordance with those general orders rather than this rule
and rule 44.5.

Editorial note
7B-13.
1+
Paragraph (5), which disapplied r.44.5 to the extent that regulations made under the Legal Aid Act 1988 determined the amount
of costs payable, was revoked by the Civil Procedure (Amendment No.3) Rules 2000 (SI 2000/1317) as a consequence of the
replacement of the Legal Aid Fund by the Community Legal Service Fund.
After the re-enactment of Pt 44 by the Civil Procedure (Amendment) Rules 2013 (SI 2013/262), with effect from 1 April 2013,
this rule became r.44.3, and was substantially amended in certain respects. See further para.44.3.1 above.
The test to be applied under each basis of assessment is whether or not the costs have been reasonably incurred or are reasonable
in amount. On the standard basis the additional test of proportionality is imposed and the court will only allow costs which are
proportionate to the matters in issue. Rule 44.5 (at para.7B-14+) sets out the factors which the court must take into account
in deciding the amount of costs.
Rule 44.4(2)—proportionality
7B-13.
2+
The test of proportionality set out in r.44.4(2)(a) (as it stood before 1 April 2013) applies to costs incurred in cases commenced
before 1 April 2013, whenever the work was done, and to costs incurred in respect of work done before 1 April 2013 in cases
commenced after that date: r.44.3(7) (see paras 44.3 and 44.3.4 above). A case is commenced when the proceedings are issued
by the court: Harrison v University Hospitals Coventry & Warwickshire NHS Trust [2017] EWCA Civ 792; [2017] 1 W.L.R.
4456, CA. The test continues to apply also to additional liabilities recoverable under pre-commencement funding arrangements
as defined by r.48.2(1): BNM v MGN Ltd [2017] EWCA Civ 1767; [2018] 1 W.L.R. 1450, CA. It does not apply to ATE insurance
premiums which are recoverable under the Recovery of Costs in Clinical Negligence Proceedings (No.2) Regulations 2013 in
cases commenced on or after 1 April 2013: Peterborough & Stamford Hospitals NHS Trust v McMenemy [2017] EWCA Civ
1941; [2018] 1 W.L.R. 2685, CA.
On the assessment of costs on the standard basis, the application of the test of proportionality set out in r.44.4(2)(a) requires a
two-stage approach. First the court should consider whether the total sum claimed is or appears to be disproportionate having
particular regard to the considerations which r.44.5(3) (as it stood before 1 April 2013) states are relevant. (From 1 April 2013,
r.44.5(3) became r.44.4(3).) If the costs as a whole appear to be disproportionate, the second stage will apply and the court will
consider whether each item of work was necessary and, if necessary, whether it was reasonably incurred. Under the second
stage an item which was not necessary or was not reasonably incurred will be disallowed.
“If, because of lack of planning or due to other causes, the global costs are disproportionately high, then the
requirement that the costs should be proportionate means that no more should be payable than would have been
payable if the litigation had been conducted in a proportionate manner. This in turn means that reasonable costs
will only be recovered for the items which were necessary if the litigation had been conducted in a proportionate
manner.”
Home Office v Lownds [2002] EWCA Civ 365; [2002] 1 W.L.R. 2450; [2002] 4 All E.R. 775, CA.
In considering what was necessary, a sensible standard of necessity has to be adopted. This is a standard which takes fully into
account the need to make allowances for the different judgments which those responsible for litigation can sensibly come to
as to what is required. The danger of setting too high a standard with the benefit of hindsight has to be avoided. The threshold
required to meet “necessity” is higher than that of “reasonable” but it is still a standard that a competent practitioner should be
able to achieve without undue difficulty. When a practitioner incurs expenses which are reasonable but not necessary they may

be able to recover the fees and disbursements from their client but the extra expense which results from conducting litigation
in a disproportionate matter cannot be recovered from the other party.
In deciding what is necessary the conduct of the other party is highly relevant. A party who is uncooperative may render
necessary, costs which would otherwise be unnecessary and it is acceptable that they should pay the costs for the expense which
they have made necessary.
Dealing with the situation where a claimant recovers relief which is significantly less than they have claimed the Court of
Appeal stated that the following approach should be followed:
“Whether the costs incurred were proportionate should be decided having regard to what it was reasonable for
the party in question to believe might be recovered, thus:
 
(i)the proportionality of the costs incurred by the claimant should be determined having regard to the sum
that it was reasonable for him to believe that he might recover at the time he made his claim;
 
(ii)the proportionality of the costs incurred by the defendant should be determined having regard to the sum
it was reasonable for him to believe that the claimant might recover should his claim succeed.
This is likely to be the amount that the claimant has claimed, for a defendant will normally be entitled to take
a claim at its face value. The rationale for this approach is that a claimant should be allowed to incur the cost
necessary to pursue a reasonable claim but not allowed to recover costs increased or incurred by putting forward
an exaggerated claim and a defendant should not be prejudiced if he assumes the claim which was made was
one which was reasonable and incurs costs in contesting the claim on this assumption.” Lownds v Home Office
[above] at para.39.
Although the amount of financial compensation may provide a useful starting point in determining proportionality, there were
cases where the need to press for other remedies would assume greater significance; for example, in defamation cases: Cox v
MGN Ltd [2006] EWHC 1235 (QB); [2006] 5 Costs L.R. 764 (Eady J).
If, applying the first stage of the test, the court concludes that the bill is not disproportionate, it is not precluded from deciding
that an item or a number of items are or appear disproportionate having regard to the matters in issue, and then applying the test
of necessity to that item or items. In the unlikely event that a Costs Judge at the initial stage is unable to say whether the bill
viewed as a whole is proportionate or disproportionate he will be obliged to carry out a detailed assessment applying the dual
test: Giambrone v JMC Holidays Ltd (Costs) [2002] EWHC 2932 (QB); [2003] 1 All E.R. 982 (Morland J).
Having found that the costs as a whole appeared disproportionate, the court had to consider the necessity of each item and could
not then decide that an item, or number of items, were in fact proportionate, and that the test of necessity should not apply to
them: Motto v Trafigura Ltd [2011] EWCA Civ 1150; [2012] 1 W.L.R. 657, CA. Nor can the court simply reduce the global
figure to one which appears proportionate: Re Michaelides [2003] EWHC 3029 (Ch); [2004] B.P.I.R. 613 (Blackburne J).
When giving a ruling on whether the costs as a whole are disproportionate it is not imperative for the court to go through the
items in r.44.5 as a check list: Ortwein v Rugby Mansions Ltd [2003] EWHC 2077 (Ch); [2014] 1 Costs L.R. 26 (Lloyd J);
Young v JR Smart (Builders) Ltd (Costs) [2004] EWHC 103 (QB); [2004] 2 Costs L.R. 298 (Henriques J).
