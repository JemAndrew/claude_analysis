Claim by an assignee under an equitable assignment
1.
6-C1
By a contract made in writing and set out in an agreement dated [date], the Defendant agreed to buy from the First
Claimant 36  a boat, The Aiolos, in consideration of the sum of £[amount].
2.
On or about [date], the First Claimant delivered The Aiolos to the Defendant.
3.
On [date], the First Claimant orally 37  assigned to the Second Claimant the debt of £[amount] owing by the Defendant
to the First Claimant. 38
4.
On [date], the Second Claimant gave notice to the Defendant of the assignment. 39
5.
The Defendant has not paid the sum of £[amount] or any sum.
6.
By reason of the facts and matters as aforesaid, the Second Claimant claims the sum of £[amount] from the Defendant.
7.
Further, the Second Claimant is entitled to and claims interest on the said sum pursuant to s.35A of the Senior Courts Act
1981 at such rate and for such period as the Court in its discretion considers fit. 40
AND the Second Claimant claims against the Defendant:
 
(1)The sum of £[amount].
 
(2)Interest as aforesaid.
[Statement of truth]

Footnotes
36
i.e. the assignor. For the reasons given in paras 6-07 to 6-08 above, the assignor should generally be joined, preferably
as claimant, otherwise as defendant.
37
There is no particular form of words that is required for an assignment (see Smith & Leslie, The Law of Assignment
(2018), paras 13.55 to 13.58) nor does the equitable assignment of a legal chose like a right under a contract or a debt
need to be in writing (see Smith & Leslie, The Law of Assignment (2018), paras 13.64 to 13.66). In particular, s.53(1)
(c) of the Law of Property Act 1925 does not apply in this case.
38
Consideration is not a requirement for an equitable assignment, although no doubt if the assignment was by way of
contract, this would be pleaded. See, further, Smith & Leslie, The Law of Assignment (2018), paras 13.79 to 13.84.
39
Notice is not, in fact, a requirement for a valid equitable assignment: see Smith & Leslie, The Law of Assignment (2018),
paras 13.69 to 13.78. However, for a number of reasons, the giving of notice of the assignment to the debtor is a good
idea: Smith & Leslie, The Law of Assignment (2018), para.13.69. In particular, it is only after notice of the assignment
that the debtor will be obliged to account to the assignee. Before notice, the debtor can account to the assignor: Smith
& Leslie, The Law of Assignment (2018), paras 26.11 to 26.22.
40
Or s.69 of the County Courts Act 1984.
