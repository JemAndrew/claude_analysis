The Consumer Credit Act 1974
20-16
This Act, with its substantial body of subordinate legislation, represents a major statutory intervention in the law of contract.
Its provisions are detailed, lengthy and extremely complex. The summary provided here can be no substitute for consulting
specialist works on the subject.
Scheme of the CCA
20-17
The purpose of the CCA was protection of debtors and hirers by:
 
1.licensing all branches of the credit and hire business;
 
2.controlling advertising and canvassing;
 
3.requiring full information to be given by creditors;
 
4.regulating the form and content of documents;
 
5.restricting rights of termination and enforcement;
 
6.giving debtors and hirers rights of early termination;
 
7.making the creditor liable for faulty goods and services;
 
8.giving relief from various types of extortionate or unfair agreements.
Financial limits
20-18
Financial limits were crucial to the operation of the CCA up to 2008 and retained some importance thereafter. For credit
agreements the limit depends on the amount of credit advanced. With hire-purchase and conditional sale agreements the credit
is the difference between the total amount payable under the agreement and the aggregate of the deposit and the charge for
credit: CCA s.9(3). With running-account credit, the limit is normally fixed at the credit limit imposed by the agreement and if
the agreement does not impose one, the court will assume a fixed or lower limit when it is clear the parties never contemplated
that the credit would exceed a particular amount: s.10(3). For hire agreements the limit depends on the total rentals the hirer
is required to pay: s.15.
The CCA regulated agreements within the financial limits current at the date of the agreement—the upper limits being:

One of the major changes brought about by the 2006 Act was the abolition of financial limits. All financial limits for regulation
were abolished in relation to all agreements made on or after 6 April 2008. Thus all “consumer credit agreements” and “consumer
hire agreements” (i.e. credit and hire agreements made with individuals) were regulated by the CCA unless the subject of an
express exemption under the Act or its subordinate legislation.
Since 2008 the exemptions have been:
 
(a)Regulated mortgage contracts or regulated home purchase plans (CCA s.16 and RAO art.60C).
 
(b)Credit agreements for credit over £25,000 where the agreement or “green deal plan” is entered into by the borrower
“wholly or predominantly for the purposes of a business carried on or intended to be carried on by the borrower” (CCA
s.16B and RAO art.60C): note that there is provision for the borrower to sign a certificate to this effect in a prescribed
form which is evidence, though not conclusive evidence, that this is a business agreement. Similar rules are applied to hire
agreements for business purposes (CCA s.16B and RAO art.600).
 
(c)Secured investment loans (“buy-to-let” mortgages) where the loan is secured on land with less than 40% of the land
occupied by the borrower or his family (CCA s.16C and RAO art.60D; but note that, for contracts made after 21 March
2016, unless exempted as business agreements, such agreements are subject to a regulatory system similar to that for
consumer credit agreements under the Mortgage Credit Directive Order 2015 (SI 2015/910).
 
(d)Certain agreements secured on land if the lender is a local authority or one of a list of institutional lenders formerly
named in Schedules to the Consumer Credit (Exempt Agreements) Order 1989 and now in rules made by the FCA (CCA
s.16 and RAO art.60E).
 
(e)Certain categories of agreement categorised by being repayable over no more than 12 months and by no more than 12
instalments as well as having no interest or other charges (CCA s.16 and RAO art.60F).
 
(f)Certain categories of agreement where there is a very low rate of interest (CCA s.16 and RAO art.60G).
 
(g)Agreements secured on land or unsecured agreements over for credit over £60,260 made with “high net worth”
borrowers, a fact which has to be certified on the agreement by a signed and prescribed rubric (this certificate being
conclusive) (CCA s.16A and RAO art.60H). Similar rules are applied to hire agreements with high net worth individuals
(CCA s.16A and RAO art.60Q).
 
(h)Some hire agreements made by utilities (CCA s.16 and RAO art.60P).
Non-commercial agreements and “small agreements” are subject to much less stringent controls.
Licencing and authorisation
20-19
Up to 1 April 2014, any person carrying on the business of providing credit or letting goods under contracts regulated by the
CCA had to obtain a licence, as did most credit-brokers, debt-adjusters, debt-counsellors, debt-collectors and credit reference
agencies. The system of licensing under the CCA was replaced by that of authorisation under FSMA. The current categories
of business which require authorisation are:
 
(a)a consumer credit business (CCA 1974 s.20(1) a RAO Ch.14A);
 
(b)a consumer hire business (CCA 1974 s.20(1) and RAO Ch. 14B);
 
(c)credit-brokerage (CCA 1974 ss.20(1) and 145(1)(a) and RAO Ch. 6A);

The significance of licencing was that agreements made by unlicensed traders (s.40) or introduced by unlicensed credit-brokers
(s.149) were unenforceable without an order from the Office of Fair Trading. Under FSMA agreements made by an unauthorised
trader are unenforceable (FSMA s.26) and those made through an unauthorised intermediary (s.26A). Money paid by the
borrower is recoverable and the borrower may be entitled to compensation (s.26A). Under FSMA s.28A, the FCA has the power
on written request by the lender to permit the agreement to be enforced if “is just and equitable in the circumstances of the case”.
Pre-contract
20-20
Pre-contractual negotiations were always strictly regulated:
 
1.An agreement to enter into a future agreement which would, if made, be regulated by the CCA is void: s.59.
 
2.A person who offers to enter into a regulated agreement as debtor or hirer can withdraw the offer by notice to the creditor
or the credit-broker (e.g. the supplier of the goods): s.57.
 
3.At least seven days before entering into an agreement secured on land (except to purchase the land), the debtor must be
sent all the relevant documents with a notice indicating his right to cancel and the creditor cannot communicate with him
during the period. Any breach by the creditor can invalidate the agreement: ss.58 and 61(2).
These provisions have been greatly extended on two occasions. First the Consumer Credit (Disclosure of Information)
Regulations 2004 (SI 2004/1481) (in force 31 May 2005) obliged creditors and owners to give prospective customers in advance
virtually all the information made obligatory for the agreement itself and in a document separate from the agreement itself, in
essence a pro forma agreement.
The second great change came with the implementation of the Consumer Credit Directive on 1 February 2011. For all credit
agreements except agreements secured on land, business agreements and agreements for credit over £60,260, an entire new
regime was introduced, starting with the pre-contract process and continuing through to the form and content of the agreement
itself. Even with the three excluded categories of agreement the creditor is permitted to ‘contract into’ the new procedures and
does so by choosing to conduct the pre-contract process under the new rules. Except for the exempted categories of agreement
where there has been no contracting in, the Disclosure Regulations of 2004 were replaced by the Consumer Credit (Disclosure of
Information Regulations) 2010 (SI 2010/1013). These oblige the creditor to provide the creditor with information in a prescribed
form, the Standard European Consumer Credit Information form (SECCI) with a reduced form (ECCI) for bank overdrafts.
Failure to comply renders any subsequent agreement improperly executed.
The Directive added three new sections to the CCA on pre-contract disclosure. CCA s.55A obliged the creditor to give a full
explanation of a series of relevant matters before the debtor signs the agreement. Section 55B made a pre-contract check on
creditworthiness compulsory and s.55C provided that the debtor must be given a copy of the proposed agreement, filled in as far
as possible (as with the 2004 Regulations) but only if he requests it. The change of régime in 2014 saw the repeal of ss.55A and
55B. They were replaced, however, by a huge code of practice, CONC, which minutely governs every aspect of pre-contract
negotiations between lender and borrower. This is on top of the obligation to give pre-contract written disclosure under the 2004
or 2010 regulations which the 2014 changes left untouched.

The formalities of the agreement
20-21
The form and contents of regulated agreements are rigidly controlled by the CCA and its attendant regulations. Until 1 February
2011, the regulations were the Consumer Credit (Agreements) Regulations 1983 but these were amended from time to time,
sometimes drastically. In outline, up to 31 May 2005, the 1983 Regulations provided that:
 
1.the agreement must have the correct heading;
 
2.it must contain all relevant information as to price of goods and services, amount of credit, rate of charge, etc.;
 
3.there must be one or more of a series of obligatory “boxes” containing statements of rights and another obligatory box
for signature: these cannot be altered by so much as a comma;
 
4.the agreement must satisfy legibility requirements, must contain all the terms agreed by the parties and must be signed
by both parties: s.61(1);
 
5.there are elaborate rules for supplying copies of the agreement to the debtor or hirer: ss.62 and 63;
 
6.where oral statements have been made to the debtor or hirer pre-contract, then unless the agreement is related to or
secured on land, agreements signed off the premises of the creditor or the credit-broker are cancellable (s.67), and it is
vital to give notice of rights of cancellation: s.64;
 
7.agreements secured on land (other than for purchase of land) are subject to an elaborate ritual: ss.58 and 61 (2).
Similar rules are applied to surety agreements (s.105): see the Consumer Credit (Guarantees and Indemnities) Regulations 1983.
The rules for the form and content of agreements were made much more complicated by the Consumer Credit (Agreements)
(Amendment) Regulations 2004 (SI 2004/1482) which came into force on 31 May 2005 and apply to agreements made after
that date. These regulations drastically amended the 1983 Regulations, thus providing two regimes, one for agreements before
31 May 2005 and one for agreements after that date. The major changes were:
 
• the prescribed information has now to be given in a fixed and immutable order;
 
• there are changes to the warning boxes and additional warning and consent boxes introduced;
 
• additional information has to be given;
 
• the legibility and prominence provisions have been greatly tightened;
 
• changes have been made to the calculation and statement of the APR.
20-22
This level of complication did not appeal to the EU legislators and the Directive imposed a lengthier but much less rigid form
of agreement, in line with its policy that the relevant information should be provided to the customer in advance by the SECCI
and explained to the customer by the creditor before the agreement was signed. This necessitated a new set of Regulations, the
Consumer Credit (Agreements) Regulations 2010 (SI 2010/1014). These provide that, in all cases where the 2010 Disclosure
Regulations (see para.20-30) apply, the creditor must ensure that the contract is in the form prescribed by the 2010 Agreements
Regulations. This means the 2010 Agreements Regulations apply in all cases where the 2010 Disclosure regulations apply
compulsorily (i.e. all credit agreements other than those secured on land, those for business purposes and those for credit over
£60,260) and in those further cases where the creditor has voluntarily chosen to comply with the 2010 Disclosure Regulations for
agreements otherwise exempt from them. An important point to note is that the EU Directive from which the 2010 Agreements
derive did not cover consumer hire at all, with the result that all hire agreements are still covered by the 1983 Agreements
Regulations and (unless very old) the version of those Regulations in force since 2005.
The form of agreement prescribed by the 2010 Agreements Regulations is much simpler (though probably longer) than that
prescribed by the 1983 Regulations, particularly as prescribed by those Regulations after 31 May 2005. The compulsory internal
headings have gone, the information can be stated much less formally and there are many fewer boxes containing warnings
or statements of rights and remedies.

Note in this context that the Directive replaced the old, very limited, right to cancel an agreement in certain circumstances with

Take caution
20-23
The upshot of all these changes is, however, that the potential pleader seeking to see whether an agreement is compliant with
the CCA has to ascertain whether the credit agreement is made:
 
(a)pre-31 May 2005 and thus covered by the “old” version of the 1983 Agreements Regulations;
 
(b)between 31 May 2005 and 1 February 2011 and probably covered by the “new” version of the 1983 Regulations (but
see (d));
 
(c)made after 1 February 2011 and compulsorily covered by the 2010 Agreements Regulations;
 
(d)made after 30 April 2010, not compulsorily covered by the 2010 Regulations (including all agreements between 30
April 2010 and 1 February 2010) but the creditor has “contracted into” the 2010 Regulations;
 
(e)made after 30 April 2010, not compulsorily covered by the 2010 Regulations but the creditor has not “contracted into”
the 2010 Regulation, where the “new” version of the 1983 Regulations will apply.
With a consumer hire agreement, (hire is untouched by the Directive), the only choices are between agreements before 31 May
2005—“old” version of the 1983 Agreements Regulations—and after that date—“new” version.
The relevance of this Byzantine code is that breach of the pre-contract disclosure rules and/or breach of the agreements rules will
render the agreement “improperly executed”. Until 6 April 2007, improperly executed agreements came into two categories.
Those that could be enforced with leave of the court in appropriate circumstances under s.127(1) and those that could never
be enforced under s.127(3) and (4), mainly agreements which omitted the information required by Sch. 6 to the Agreements
Regulations or breached certain of the rules relating to copies and cancellation notices. Section 15 of the 2006 Act abolished the
second category in respect of agreements entered into on or after 6 April 2007. Now all non-compliant agreements are subject
to discretionary enforcement by the court.
On the bright side, the 2014 changes have left the former rules about form and content of agreements unaltered and unaffected
by CONC.
Statements
20-24
The CCA has always contained detailed provisions entitling the debtor to be given a copy of his original agreement and for
the supply (both on demand and automatically) of statements of account. These requirements were hugely and horrendously
magnified by the CCA 2006 and, again, by the Directive Regulations. This has proved a fertile source of litigation, particularly
by claims farmers, because failure to provide a copy agreement or the appropriate statement places the creditor in a ‘period of
default’ during which he cannot enforce the agreement at all and interest ceases to run. Only by serving the copy agreement
or statement in accordance with the rules can the creditor re-activate the agreement and enforce it, but the lost interest is gone
for ever. This, too, is, as yet, unaffected by the 2014 changes.

Arrears
20-25
If a debtor or hirer falls into arrears by a modest amount (two monthly or, in some cases, four weekly instalments), the creditor
or owner must serve an arrears notice which is similarly detailed and combined with warning boxes. In this instance he must
also serve the debtor with a copy of the current edition of the OFT’s “Arrears Information Sheet”.
The time limits for serving these statements and notices are strict. The sanctions are severe. As with statements, any creditor
or owner who fails to serve a complete statement or notice within the prescribed time (and with the OFT sheet) will find that
the agreement is unenforceable by him until he complies and that, for any period during which he has not complied, he can
never recover interest or any default charges. These changes came into force on 1 October 2008 and apply not only to new
agreements but to any agreement still current at that time. The change of régime has substituted the FCA for the OFT as the
originator of the information sheets and the arrears provisions are now buttressed by obligations laid on the lender by CONC,
putting it crudely, to be nice to defaulters and not to press them too hard. Note that there are further rules for dealing with
defaulters contained in CONC.
Enforcement
20-26
Before any right can be exercised by the creditor or owner, save the right to recover instalments due under the agreement, notice
must be given, even if that right is not dependent upon the debtor or hirer being in breach of contract. Notice of the exercise of
rights other than the right to terminate must be given under CCA s.76, notice of the exercise of a right exercisable on default
under s.87 and notice of the right of termination for reasons other than default (e.g. death or bankruptcy) under s.98. With notices
of default, if the breach is capable of remedy, the debtor or hirer must be given the opportunity to remedy it and, if not, he must
be notified of the sum required to be paid in compensation with, in each case, a date for compliance: s.88. If the debtor or hirer
complies then the breach is wiped out: s.89. All notices must be in the form prescribed by the Consumer Credit (Enforcement,
Default and Termination Notices) Regulations 1983. Copies of all notices must be served on sureties: CCA s.111. Compliance
with these rules is a condition precedent to exercising the rights relied on.
Where more than one-third of the total sum payable has been paid under a hire-purchase or conditional sale agreement, even
after the proper notices have been served, the creditor cannot repossess the goods without a court order (s.90), and if he does,
the debtor will be released from all liability under the agreement and can recover all money previously paid to the creditor:
CCA s.91.
The 2006 Act made significant changes to the enforcement provisions. The CCA s.88 lays down the rules for default notices
served under s.87. Under s.88(1) a default notice must specify:
 
(a)the nature of the alleged breach;
 
(b)if the breach is remediable, what is to be done to remedy it and the date before which it is to be done;
 
(c)if the breach is not remediable, the sum to be paid by way of compensation and the date before which it is to be paid.
Since 1 October 2006, under s.88(2) and (3) the relevant date under s.88(1) must be not less than 14 days after the date of service
of the notice and the creditor or owner may not take any enforcement action under s.87(1) until that period has elapsed.
The 2006 Act made other changes to default notices. Changes to ss.87 and 88 of the CCA together with the Consumer Credit
(Information Requirements and Duration of Licences and Charges) Regulations 2007 (SI 2007/1167) considerably increased
the amount of information and statements of rights to be included in default and termination notices and there is an obligation
to accompany such a notice with a copy of the current edition of the OFT’s “Default Information Sheet” (now produced by
the FCA). The sanction for non-compliance is that the agreement is unenforceable during any period of non-compliance and
interest and default charges cease to be payable.

The Directive made no material changes to the enforcement provisions and neither did the change to FSMA regulation in

enforcement such as court proceedings or taking possession of mortgaged properties is very much frowned upon unless it is
really a “last resort”.
Termination by the debtor or hirer
20-27
Sections 94 to 97 and ss.99 and s.100 give various rights to the debtor to terminate credit agreements ahead of time and provide
for the outstanding charges to be rebated in consequence. Section 101 gives a hirer the right to terminate a hire agreement after
the agreement has run at least 18 months.
It should also be noted that the previously existing right of the debtor to repay the credit early under CCA s.94 has been expanded
by the Directive as from 1 February 2011 to a right to pay off part of the credit and not solely the whole credit. There is a new
right for a creditor who suffers loss as a result of early repayment to recover a limited “compensation”.
Pawn
20-28
Contracts of pledge are strictly regulated, except for non-commercial agreements and pledges of documents of title or bearer
bonds: s.114(3). The pawnor must be given a pawn-receipt (s.114(1)) in the form specified by the Consumer Credit (Pawn-
Receipts) Regulations 1983. Pawning by minors is forbidden: s.114(2). The pawn is redeemable for at least six months (longer,
if so agreed) (s.116) and must be delivered up to the person tendering the pawn-receipt and the sum due: s.117. If the pawn is
unredeemed within the period, then, if the pawn is for less than £25 and the redemption period is six months, property in the
pawned goods passes automatically to the pawnee: otherwise the pawn becomes realisable by sale on notice to the pawnor, the
pawnee giving notice of the result of the sale to the pawnor and paying to him any surplus over the amount due: ss.120–121.
Note that, despite pawn being outside the scope of the Directive, the 2010 Disclosure Regulations and the 2010 Agreements
Regulations contain special (and slightly strange) provisions for pawn agreements which contain pitfalls for the unwary.
Negotiable instruments
20-29
It is forbidden to take a negotiable instrument other than a bank note or cheque in discharge of the obligations of a debtor or
hirer or of a surety under a regulated agreement: CCA s.123(1). A cheque taken in discharge of such an obligation can only be
negotiated to a bank and no negotiable instrument (including a cheque) may be taken as security for any such payment: s.123(2)
and (3). Breach of these rules makes the agreement itself and, where relevant, the security agreement unenforceable without
leave of the court (s.124) and the validity of the instruments themselves is adversely affected: s.125.

Extortionate credit bargains
20-30
The CCA introduced the concept of extortionate credit bargains by ss.137-140. If the court finds a credit bargain to be
extortionate by requiring the making of “grossly exorbitant” payments or by “otherwise contravening the principles of fair
dealing”, the court, can re-open the agreement and largely rewrite it: ss.137–140. These provisions apply to all credit agreements
where the debtor is an individual and are not confined to agreements regulated by the CCA. Further this part of the CCA applies
fully retrospectively and applies to an agreement whenever it was made (even, theoretically, before 1974). Although the court
has the widest possible powers, in practice, few agreements have yet been held to be extortionate. The provisions do not apply
to agreements made on or after 6 April 2007 for the reasons set out in the following paragraphs.
Unfair relationships
20-31
Extortionate credit bargains were replaced on 6 April 2007 by a concept of “unfair relationships between creditors and debtors”
which was intended to be far wider in its ambit and to give judges much more discretion to intervene in the operation of
agreements which, in some way or other, offend against ordinary canons of fair dealing. CCA ss.140A-140C apply to all credit
agreements (including otherwise exempt agreements) except for regulated mortgages but (like ss.137–140) do not apply to hire
agreements.
Section 140A confers on the court the power to make an order under s.140B:
“if it determines that the relationship between the creditor and the debtor arising out of the agreement (or the
agreement taken with any related agreement) is unfair to the debtor because of one or more of the following—
 
(a)any of the terms of the agreement or of any related agreement;
 
(b)the way in which the creditor has exercised or enforced any of his rights under the agreement or any
related agreement;
 
(c)any other thing done (or not done) by, or on behalf of, the creditor (either before or after the making of
the agreement or any related agreement).”
Section 140A(2) enables the court to have regard to “all matters it thinks relevant” which means that the court can have regard
to the acts or omissions of any associate or former associate of the creditor and to treat them as the acts or omissions of the
creditor himself.
The court may do any or all of the following:
 
(a)require repayment of any payment made to anyone by the debtor or by a surety by virtue of the agreement or any related
agreement;
 
(b)require the creditor to do or cease doing anything in connection with the agreement or any related agreement;
 
(c)reduce or discharge any sum payable by the debtor or a surety;
 
(d)direct the return to a surety of any property provided by way of security;
 
(e)set aside any obligation of the debtor or surety;
 
(f)alter the terms of the agreement or any related agreement;
 
(g)order accounts to be taken between any persons.

New cause of action
20-32
It is important to note that FSMA created a new cause of action. This pre-dates the 2014 changes but has achieved increased
importance in the light of consumer credit being regulated by FSMA. The FCA handbooks are (in general terms) divided into
Rules (designated with the letter R) and Guidance (with the letter G). Breach of Rules gave rise to a right of action by the
consumer against the trader under s.150 of FSMA. In the 2014 re-organisation, s.150 became s.138D. The relevant parts of
s.138D read:
 
(2)“A contravention by an authorised person of a rule made by the FCA is actionable at the suit of a private person who
suffers loss as a result of the contravention, subject to the defences and other incidents applying to actions for breach of
statutory duty.
 
(4)In prescribed cases, a contravention of a rule which by virtue of subsection (1) or (2) would be actionable at the suit of
a private person is actionable at the suit of a person who is not a private person, subject to the defences and other incidents
applying to actions for breach of statutory duty.
 
(6)‘Private person’ has such meaning as may be prescribed.”
The term “private” person is defined in the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Rights of Action) Regulations 2001
(2001/2256) reg.3(1) of which states:
 
(1)“In these Regulations, private person means—
 
(a)any individual, unless he suffers the loss in question in the course of carrying on—
 
(i)any regulated activity; or
 
(ii)…
 
(b)any person who is not an individual, unless he suffers the loss in question in the course of carrying on business
of any kind;
 
(c)a relevant recipient of credit (within the meaning of the Regulated Activities Order) who is not an individual and
who has suffered the loss in question in connection with an activity of the kind specified by article 36A, 39D, 39E, 39F,

Claims relating to faulty goods or services: general
20-33
Claims relating to services do not cause any real problems. Contracts for services cannot by definition be the subject of sale,
hire-purchase or conditional sale. Thus any credit element would be confined to what is in effect (though not necessarily in
form) a loan. If the loan is from a person other than the supplier of the services, the customer does not, in general, have any
rights against the lender unless the loan is regulated by the CCA. If, however, the loan is within the categories of transaction
set out in s.75 and s.75A of the CCA, the customer has the same remedies against the lender as he has against the supplier.
As for goods, conditional sale and credit sale are within the Sale of Goods Act, hire-purchase is covered by analogous provisions
in the Supply of Goods (Implied Terms) Act 1973 and hire by provisions of Supply of Goods and Services Act 1982. For
consumers, these provisions have now been replaced by those of the Consumer Right Act 2015. Where goods are purchased
with the aid of credit covered by ss.75 and 75A of the Consumer Credit Act, the buyer will have identical rights against both
the seller and the creditor.
In general, the Acts of 1973, 1979, 1982 and 2015 imply, or more realistically in the case of consumers, compulsorily insert,
into the relevant contracts terms providing that:
 
1.the seller or owner has title to the goods and can pass it to the buyer or hirer;
 
2.the goods are reasonably fit for the purpose for which the buyer or hirer requires them, are of satisfactory quality and
conform to description and sample.
As will be seen under Section 25: Supply of Goods and Digital Content, the scope of the provisions as to fitness and quality
have been considerably enhanced by the Consumer Rights Act 2015 and the reader is referred to that Section for a detailed
discussion of the rights.
Liability of the creditor or owner under the CCA for faulty goods and
services
20-34
This is a fruitful source of litigation. At common law where there is the tripartite relationship involving the creditor, the hirer and
the supplier, it is often difficult to make the creditor liable for the misrepresentations and breaches of warranty of the supplier. 11
The CCA, however, gives the debtor or hirer considerable rights against the creditor. Section 56 applies to “debtor-creditor-
supplier agreements”. Although the definition of these agreements in s.12 is elaborate, in essence these are credit agreements
where the immediate supplier is the creditor himself (e.g. hire-purchase or conditional sale) or is someone with whom the
creditor has or intends to have an arrangement (either in respect of the individual transaction or a class of transactions). Section
56 provides that where, prior to the making of an agreement in this category, there are “antecedent negotiations” conducted by
a creditor, credit-broker or supplier, then any person conducting the negotiations is deemed to act as the agent of the creditor
or owner as well as on his own behalf. “Negotiations” is defined sufficiently widely as to encompass collateral agreements
between the supplier and the debtor or hirer. It is important to note that s.56 does not apply to hire agreements and there is no
equivalent provision for them in the CCA. The hirer is thus thrown back on the common law.
Sections 75 and 75A (the latter added by the Directive as from 1 February 2011 and essentially providing a like remedy to
s.75 but for transactions above £30,000, the cap for s.75, and £60,260, the cap for the Directive) apply to debtor-creditor-
supplier agreements where the supplier is not the creditor himself (mainly restricted use loans or agreements such as credit-token
agreements where the debtor can only use the token with suppliers who have a prior arrangement with the creditor). Thus it does
not apply to hire-purchase or conditional sale or to hire agreements. Note this section has extra-territorial effect: see para.20-17.

Under s.75 (and, in general, under s.75A) where the debtor has a claim for misrepresentation or breach of contract against the
supplier, the debtor has a “like claim” against the creditor, and the creditor and the supplier are jointly severally liable to the
debtor (as to the application in cases involving rescission of contract see Durkin v DSG Retail Ltd 12 ). The section does not
apply to non-commercial agreements or where the cash price of the goods or services is less than £100: s.75(3).
The creditor is entitled to an indemnity from the supplier against loss sustained by him in satisfying the debtor’s claim and the
reasonable costs of defending it: s.75(2). The creditor is entitled to have the supplier joined as a party to any action brought
by the debtor: s.75(5).
Hence the holder of a credit card who uses it to purchase goods or services within the financial limits may bring an action
for misrepresentation or breach of contract directly against the credit card company if the goods or services turn out to be
unsatisfactory.
