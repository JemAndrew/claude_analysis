Chapter 6 - Assignment of Contractual Rights
Contractual rights as choses in action
6-01
The law of assignment is concerned with the transfer of things, or choses, in action. A chose in action “describes all personal
rights of property which can only be claimed or enforced by action and not be taking physical possession”. 1  Because the rights
in a chose cannot be enforced by taking physical possession, the essence of a chose in action is that it is a right or interest in an
intangible. Choses in action stand in contra-distinction to choses in possession: rights in a chose in possession can be enforced
by taking physical possession. 2
6-02
The English law understanding of what is a chose in action is wide and diverse. Thus, it includes (for example) intellectual
property and shares. 3  In a number of cases, the manner in which these rights are enforced and transferred is very specific to the
chose, and their discussion lies beyond the scope of this work. 4  This chapter considers the transfer of rights under a contract,
including debts. 5
6-03
Choses in action can be either legal or equitable. Legal choses in action are those which, before the Supreme Court of the
Judicature Act 1873 came into operation, could be recovered or enforced by an action at law. Equitable choses are those which
were enforceable only by what was formerly called a suit in equity. 6  Rights under a contract, including debts, are instances
of legal choses in action. 7  Although the practical significance of the distinction between legal and equitable choses is much
reduced since the enactment of the Supreme Court of the Judicature Act 1873, the distinction does—unfortunately—still matter,
and (to the extent necessary for precedents of pleadings) is considered further below.
Methods of transfer of contractual rights
6-04
Contractual rights (including debts) can be transferred in a number of ways:
 
(a)By way of an equitable assignment.
 
(b)By way of an assignment under s.136 of the Law of Property Act 1925.
 
(c)By way of a promise to assign. 8
An interest in a contractual right can also be held or transferred by a declaration of trust. In such a case, a trustee holds the
contractual right and enforces it on behalf of the beneficiary. 9  Trusts are not considered further in this chapter. 10

Equitable assignments
6-05
In order to effect an assignment of a chose in action in equity:
 
(1)The assignor must have manifested an intention to transfer the chose.
 
(2)The thing being assigned must be a chose in action, in present existence, certain or capable of being ascertained.
 
(3)The identity of the assignee must be clear.
 
(4)The identity of the assignee must be clear. 11
6-06
One crucial question is whether the assignor must be a party to any action enforcing the chose and—if so—whether the assignor
ought to be claimant or defendant. Where an assignor transfers his interest in an equitable chose to an assignee, the assignee
can sue in his own name, and there is no substantive reason for joining the assignor, whether as claimant or defendant. 12
6-07
However, the outright transfer of a chose from assignor to assignee can only occur in the case of equitable choses. So far as
legal choses in action are concerned, like debts and rights under a contract, there is a barrier to the outright transfer of the chose,
because a legal chose in action cannot be transferred at common law (the common law does not recognise assignments) and
equity (following law) cannot effect such a transfer. As Meagher, Gummow & Lehane note, it is impossible to see how a person
not the legal owner of a chose can prosecute an action at common law seeking to enforce an equitable chose. 13  Indeed, if this
were not the case, it is difficult to see what purpose is served by s.136 of the Law of Property Act 1925.
6-08
More recent authorities are much more equivocal on the question of joinder of the assignor in the case of assignment of legal
choses, suggesting that this is generally desirable for procedural reasons and not for substantive reasons. 14  Whilst the more
recent authorities appear to suggest, albeit without analysing the question, 15  that the assignee alone can claim to enforce a
legal chose, undoubtedly the safest approach, as a matter of pleading practice, is to ensure that the assignor is joined (with the
assignee) as claimant in the action. 16  A less satisfactory alternative is to join the assignor as a defendant. See Precedent 6-C1.
Assignments under s.136 of the Law of Property Act 1925
6-09
Section 136 of the Law of Property Act 1925 entitles the assignee of a debt or other legal chose in action to recover it by
way of proceedings brought in his own name, provided the conditions of the action are met. 17  The purpose of s.136, and its
predecessor, s.25(6) of the Judicature Act 1873, was to dispense, in those cases where it served no purpose, with the equitable
rule which requires an assignee to join the assignor as a party when seeking to enforce his rights. 18
6-10
For an assignment to fall within s.136, the following conditions must be met:
 
(1)The assignment must be of a debt or other legal chose in action.
 
(2)The assignment must be absolute and not purport to be by way of charge only. An assignment will not be absolute where
it is conditional; where it is part of a debt; or where it is given by way of legal charge. 19
 
(3)The assignment must be in writing under the hand of the assignor.

(4)The debtor must be given express notice in writing of the assignment. 20  See Precedent 6-C2.
6-11
It is possible—rather than to assign a chose—to promise to assign a chose. At common law, such an agreement has no proprietary
implications. According to the common law, a contract operates in personam, and breach of a contractual promise to assign will
give rise only to the remedy of damages. In equity, however, the effect of such a contract is very different, for equity looks on
that as done which ought to be done. Thus, an agreement to assign is treated by equity as though it has been performed at the
time when it ought to have been performed, with the same consequences as if it had then been completely performed. 21
6-12
There are two cases where a promise to assign will come to the rescue of an assignment that would otherwise fail:
 
(1)A future chose in action is not existing property, and for that reason cannot be assigned. Any purported assignment of
the “chose” would have no subject-matter. But, provided there is an enforceable contract, equity will give effect to the
“assignment” of a future chose, causing it to vest in the assignee the moment it is received by the assignor. Equity treats
a purported assignment made for valuable consideration as a contract to assign in the future if and when the chose comes
into existence. 22
 
(2)An assignment may fail because the necessary requirements for the assignment have not been met and the “assignment”
is ineffective. In such a case, provided again there is an agreement to transfer, equity will step in to assist. 23  See Precedent
6-C1. 24
Defences to a claim brought by an assignee
6-13
The defences that a debtor may plead in response to a claim brought by an assignee may be considered under a number of heads:
 
(1)Defences arising because the assignee takes subject to equities.
 
(2)Defences arising because the assignment is ineffective due to the fact that the chose in question is incapable of
assignment.
 
(3)Defences arising due to deficiencies in the assignment itself.
The assignee takes subject to equities
6-14
Whether an assignment is an equitable one or by way of s.136, the assignee takes subject to equities. Equities fall into two
classes. First, there are equities which serve as defences to the assignee’s claim against the debtor and which relate directly to
the chose being assigned. Thus, were an assignee to make a claim for breach of contract against the debtor in respect of a right
assigned to him, the debtor would be able to take advantage of any defence that he could have advanced against the assignor. 25
6-15
Secondly, there are equities that are cross-claims between the debtor and the assignor, which the debtor is entitled to advance
as against the assignee in diminution or extinction of any claim that the assignee may have as against the debtor. Such cross-
claims relate not to the chose in action itself, but to the state of an account between the debtor and the assignor, which becomes
relevant as a defence by the debtor to the claim advanced against him by the assignee. 26

Such defences on the part of the debtor are pleaded in the debtor’s defence as if the claim were being brought by the assignor.
They are defences that the assignee takes subject to: they do not arise specifically because of the assignment.
Ineffective assignment because the chose in question is incapable of
assignment
6-17
The debtor may contend that the assignment is ineffective because the chose is incapable of assignment. This may be for a
variety reasons:
 
(1)The chose in action may be a “burden”, rather than a “benefit”. Burdens are generally not assignable, the limited
exception—so far as contractual rights are concerned—being that “conditional benefits”, i.e. benefits with “strings”
attached can be assigned. 27
 
(2)The chose may be unassignable on the grounds of public policy. For instance, social security benefits are unassignable,
as are the salaries and pensions of public officers payable to them for the purpose of keeping the dignity of their office
or to ensure the due discharge of their duties. 28
 
(3)Assignments of bare rights to litigate are contrary to public policy and prohibited by the rules against champerty and
maintenance. 29  What amounts to a “bare” right to litigate is a difficult question. It is clear that equity considers a debt to be a
form of property, and regards any action at law necessary to reduce the property into possession as merely an incident which
follows on from the assignment of the debt. 30  Such claims and assignments are unaffected by the rules against champerty
and maintenance. 31  Other rights under a contract can be affected by the rules against champerty and maintenance. The
assignment of a right under a contract will be regarded as a “bare” right to litigate—and, hence, unenforceable—where the
assignee has no “genuine commercial interest” in the transfer of the chose. 32
 
(4)The obligation may be a personal one, which for this reason cannot be assigned. 33
 
(5)A contractual prohibition of assignment may render a chose unassignable. 34  See Precedent 6-C3.
Deficient assignments
6-18
The debtor may contend that the assignment itself was deficient, and was thus ineffective to transfer the chose from assignor
to assignee. Here it is necessary to distinguish between the three different types of assignment considered in para.6-02, for the
necessary requirements for validity vary from type to type. Thus:
 
(1)Where the assignee is relying upon a promise to assign, unless that promise is supported by what equity regards as
consideration, equity will not lend its assistance. 35  Consideration is not, however, required for either an equitable or a
s.136 assignment.
 
(2)In the case of a s.136 assignment, the assignment will have to be by writing under the hand of the assignor and express
notice of the assignment will have to be given to the debtor. If these requirements are not met, then there can be no effective
s.136 assignment, although there may be an equitable assignment (or a promise to assign).
 
(3)It may be that the assignment insufficiently identifies the chose in question, in which case the assignment will fail
whatever its type. See Precedent 6-C3.
