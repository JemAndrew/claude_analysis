Introduction
11-01
A bond is an instrument by which one person (the obligor) binds himself to pay another person (the obligee) a specified sum
of sum of money immediately or at a fixed future date. Bonds may be divided into two principal categories:
 
(1)“simple” or “single” bonds 1  (creating no more than an obligation to pay a specified sum, without any condition);
 
(2)“double” or “conditional” bonds, which may be subdivided into “common money bonds” (given solely to secure the
payment of money) and “bonds with special conditions” (conditional other than upon the payment of money).
However, in modern times, the term “bond” has become used to encompass any form of instrument which embodies a promise to
pay money. For example, in commercial and building transactions, use is frequently made of documents which provide security
for the satisfactory performance of the relevant contract (usually referred to as “performance bonds”). In fact, such bonds are
often in the form of a conditional guarantee, rather than a bond in its true sense. 2
The traditional structure of a conditional bond is to set out the obligatory part (the promise to pay a sum of money), then the
recitals (for example setting out the contracts the performance of which is to be secured) and finally the conditions (which
contain the underlying nature of the transaction). A claim on a bond will generally set out the essential nature of the bond, the
breaches or non-performance of the condition giving rise to liability, and the sum claimed.
It should be emphasised that the use of conditional bonds has been strongly discouraged by both conveyancers and the courts,
the criticism being that the form of the bond serves to obfuscate the true purpose of the document and the true nature of the
underlying obligations. 3  In most situations, it is now preferable for parties simply to enter into a guarantee or other contractual
arrangement whereby the true nature, extent and terms of the obligation undertaken by the obligor to the obligee are clearly
set out.
Bonds are to be construed in accordance with commercial common sense, and in particular in accordance with the commercial
purpose of the instrument (e.g. to protect the payer against the insolvency of the payee). 4
Actions on common money bonds
11-02
A common money bond is a bond given to secure the payment of a sum of money under penalty (typically twice the sum
advanced). Thus, if A borrows £5,000 from B to be secured by A’s bond, A would, on the face of the bond, acknowledge that
he owed B £10,000; but a condition would be added that if A paid £5,000 on a specified day, together with interest, the bond
should be void. At common law, the whole penalty was recoverable. The courts of equity, however, granted relief upon the
payment of the amount really due. The power to grant relief in certain cases was subsequently given to the courts of law by
the Administration of Justice Act 1705. By s.12 of that Act it is a good defence to an action on the bond that the obligor has,
before the action, paid to the obligee the principal and interest due by the condition together with costs. It is proper, then, to

Actions on bonds with special conditions
11-03
Where a bond is conditioned for the performance of an act other than the payment of money, the question is whether the sum
named in the obligatory part of the bond is deemed to be a penalty (which is irrecoverable) or liquidated damages (which are
recoverable). The law on penalties was revised in ParkingEye Ltd v Beavis: 6  the modern test is whether the obligee had a
legitimate interest in ensuring performance by the obligor and the sum named in the obligatory part of the bond is not extravagant
or unconscionable in comparison to that interest. 7
Payment and discharge of a bond
11-04
The clearest defence to a claim on a bond is that the sum owed by the obligor under the bond has been paid. Where the defendant
has, prior to the claim, offered to pay the sum due, the defence of “tender before claim” should be pleaded; where this defence
is relied upon the defendant must make a payment into court of the amount he says was tendered 8  (and the usual restriction
upon the disclosure of a Part 36 payment does not apply 9 ).
A bond may be discharged by agreement, repudiatory breach on the part of the claimant, frustration, material alteration, 10
or cancellation. Both a material alteration 11  and a cancellation 12  must be intentional, although in the case of the former the
alteration need not have been made by the obligor: the bond is discharged even if the alteration is made by a stranger whilst
the bill is in the custody of the obligee. 13
Restraining payment on a performance bond
11-05
In certain limited circumstances, the applicant for a performance bond may apply for an injunction restraining the beneficiary
from making a claim upon or (if such a claim has already been made) restraining the obligor (i.e. the paying bank) from making
payment under the bond. 14
The most common situation is where the applicant seeks to invoke the so-called “fraud exception”. In this situation, the applicant
must establish a clear case of fraud to the knowledge of the party he is seeking to injunct: this will involve establishing that (i)
the beneficiary has no right to payment under the bond; (ii) the beneficiary has no genuine belief in such right 15 ; and (iii) (in
the case of an injunction against the bank) the bank must have notice of the fraud. These are very difficult hurdles to overcome
and cases where such injunction applications have succeeded are rare. Where the application is being made against a bank the
applicant faces a further difficulty, since in such a case damages will almost always be an adequate remedy for any breach of
duty by the bank. 16
In addition to the fraud exception, there may occasionally be situations in which the contractual provisions between the applicant
and beneficiary impose restrictions or prevent calls being made on the bond. It has been held that the autonomy principle is not
undermined in the very special case where a party has expressly agreed not to draw down unless certain conditions were met:

to seek to draw down in breach of those terms would be a breach of an express negative covenant which could be restrained

The only other situation in which a beneficiary can be restrained from making a claim (or the bank restrained from making
payment) under a performance bond is where there has not been strict compliance with the terms of the instrument. For example,
the bond may stipulate that any demand upon it must be made within a specified period of time, or must be preceded by a
letter requiring the applicant to remedy a breach of an underlying sale contract. If such formalities have not been adhered to,
then payment will not be due under the bond according to its own terms, and hence a threatened claim or payment could be
restrained by the court.
