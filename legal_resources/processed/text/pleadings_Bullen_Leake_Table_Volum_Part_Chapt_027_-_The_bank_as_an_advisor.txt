The bank as an advisor
9-20
Banks have notoriously faced a large number of claims for the alleged “mis-selling” of financial products. However, “mis-
selling” is not a legal cause of action, and it is necessary in each case to identify the applicable causes of action, which typically
might include (a) breach of contract, (b) negligent misrepresentation, (c) negligent advice, and/or (d) breach of a statutory
duty arising from a failure to comply with applicable regulatory rules. 115  It is possible that the particular banker/customer
relationship may also give rise to a potential breach of fiduciary duty claim, but such claims are unlikely to be successful save
in rare circumstances. What follows is a brief outline of some of the more common causes of action that arise in this context.
Negligent advice
9-21
There is no general duty upon a bank to advise as to the nature or effect of a proposed financial arrangement. In Property
Alliance Group v Royal Bank of Scotland Plc, 116  the Court of Appeal stated the general principle to be as follows:
“The starting point is … that a bank negotiating and contracting with another party owes in the first instance no
duty to explain the nature or effect of the proposed arrangement to that other party.”
The Court of Appeal went on to state that if a bank does give an explanation or tender advice, then it may owe a duty of care in
doing so, but how far that duty goes must depend on the precise nature of the circumstances and of the explanation or advice
which is tendered. For example, in JP Morgan Chase Group v Springwell Navigation Corp, 117  it was held that the bank owed
no duty of care where the customers were sophisticated investors who had dealt with the bank on a non-advised basis, did not
seek to rely on advice, and took their own decisions as to whether to invest.
It has been held that such a duty of care, even where established, would not ordinarily extend beyond those products which the
bank has chosen to sell to its customer; there is no duty to go further and explain other products which could be relevant but
which it has chosen not to offer (still less, to advise on other products that may be available from competitors in the market). 118
Breach of contract
9-22
The duty to advise may arise as a matter of contract. If the bank gives advice pursuant to a specific contractual obligation to do
so, then there will be an implied term by virtue of s.13 of the Supply of Goods and Services Act that the service will be carried
out with reasonable skill and care. For contracts concluded on or after 1 October 2015, an equivalent term will be implied under
the Consumer Rights Act 2015. Exceptionally, the contract itself may be implied: in Rubenstein v HSBC 119  ostensibly extra-
contractual advice given prior to a contract to trade was held to result in a collateral contract to provide that advice.

Negligent misrepresentation
9-23
If the bank provides information about a transaction or product which includes a false statement, then it may face liability for
negligent misstatement (or deceit, if the bank knew that the statement was false). Further, a statement of opinion may carry
with it an implied statement to the effect that the representor has reasonable grounds for holding that opinion. 120  However, the
Court of Appeal in Springwell 121  said that there may be certain opinions which, in the circumstances, are not accompanied
by such an implied representation, the existence of which will depend on the context of the communication and the parties’
respective positions, knowledge and experience.
Disclaimers
9-24
A duty to advise (whether contractual or tortious) can in principle be excluded by relevant contractual clauses in the bank’s
documentation. There have been a number of cases in which express contractual disclaimers of any advice having been given
have been found to negate the existence of a duty of care as a matter of contractual estoppel. 122  Such disclaimers are commonly
founded in so-called “basis clauses” (i.e. clauses that set out the basis upon which the bank has agreed to engage with its
customer, typically as being non-advisory). However, in First Tower Trustees Ltd v Superstores International Ltd, 123  the Court
of Appeal said that basis clauses fall within s.3 of the Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977, and hence must satisfy the requirement
of reasonableness within s.11 of that Act; although this was not a banking case (it concerned replies to enquiries in connection
with leases), the principle expounded by the Court of Appeal may be wide enough to apply to non-advisory “basis clauses”
in the banking context.
Fiduciary duty
9-25
A bank does not become a fiduciary of its customer merely by giving advice. 124  The assertion of a fiduciary relationship has
been widely argued in the “mis-selling” context, but has not been established, 125  and is generally inconsistent with the “arms-
length” relationship that exists between a bank and its customer; it should not be pleaded unless there exceptional circumstances
giving rise to a relationship of trust and confidence such that the bank is obliged to subjugate its own interests to those of its
customer.
Regulatory duties
9-26
Banks providing advisory services in relation to financial products must be authorised to do so by the Financial Conduct
Authority (FCA). An authorised bank providing such advice is subject to the conduct rules contained in the FCA Handbook.
Different parts of the Handbook deal with different types of product: the Conduct of Business Sourcebook (COBS) deals with
investments; the Insurance Code of Business Sourcebook (ICOBS) deals with non-investment insurance; the Mortgages and
Home Finance Conduct of Business sourcebook (MCOB) deals with regulated mortgages.

A breach of any of these rules is actionable (subject to the usual defences available in respect of a claim for breach of statutory
duty) under s.138D of the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA), but only by a “private person”. 126  Claimants who
are not “private persons” have nevertheless sought to plead duties under FCA rules on the basis that they “inform” the bank’s
common law duties, with mixed success. It has been held that the COBS rules are not to be implied into the banker/customer
relationship (although they may in theory be incorporated expressly), and that FSMA sets out a self-contained regulatory code,
such that it would be unnecessary and inappropriate for the courts to seek to fortify this through the creation of parallel common
law duties and remedies. 127  On the other hand, the courts have also found that in certain circumstances reference may be made
to the COBS rules in determining the applicable standard of care in tort, on the basis that the skill and care of a competent
professional ordinarily includes compliance with the relevant regulatory rules. 128  The Court of Appeal in Green v RBS 129  held
that the statutory duty owed by a bank to its customer is not co-extensive with the parallel common law duty of care; although
the ambit of the common law duty may be informed by certain of the COBS rules, the regulatory rules impose obligations
greater than those imposed under the common law.
