12-03
Under FSMA s.22, an activity is a regulated activity if it is an activity of a specified kind which is carried on by way of business
(or in the case of (iii), carried on by way of business in Great Britain) and:
 
(i)relates to investment of a specified kind (or in specified cases, is carried on in relation to property of any kind) 11 ; or
 
(ii)relates to information about a person’s financial standing, the setting of a specified benchmark or administering a
benchmark 12 ; or
 
(iii)is, or relates to, claims management services. 13
The 2012 Act s.7 expanded the scope of s.22 to cover activities to be specified which are carried on by way of business and which
relate to the financial standing of a person (i.e. credit-rating agencies) and the setting of a specified benchmark (e.g. the fixing of
the London Interbank Offered Rate (LIBOR)). The Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Benchmarks) Regulations 2018
(SI 2018/135) reg.38 further amended FSMA s.22 to add “administering a benchmark.” FSMA s.22 was further extended by
the Financial Guidance and Claims Act 2018 s.27 to cover activities which are or relate to claims management services.
An activity is of a “specified kind” if it is specified by an order made by Treasury. Sch.2 to FSMA supplements s.22. Parts I,
IA, IIA and IIB of the schedule contain an indicative list of “regulated activities”, described in general terms. However, FSMA
s.22(3) makes clear that nothing in Sch.2 limits the power of the Treasury to specify activities.
The order by which the Treasury specifies regulated activities is the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Regulated
Activities) Order 2001 (SI 2001/544) (the RAO). Since it first came into force on 1 December 2001, the RAO has been the subject
of many amendments and will continue to be amended as necessary. Some of the amendments have been for the purpose of giving
effect to EU legislation which required the regulation of certain financial services activities (for example, Directive 2004/39/
EC 14  on markets in financial instruments [2004] OJ L145/1, which was subsequently repealed and replaced by Directive
2014/65/EU on markets in financial instruments [2014] OJ L173/349). Other amendments reflect UK domestic developments
which result in the introduction of new regulated activities (for example, the decision to transfer responsibility for the regulation
of consumer credit from the Office of Fair Trading to the FCA resulted in new credit activities, such as entering into regulated
credit agreements and credit broking, being specified 15 ). The activities specified in the RAO at the time of writing are listed
in summary form below. Reference should be made to the RAO 16  for the precise and detailed description of the regulated
activities and exclusions.
 
(i)accepting deposits 17 ;
 
(ii)issuing electronic money 18 ;
 
(iii)investment activities which are dealing in investments as principal 19  or agent, 20  arranging deals in investments, 21
managing investments, 22  advising on investments, 23  safeguarding and administering investments, 24  operating a
multilateral trading facility 25  or an organised trading facility, 26  sending dematerialised instructions, 27  managing 28  or
acting as a trustee or depository 29  of a UCITS, 30  managing 31  or acting as a trustee or depository 32  of an alternative
investment fund, establishing, operating or winding up a collective investment scheme 33 ;

Pursuant to FSMA s.22A, the Treasury has designated certain of the above regulated activities as PRA-regulated activities. 78
The designated activities include accepting deposits and effecting or carrying out a contract of insurance as principal. Firms
with permission to carry on a PRA-regulated activity (PRA-authorised firms) will be regulated by both the PRA and the FCA.
All other firms (FCA-authorised firms) are regulated by the FCA only. The distinction between PRA-authorised firms and FCA-
authorised firms is not important for the purposes of the proceedings covered in this title.
For an activity to be a regulated activity, it must be “carried on by way of business” (FSMA s.22). This is referred to in this title
as the “business element”. The business element is not defined in FSMA or in the RAO. The FCA has given guidance in Ch.2.3
of its Perimeter Guidance Manual (PERG). The guidance states that whether or not an activity is carried on by way of business
is ultimately a question of judgement that takes account of several factors (none of which is likely to be conclusive). The factors
include the degree of continuity, the existence of a commercial element, the scale of the activity and the proportion which the
activity bears to other activities carried on by the same person but which are not regulated. The nature of the particular regulated
activity that is carried on will also be relevant to the factual analysis. 79
The Treasury has power under FSMA s.419 to change the meaning of the business element by including or excluding certain
activities. The Treasury has exercised this power through the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Carrying on Regulated
Activities by Way of Business) Order 2001 (SI 2001/1177), as amended, from time to time. The effect is that the business
element differs depending on the regulated activity in question.
