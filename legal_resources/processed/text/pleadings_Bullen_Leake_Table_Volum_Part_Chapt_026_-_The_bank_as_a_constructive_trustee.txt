The bank as a constructive trustee
9-16
A bank is not ordinarily a trustee of the moneys in its customer’s account. 91  Even where the account is a “trust account”, the
trustee is not the bank but the account holder, which holds assets on behalf of the beneficiaries of the trust. The strict legal
position is that the bank is the absolute owner (in law and equity) of the money in a deposit account: when the customer pays
money into his account, his right to the money is replaced by a cause of action (i.e. a debt claim) exercisable against the bank. 92
Where the account is a “trust account”, the trustee is not the bank but the account holder, which holds the asset (i.e. the cause
of action in debt) on behalf of the beneficiaries of the trust. 93
However, in relatively rare cases, a bank may become liable as a constructive trustee if either (i) it knowingly receives and
becomes chargeable with trust property (“knowing receipt”), or (ii) it dishonestly assists in a breach of trust (“dishonest
assistance”). 94
Knowing receipt
9-17
The elements of a knowing receipt claim are as follows 95 :
 
(1)the existence of a trust or fiduciary relationship;
 
(2)a disposal of the claimant’s assets in breach of fiduciary duty;
 
(3)the beneficial receipt of assets which are traceable as representing the claimant’s assets;
 
(4)knowledge on the part of the bank that the assets it has received are traceable to a breach of fiduciary duty.
“Beneficial receipt” is established only where the bank receives the moneys for its own use and benefit. There is conflicting
authority on the question of whether “beneficial receipt” is established where the bank uses the money to reduce or discharge
the customer’s overdraft. 96
Normally, however, a collecting bank receives money not for its own benefit, but as agent for the customer: this will be the case
when there is a payment into an account in credit in the ordinary course.
As regards “knowledge”, it has been held that “the recipient’s state of knowledge must be such as to make it unconscionable
for him to retain the benefit of the receipt”. 97  The degree of knowledge required to make the recipient bank’s conduct
unconscionable depends on the context, but in general it must be shown that the recipient knows enough about the facts
surrounding the misapplication of the trust property to make it unconscionable for him to retain the payment. 98
If a bank, without notice of a breach of trust, gives good value for the receipt of funds, it has the defence of “bona fide purchaser
without notice”: that operates as a complete defence, and is not limited to the value paid out since the court does not inquire
as to the adequacy of the consideration. 99

A defence of “ministerial receipt” may also be available: in essence, the defence is that the collecting bank has received money
on its customer’s behalf (i.e. as an agent) and has accounted for it to its customer. 100  In modern terms, such a situation is
best seen as one in which an essential element of the cause of action (“receipt” in the context of a knowing receipt claim, and
“enrichment” in the context of a restitutionary claim) is simply missing (the bank not having received anything for its own
account or in its own right), rather than being a defence per se. 101  On either basis, the bank has no liability because the receipt
of money is matched by an immediate balancing liability to the bank’s customer. 102
The “defence” is said 103  to be subject to certain exceptions, namely that the bank will not be exonerated if: (a) the bank
had notice of the claimant’s claim before paying the money to (or to the order of) the principal; (b) the bank had been acting
as principal in the transaction (for example, by acting for an undisclosed principal); or (c) the bank received the money in
consequence of some wrongdoing to which it was a party or of which it had knowledge, or is otherwise on notice of the unlawful
provenance of the funds. 104  However, on the facts of the cases said to support these propositions, 105  the bank was in fact
acting as principal, and these apparent “exceptions” must be treated with caution in the light of Royal Brunei v Tan 106 : the
“exceptions” are better seen as situations where the bank was the principal in the transaction or where the real basis of the
bank’s liability was knowing receipt or dishonest assistance; the older cases should not now be seen as giving rise to a different
or wider basis of liability than that established in Tan.
Dishonest assistance
9-19
The elements of a dishonest assistance claim are as follows 107 :
 
(1)the existence of a trust or fiduciary relationship 108 ;
 
(2)a breach of trust on the part of the fiduciary (although it is not necessary to establish dishonesty on the part of the trustee
or fiduciary; indeed, his breach may be entirely innocent) 109 ;
 
(3)the bank was an accessory to, or assisted in, the breach of trust or fiduciary duty;
 
(4)dishonesty on the part of the bank.
The test of “dishonesty” is whether the bank was dishonest by the normally acceptable standards of honest bankers. In Barlow
Clowes International Ltd v Eurotrust International Ltd, 110  Lord Hoffmann explained the test as follows:
“Although a dishonest state of mind is a mental state, the standard by which the law determines whether it is
dishonest is objective. If by ordinary standards a defendant’s mental state would be characterised as dishonest, it
is irrelevant that the defendant judges by different standards.”
This objective test is now the universal test of dishonesty, applicable in both the civil and criminal law, following the decision
of the Supreme Court in Ivey v Genting Casinos UK Ltd. 111
Particular care must be taken by the pleader to ensure that there is proper material to support a plea of dishonesty. 112  The
facts pleaded in support of the plea, however, may properly be a combination of facts known to the defendant, the defendant’s
suspicions, and instances of conscious decisions by the defendant not to make enquiries. If, for example, a bank manager, having
suspicions, fails to ask obvious questions for fear of what he might learn, then that might be held to be conscious impropriety
amounting to dishonesty, for which the bank may be vicariously liable. 113  However, the courts will be wary of too readily
drawing a conclusion of dishonesty: there have been a number of cases in which a failure to make inquiries that ought to have
been made with the benefit of hindsight, taken in the context of the busy day-to-day activities of a bank manager, did not imply
dishonest behaviour. 114
