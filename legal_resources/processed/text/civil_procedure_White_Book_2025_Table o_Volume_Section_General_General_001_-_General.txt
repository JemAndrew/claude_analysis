Section 5 - International Jurisdiction and Enforcement of Judgments

General
Civil Jurisdiction and Judgments Act 1982
5-1
When by the Accession Convention signed at Luxemburg on 9 October 1978, the United Kingdom, together with Denmark
and Ireland, joined the European Community, it undertook to accede to any Conventions provided for by art.220 of the Treaty
of Rome, including the Convention on Jurisdiction and Enforcement of Judgments in Civil and Commercial Matters signed
at Brussels on 23 September 1968, and to enter into negotiations with the original Member States “in order to make the
necessary adjustments thereto”. The 1968 Brussels Convention, as amended following negotiations, was implemented in the
UK by the Civil Jurisdiction and Judgments Act 1982. Subsequently the 1968 Convention was superseded, in 2001, by the
Judgments Regulation (sometimes referred to as “the Brussels I Regulation”), which was in turn superseded, in 2012, by the
Judgments Regulation (recast). In 1988 the Member States of the European Community and certain EFTA States concluded the
Lugano Convention, a Convention having similar effects to the 1968 Convention, which was in turn superseded by the Lugano
Convention 2007. Changes to UK legislation made necessary by these developments, and by the accession to the EU by new
Member States, were made to the 1982 Act as and when required, in particular by the Civil Jurisdiction and Judgments Act
1991, the Civil Jurisdiction and Judgments Order 2001 (SI 2001/3929), and the Civil Jurisdiction and Judgments (Amendment)
Regulations 2014 (SI 2014/2947).
See Vol.2 of the 2019 edition of Civil Procedure paras 5-1 to 5-6 for discussion of the structure of the pre-Brexit 1982 Act, the
Brussels and Lugano Conventions, the Brussels I Regulation and the Judgments Regulation.
The Civil Jurisdiction and Judgments (Amendment) (EU Exit)
Regulations 2019
5-2
The Civil Jurisdiction and Judgments (Amendment) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019 (SI 2019/479), as amended by the Civil,
Criminal and Family Justice (Amendment) (EU Exit) Regulations 2020 (SI 2020/1493), effected substantial changes to the
1982 Act on 31 December 2020.
Part 5 of the 2019 Regulations (as amended) revoked, amongst other things: Regulation (EC) No.44/2001 on jurisdiction and
the recognition and enforcement of judgments in civil and commercial matters; Regulation (EU) No.1215/2012 on jurisdiction
and the recognition and enforcement of judgments in civil and commercial matters (recast); and Council Decision of 15 October
2007 (2007/712/EC) on the signing, on behalf of the Community, of the Convention on jurisdiction and the recognition and
enforcement of judgments in civil and commercial matters (i.e. the 2007 Lugano Convention).
Parts 2 and 3 of the 2019 Regulations (as amended) further effected substantial amendments to the 1982 Act and subordinate
legislation. In particular, Pt 2 deleted references within the 1982 Act to the Brussels and Lugano Conventions and the Judgments
Regulation (recast). Further consequential amendments were also effected to: the Civil Jurisdiction and Judgments Act 1982
(Interim Relief) Order 1997; the Civil Jurisdiction and Judgments Act 1982 (Gibraltar) Order 1997; the Civil Jurisdiction and
Judgments (Authentic Instruments and Court Settlements) Order 2001; and the Civil Jurisdiction and Judgments Order 2001.

The 2005 Hague Convention
5-3
On 1 October 2015 the Hague Choice of Court Convention 2005 (the “2005 Hague Convention”) entered into force for the
then Member States of the European Union, including the UK (but excluding Denmark, which later became a member from
1 September 2018). During the Brexit implementation period (following the UK’s exit from the EU on 31 January 2020 and
ending on 31 December 2020) the UK continued to be treated as an EU member state for these purposes (pursuant to art.129
of the UK/EU Withdrawal Agreement).
The UK then acceded to the Convention in its own right as from 1 January 2021. Section 1(2) of the Private International
Law (Implementation of Agreements) Act 2020 provides (by introducing a new s.3D to the 1982 Act) that the 2005 Hague
Convention shall have the force of law in the UK from 31 December 2020.
Unlike the Judgments Regulation (recast), the 2005 Hague Convention only applies where there is an exclusive choice of court
agreement in favour of a state which is party to the Convention and which was entered into after the Convention came into
force for the chosen state. Paragraph 7 of Sch.5 to the 2020 Act provides that, for the purposes of the 2005 Hague Convention
as it has the force of law in the UK, the date of its entry into force for the UK is 1 October 2015. The relevant dates for the
other contracting states are: 1 October 2015 (Mexico and the EU apart from Denmark); 1 October 2016 (Singapore); 1 August
2018 (Montenegro); and 1 September 2018 (Denmark). According to the Hartley/Dogauchi Explanatory Report on the 2005
Hague Convention (paras 105-106) asymmetric or hybrid jurisdiction clauses are not exclusive choice of court agreements for
the purposes of the Convention. See further Vol.1 para.6.33.3.
The 2019 Hague Convention
5-4
The 2019 Hague Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Judgments in Civil and Commercial Matters sets
out common rules for the recognition and enforcement of foreign judgments in civil and commercial cases between signatory
states of the Convention. When it enters into force in the UK, it will provide a simplified procedure for the enforcement of
judgments obtained in other contracting countries where no exclusive jurisdiction clause was entered into. It was ratified by the
UK on 27 June 2024 and will come into force for the UK on 1 July 2025. It will then apply to judgments in proceedings started
after that date. Currently, the convention is in force between the EU (apart from Denmark) and Ukraine only (and will enter
into force for Uruguay on 1 October 2024). The other countries that have signed but not yet ratified the convention are: Israel,
the US, Costa Rica and Russia. See para.5-240 for the full text of the 2019 Hague Convention.
