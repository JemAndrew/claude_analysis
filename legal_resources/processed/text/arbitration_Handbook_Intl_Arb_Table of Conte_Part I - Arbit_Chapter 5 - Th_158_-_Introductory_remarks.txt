Introductory remarks
29-1
Article 29 was one of the novel provisions of the 2012 Rules. Article 29 corresponds to similar provisions adopted with
respect to a number of international arbitration rules. Article 29 permits parties to an ICC arbitration agreement, under certain
circumstances, to obtain provisional relief from a so-called Emergency Arbitrator prior to the transmission of the file to
the Tribunal. 535  Article 29 is not applicable to arbitration agreements entered into prior to 1 January 2012. For arbitration
agreements providing for ICC arbitration that are entered into after 1 January 2012, art.29 is applicable unless its application has
been excluded. As discussed under art.28(2) and 29(7), the Emergency Arbitrator provisions are an alternative to seeking interim
relief from state courts. There appears to be considerable support for having Emergency Arbitrator provisions in arbitration
rules, although many parties appear to view recourse to state courts as adequate and in some cases preferable due to issues of
enforceability. 536  The ICC has summarized the use of Emergency Arbitrator Provisions as follows 537 :
 
“During the course of 2020, 32 Emergency Arbitrator (‘EA’) applications were filed, involving parties of 33
nationalities. Of these applications, 14 involved multiple parties (with as many as 12 responding parties in
one domestic dispute), and seven involved states or state entities in commercial disputes.  Of the 27 EA orders
rendered in 2020, the requested relief was dismissed in 14 cases granted in five cases, and partially granted
in six cases; one EA proceeding resulted in an order by consent and another in a ‘termination order’.  Since
their introduction in 2012 until 1 March 2020, the ICC Emergency Arbitrator Rules have enabled parties to
apply 120 times for ‘Emergency Measures’ and receive a decision within a 15-day time limit and prior to the
constitution of an arbitral tribunal (Article 29 and Appendix V to the Arbitration Rules).
 
While half of the EA applications relate to the construction/engineering and energy sectors, other disputes
originated from the chemical industry, general trade and distribution, health and pharmaceuticals, industrial
equipment, telecommunications/specialised technologies, transportation, and leisure and entertainment.”
29-2
Article 29 provides for the granting of interim and conservatory relief of the type provided for in art.28 by an Emergency
Arbitrator if such relief cannot await the constitution of the Tribunal. Article 29 does not contemplate or allow any kind
of fast-track or summary proceedings on the merits. The Emergency Arbitration proceedings will normally precede arbitral
proceedings, but will not substitute such proceedings. 538  Article 29 does not preclude recourse to national courts under art.28(2)
in particular. In many circumstances, parties may prefer to apply to national courts for relief and not to use art.29. This is
particularly the case where the national court in the relevant jurisdiction is open to applications for interim relief and where the
court order for relief can be directly enforced, and/or where the type of provisional relief is reserved to the jurisdiction of the
state courts, such as, for instance, attachment orders. However, as is discussed below under art.29(7), parties should be aware
that the existence of the Emergency Arbitrator provisions may affect the scope of possible relief in state courts.
29-3
When art.29 was introduced in the 2012 Rules, the ICC dispute resolution system was familiar with the possibility of providing
parties with provisional relief prior to the constitution of a Tribunal. Since 1990, by agreeing to the ICC Pre-Arbitral Referee
Rules, parties were able to obtain a decision from a pre-arbitral referee. 539  The major defect of the ICC Pre-Arbitral Referee
Rules was the need to have the parties separately agree on them and parties rarely did so. During the last 25 years there have
been only 10 cases administered by the ICC under its ICC Pre-Arbitral Referee Rules.

29-4
The impossibility of obtaining provisional relief under the Rules prior to the constitution of a Tribunal was considered by the
Task Force to be a serious gap of the 1998 version of the Rules. However, the Task Force considered that the only way to remedy
that gap was to provide that the Emergency Arbitrator Provisions are applicable generally if the parties have agreed to ICC
arbitration after 1 January 2012. For arbitration agreements entered into prior to that date, at least to the extent that they do not
expressly refer to the current version of the ICC Rules, there is an issue as to whether the parties could have anticipated and
therefore could be considered to have agreed to the Emergency Arbitrator Provisions.
29-5
Article 29 allows a party to apply for relief from an Emergency Arbitrator, but at a cost of US$40,000 payable up-front by the
applicant. This covers the ICC administrative costs and the fees and expenses of the Emergency Arbitrator. The lump sum fee
can, in some circumstances, be increased, but will never be decreased; it applies irrespective of the amount in dispute. 540  In
addition, the parties will incur costs for legal fees in connection with the Emergency Arbitrator Proceedings and the unsuccessful
party may be liable for all or a portion of the costs of the other party.
29-6
Article 29(1)–(7) is the cornerstone provision in the Rules for the new Emergency Arbitrator mechanism. It needs to be read
in conjunction with App.V of the Rules, which is entitled “Emergency Arbitrator Rules”. For ease of reference the Emergency
Arbitrator Rules together with art.29(1)–(4) of the Rules are referred to as the “Emergency Arbitrator Provisions”. These new
provisions are quite elaborate and mirror the comparable provisions in the Rules to a large extent, without, however, referring
to them. In that sense, the Emergency Arbitrator Provisions set up a standalone regime for Emergency Measures. In addition, as
discussed below Part V of the 2021 ICC Note provides a summary of the ICC’s practice with respect to Emergency Arbitrators.
29-7
Since their introduction in 2012, there have been until the end of 2020, 154 applications received for relief from an ICC
Emergency Arbitrator. In 2020, the ICC received 32 applications under art.29. 541  The experience of other arbitration institutions
also suggests that there is a role for provisions such as the Emergency Arbitrator Provisions. However, in deciding whether
to bring Emergency Arbitrator Proceedings, parties are well advised to keep in mind one of the key conclusions from
Emergency Arbitrator Proceedings: Report of the ICC Commission on Arbitration and ADR Task Force on Emergency
Arbitrator Proceedings (the “Commission Report on Emergency Arbitration”) 542 :
“A key finding based on the cases analysed in this Report is that relief has been granted only in a minority of ICC
EA Applications … It appears from the analysed cases that EAs are minded to strictly apply particular threshold
requirements set by the EA Provisions, such as the key requirement that relief “cannot await the constitution of
an arbitral tribunal” (Article 29(1)). Yet, EAs have in multiple cases been persuaded to grant interim relief and
the EA Provisions are thus an important addition to the ICC Rules, filling a previously existing void.”
