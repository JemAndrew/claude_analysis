Pleadings points for a mortgage claim
General
15-05
Note that the claimant cannot get judgment in default in relation to possession proceedings, and cannot obtain summary judgment
for possession of residential property. 6
Ensure that the mortgage claim is brought in respect of all debts, accounts, agreements and/or liabilities secured by the mortgage
or charge to avoid the kinds of problems which occurred in Lloyds Bank v Hawkins, 7  where it was held that a subsequent action
on a guarantee was precluded by an earlier judgment on a claim on an all monies charge. See also, to similar effect, Commercial
First Business Ltd v Munday. 8
It is now clear that the limitation period is six years in respect of interest and 12 years in respect of capital, in each case after
the right to receive the money accrued. 9  However, a mortgagee may be able to retain time-barred interest in accounting for the
proceeds of sale of mortgaged property—this is an aspect of the principle that limitation bars the remedy and not the substantive
right. See the discussion in Fisher and Lightwood’s Law of Mortgage, 15th edn at para.26.36.
Section 29 of the Limitation Act 1980 may be relevant in fixing the date on which a right of action is treated as having accrued.
If the mortgagee makes any payment in respect of the debt or acknowledges the claim, the mortgagor’s right of action is treated
as having accrued on and not before the date of payment or date of acknowledgement respectively. 10  See Bradford & Bingley
Building Society v Rashid 11  where the House of Lords held that an acknowledgement of a debt was admissible since it was
not made in without prejudice correspondence (since the communication was apparently open and the only question was how
an admitted debt was to be paid).
As to whether payments should be credited against repayment of interest or capital see West Bromwich BS v Crammer. 12
Failure to file or serve a defence does not prevent the defendant from taking part in the proceedings, although it may be taken
into account when deciding what costs order to make: see CPR r.55.7(3).
The Mortgage Pre-Action Protocol
15-06
There is a pre-action protocol for possession claims based on residential mortgage arrears, the most recent amendments having
come into effect on 30 January 2017. Although it does not contain any specific sanctions, it states “This Protocol describes
the behaviour the court will normally expect of the parties prior to the start of a possession claim…” (para.2.1). Form N123
is a checklist of the requirements of the Pre-Action Protocol, and by PD 55A para.5.5, the claimant must bring two completed
copies of Form N123 to the possession hearing.

Where to bring the claim for possession
15-07
The basic rule is that possession claims must be started in the county court hearing centre which serves the address where the
property is situated. However, a claim may be started in the High Court if there are exceptional circumstances (see PD 55A
paras 1.1–1.4). The value of the property and the amount of the financial claim may be relevant circumstances, but they alone
will not normally justify starting the claim in the High Court. High Court claims for the possession of land subject to a mortgage
will be assigned to the Chancery Division: see the Chancery Guide 2016 at 29.2.
The claim form
15-08
The claim form must be in prescribed form PD 55A para.1.5 and must be verified by a statement of truth. If proceedings are
issued in the High Court, the claim form must be accompanied by a certificate stating the reasons for bringing the claim in the
High Court, verified by a statement of truth (CPR 55.3(2)).
Particulars of claim
15-09
Particulars of claim must be filed and served with the claim form. All particulars of claim must (PD 55A para 2.1):
 
(1)comply with CPR Pt 16. This means that they must contain a concise statement of facts and, if interest is sought, give
details;
 
(2)identify the land to which the claim relates;
 
(3)state whether it is residential property;
 
(4)state the ground on which possession is claimed;
 
(5)give full details of any mortgage or tenancy agreement;
 
(6)give details of every person who, to the best of the claimant’s knowledge, is in possession of the property; and
 
(7)include a copy of any written agreement upon which the claim is based PD 16 para.7.3(1).
Additionally, for a two-year period beginning on 1 January 2019, where claims are commenced in the Business and Property
Courts, it will be necessary to consider the Disclosure Pilot Scheme under PD 51U.
Proceedings against mortgagors
15-10
The additional requirements for particulars of claim where they relate to a mortgage are set out in PD 55A paras 2.5–2.5A.

(1)(in the case of residential property): whether a Class F land charge has been registered, or whether a notice under the
Matrimonial Homes Act 1983 has been entered or whether a notice under the Family Law Act 1996 has been registered.
If this is the case, the claimant must serve notice of the proceedings on any such person 13 ;
 
(2)the state of the mortgage account, including the amount of the advance, periodic payments and interest required, the
amount needed to redeem the mortgage including solicitors’ costs and administration charges and the rate of interest
payable. If the mortgage is a regulated consumer credit agreement, they must state the total amount outstanding.
If the claim is based on arrears the particulars of claim must set out:
 
(1)in schedule form, the dates when the arrears arose, all amounts due, the dates and amounts of all payments made since
the last default (or for the last two years, whichever is the shorter) and a running total of the arrears;
 
(2)details of all other payments to be made and claimed;
 
(3)any relevant information about the defendant’s circumstances, including details about benefits and payments direct; and
 
(4)any previous steps taken to recover arrears, with full details of any court proceedings.
Note also that PD 55A para.2.5(4), (5) and (7) provide that the following must also be included in particulars of claim in
proceedings against mortgagors:
 
(1)whether or not the loan which is secured by the mortgage is a regulated consumer credit agreement and, if so, specify
the date on which any notice required by ss.76 or 87 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974 was given;
 
(2)if appropriate details that show the property is not one to which s.141 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974 applies;
 
(3)give details of any tenancy entered into between the mortgagor and mortgagee (including any notices served).
Notices to be sent before possession hearing
15-11
Where a mortgagee seeks possession of land which consists of or includes residential property, CPR r.55.10 applies and requires
that:
 
“(1)The claimant must send, within 5 days of receiving notification of the date of the hearing by the court, a
notice to (a) the property, addressed to ‘the tenant or the occupier’; (b) the housing department of the local
authority where the property is located; and (c) any registered proprietor of a registered charge over the
property, which must:
 
—state that a possession claim for the property has started
 
—show the name and address of the claimant, the defendant and the court which issued the claim form;
and
 
—give details of the hearing.
 
(2)The notice to the housing department of the local authority must also state the full address of the property.
 
(3)The claimant must produce at the hearing:
 
—a copy of the notices; and
 
—evidence that they have been sent.”
