Chapter 4 - Anti-Suit Injunctions
Introduction
4-01
Where parties have entered into an arbitration agreement to resolve their disputes by arbitration and one of the parties commences
or threatens to commence proceedings in a forum in breach of that agreement, it is possible to obtain an anti-suit injunction
restraining that party from commencing or continuing proceedings in breach of the arbitration agreement. 1  The modern
approach is for English courts not to feel diffident about protecting a party’s positive right to have any disputes resolved only
by the mechanism agreed in the arbitration agreement and the negative right not to be sued in any other forum. 2
Jurisdiction
4-02
The English court’s discretionary power to grant interim or final anti-suit injunctions derives from s.37(1) of the Senior Courts
Act 1981, not from s.44 of the 1996 Act. 3  This is because an anti-suit injunction is not granted “for the purposes of and in
relation to arbitral proceedings”, but for the purposes of and in relation to the negative promise contained in the arbitration
agreement not to bring foreign proceedings. 4  However, the general power provided by s.37 of the Senior Courts Act 1981 must
be exercised sensitively and, in particular, with due regard for the scheme and terms of the 1996 Act when any arbitration is
on foot or proposed. 5  It is unclear to what extent this approach requires the considerations regarding urgency in s.44 of the
1996 Act to be applied.
Any person claiming under or through a party to the arbitration agreement may be restrained from bringing proceedings in
breach of an arbitration agreement. 6  Anti-suit injunctions may also be available to prevent a party who has lost the arbitration
from seeking to re-open the same issues in a different forum. 7  Anti-suit injunctions may also restrain a party relying on the
curial jurisdiction of a court other than that in the seat of the arbitration. 8
In Allianz SpA v West Tankers Inc (The Front Comor) 9  the CJEU held that it was incompatible with the Brussels Regulation for
the English court to grant anti-suit injunctions in relation to proceedings or threatened proceedings in breach of an arbitration
agreement in the courts of other European Member States. This brought the jurisprudence relating to anti-suit injunctions in
support of arbitration agreements into line with the jurisprudence relating to exclusive court jurisdiction agreements. 10
The CJEU decision in Gazprom OAO v Lithuania 11  did not directly address whether the Recast Regulation (No. 1215/2012) 12
changed this position. It simply confirmed that it was not incompatible with the Brussels Regulation for a Member State to
recognise and enforce an anti-suit injunction granted by an arbitral tribunal because arbitration does not fall within the scope
of the Brussels Regulation. 13  However, the West Tankers and Gazprom decisions were analysed in detail in Nori Holding Ltd
v Public Joint-Stock Co Bank Otkritie Financial Corp at [69]–[102] 14  and the West Tankers decision was found to remain an
authoritative statement of EU law (see [99]).

Principles
4-03
The grant or refusal of an anti-suit injunction will be determined pursuant to English law and will only be granted to protect
a substantive legal or equitable right or to restrain proceedings that are or will be vexatious or oppressive. 15  In relation to
the grant of anti-suit injunctions to restrain the breach of an arbitration agreement, these principles give rise to the following
considerations: (a) is there a valid arbitration agreement 16 ; (b) is the claim or threatened claim in the foreign forum made by (or
with the collusion of) a party to the arbitration agreement 17 ; (c) is the claim within the scope of the arbitration agreement 18 ;
(d) is the application made without delay; (e) has the applicant submitted to the jurisdiction of the foreign court 19 ; (f) is the
respondent amenable to the jurisdiction of the English court 20 ; and (g) is it right, on the balance of convenience, to make the
order? 21
If the applicant can establish a strong prima facie case that there has been a breach or threatened breach of a valid arbitration
agreement, then the English court will ordinarily grant an anti-suit injunction unless there are “strong reasons” not to do so.
The burden is on the defendant to establish the strong reasons. 22
One of the factors that may be relied upon as a strong reason not to grant an anti-suit injunction is if the applicant has failed to
apply promptly and before the foreign proceedings are too far advanced. 23  The length of the delay is likely to be less important
than the extent to which the foreign proceedings have advanced. Further, where the foreign proceedings have advanced to
determining the question of jurisdiction this may be given less weight than where the merits have been decided. Whether the
delay justifies the court refusing to grant an anti-suit injunction is therefore heavily dependent upon the facts. 24
Another strong reason not to grant an anti-suit injunction might be if there is a risk of parallel proceedings and conflicting
decisions. 25  However, where the risk of parallel proceedings is not caused by the anti-suit injunction and the proceedings will
continue irrespective of such injunction, then this factor may not be given much weight. 26
Whereas the English courts have expressed a need to be cautious about granting anti-suit injunctions, 27  the approach to the
issue of comity in relation to anti-suit injunctions is that they are directed at the offending party and not at the foreign court. 28
Further, it has been held that anti-suit injunctions are not a breach of comity since they merely require a party to honour his
contract. 29  Nevertheless, it has been held that the issue of delay is closely connected to the issue of judicial comity and therefore
the longer the delay and the more advanced the foreign proceedings, the more unlikely it becomes that the court will exercise
its discretion. 30
A party who breaches an anti-suit injunction may be subject to the penal sanctions expressly stated in the penal notice on the
order. These sanctions mean that the party may be held to be in contempt of court and may be imprisoned, fined or have its
assets seized. 31
Procedure
4-04
The procedure for seeking anti-suit injunctions in support of an arbitration agreement seems to be to issue substantive
proceedings by way of an arbitration claim form claiming a final anti-suit injunction to protect the arbitration and to claim for
interim relief by application notice in the context of the final claim. A more detailed explanation of the procedure to be followed
can be found in Raphael’s The Anti-Suit Injunction. 32
