The operation of a bank account
Accounts in credit
9-04
A bank account is a statement of the extent of indebtedness as between the bank and its customer. Where an account is in credit,
the bank is indebted to the customer; where the account is overdrawn, the customer is indebted to the bank. Moneys deposited
by the customer with his bank may be appropriated by the bank for its own use 13 ; the deposit merely creates a chose in action,
so that the bank is liable to repay the debt owed to the customer in accordance with the agreement between the parties. 14
However, in the absence of agreement to the contrary, the customer’s cause of action against the bank is not completed until
demand is made, at which point time begins to run for the purposes of the Limitation Act 1980. 15  Unless the demand, properly
construed, also amounts to a termination of the banker-customer contract, it does not bring that contract to an end, with the
result that the customer is entitled (in the absence of an express or implied contractual limitation) to make a further demand
so that time will begin to run from the later demand. 16
Overdrawn accounts
9-05
Where a customer has an overdrawn account, he owes a debt to the bank in the sum of the debit balance. An overdraft facility
will almost always be repayable on demand by its express terms; indeed, if it is not then the facility is more likely to be regarded
as a term loan than an overdraft. Hence, even in the absence of an express term, an overdraft will be repayable on demand
unless the customer can demonstrate some express or implied agreement to the contrary. 17  Where the facility letter states that
the facility is repayable on demand, but also provides that the bank’s present intention is to make the facility available until a
specified future date, the bank nevertheless retains the right to call in the facility at will at any time. 18  No duty of care is owed to
the customer or an interested third party when a bank exercises its right of withdrawal of overdraft facilities at will. 19  In Lloyds
Bank Plc v Voller, 20  the Court of Appeal held that where a current account is opened by a customer with no express agreement as
to what the overdraft facility should be, then, where a customer draws a cheque which causes the account to become overdrawn,
the customer impliedly requests the bank to grant the customer an overdraft upon its usual terms as to interest rates and charges.
However, in respect of overdrafts or other credit facilities with individuals, banks should be wary of the “unfair relationship”
provisions of ss.140A-140D of the Consumer Credit Act 1974. The fact that a facility is repayable on demand is most unlikely,
of itself, to give rise to an unfair relationship, since such a provision is commonplace. 21  But under s.140A(1)(a), the court
may determine the relationship between the bank and the individual debtor to be unfair, depending on all the circumstances,
by reason of “the way in which the creditor has exercised or enforced any of his rights under the agreement”; hence, if the
bank were to make demand peremptorily or arbitrarily, it may face a challenge under this provision. 22  By s.16(6C) of the Act,
ss.140A–140D do not in any event apply to consumer credit agreements secured by a regulated mortgage.

Account charges
9-06
Banks customarily levy charges to some of their account holders. It was contended in Office of Fair Trading v Abbey National
Plc 23  that the OFT should be entitled to investigate whether standard bank charges arising out of unauthorised overdrafts were
unfair within the meaning of reg.5(1) of the Unfair Terms in Consumer Contracts Regulations 1999. The Supreme Court held
that since such bank charges constituted part of the price or remuneration for the banking services supplied as a whole, they did
not fall to be assessed under reg.5(1) as they fell within the exception in reg.6(2). In addition, at first instance Andrew Smith J
had held that such bank charges were not penalties since they were not attributable to any breach by the customer of an express
or implied contractual term, 24  and that finding was not appealed. Following the Supreme Court decision, the OFT considered
the possibility of investigating bank charges using other enforcement tools, in particular the unfair relationship provisions in
ss.140A–140D of the Consumer Credit Act 1974, but decided that there were not good grounds for concluding that a collective
challenge alleging breach of such provisions would have good prospects of success. 25
Interest
9-07
It is customary for banks to pay interest on accounts in credit and to levy interest on sums overdrawn. A bank is entitled to
charge different interest rates to different categories of borrower. 26  It is also entitled, if the loan agreement so provides, to vary
the interest rate at its discretion. However, it has been held that a contractual discretionary power to vary interest rates is subject
to an implied term that the rate would not be set dishonestly, for an improper purpose, capriciously, arbitrarily or irrationally;
but that does not mean that the bank is unable to act in its own commercial interests in setting the appropriate rate of interest. 27
Higher interest rates are commonly charged on borrowings that exceed a specified overdraft limit, and a contractual term
providing for a higher rate of interest on such “unauthorised” or “excess” borrowing would not normally be regarded as a
penalty. 28  However, the law on penalties may apply to “default” interest rates, if applied by reason of the customer’s failure to
repay an overdraft facility on demand. The law on penalties was revised by the Supreme Court in ParkingEye Ltd v Beavis. 29
The modern test is whether the bank had a legitimate interest in ensuring performance by the customer and the default rate of
interest was not extravagant or unconscionable in comparison to that interest. In general, however, a defaulting borrower may
justifiably be regarded as being a greater credit risk than the non-defaulting borrower, justifying the application of a higher
rate of interest. 30
The bank’s entitlement to set differential interest rates, where the borrower is an individual, will also be subject to the “unfair
relationship” provisions in ss.140A–140D of the Consumer Credit Act 1974, considered earlier. Section 140A(1)(a) provides
that a Court may determine that the relationship between a lender and a borrower arising out of a credit agreement be unfair on
the basis of “any of the terms of the credit agreement”. However, the court is required to take into account not only the interests
of the borrower but also the commercial perspective of the bank as lender: see s.140A(2). Hence, it is possible that the courts
will continue to take the view that provided a bank has been setting its interest rates in accordance with genuine commercial
considerations, they ought not to be impugned.
The bank’s right of set off
9-08
A bank has the right to combine two or more accounts held by its customer without notice, and to set one off against the other
(in the absence of agreement to the contrary). 31  The accounts need not be at the same branch. 32  The right of set off provides a
defence, for example, to a claim for wrongful refusal of a payment instruction, since a bank may legitimately refuse to comply

particular account. The bank is not required to notify the customer of its intention to exercise the right of set off, and the right
