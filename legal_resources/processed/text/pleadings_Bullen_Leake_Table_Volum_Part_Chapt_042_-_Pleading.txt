Pleading
General
10-22
There is a substantial volume of old case law, much of it pre-dating the 1882 Act, dealing with technical questions of pleading
in this context. Much, if not all is now obsolete. The Civil Procedure Rules require that particulars of claim should be a “concise
statement of the facts relied on”. Where, under the approach favoured by the CPR, the parties have clearly identified their
positions and the matters, if any, in contention are known (for example, as to the consideration for a bill), the claimant should
take care to set out in detail his case upon the facts relevant to the issues which are in dispute.
In many cases actions brought to enforce payment upon a bill of exchange are in the nature of debt enforcement; as discussed
under “defences” (paras 10-24 and 10-25), there are relatively few defences available to claims for payment upon a bill. Under
the Rules of the Supreme Court it was therefore quite common for a writ to be served bearing only a general indorsement,
in anticipation of judgment in default being entered upon it. Under the CPR, except in the Commercial Court, it is generally
necessary for fully pleaded particulars of claim to have been delivered, and the general practice is to encourage service of full
particulars of claim with, or indorsed upon, the original claim form (CPR PD 16 para.3.1).
Bills of exchange typically involve a chain of liabilities. It is common for a holder to claim against both the acceptor, and the
drawer or any prior indorsers of the bill, in the same proceedings. Similarly, the defendants to an action may in turn claim
indemnities (for example, prior indorser against drawer) from other defendants, or from non-parties. Such claims would now
fall within Pt 20 of the CPR, and the forms to be used in them will correspond with necessary modifications, to those set out
below, and under “Defences”.
The statement of case should identify the bill, its date, and the amount of it, and it should identify the parties to it. 61  It is
suggested that it is sensible to annex a copy to the pleading since this both avoids the need to transcribe precisely the terms
of the instrument (which in some cases may be highly material where, for example, it is disputed that the instrument is on its
proper construction a bill), and places before the court the evidence necessary to found an application for summary judgment,
should one follow.
The particulars should identify the parties to the action, and the capacity in which they sue or are sued. Where the claimant sues
as holder he should set out the chain of indorsements by which title became his. A holder may strike out from a bill indorsements
which are not necessary to his title, so that if a bill has been the subject of a series of indorsements in blank it is not strictly
necessary to plead them all. In that event, at trial all those indorsements not pleaded or relied on must be struck out 62  and the
remedy against the indorsers is lost. In practical terms it is preferable, certainly if there is any doubt in the matter, to set out all
the indorsements and preserve the rights against all parties.

It has been held that it is unnecessary to allege that an instrument has been delivered, since this is implicit in the allegation that
it has been drawn 63  and by virtue of s.21(3) there is a rebuttable presumption to that effect. Nonetheless, it is suggested that
under the CPR it is better to aver the fact of delivery, since any contract upon a bill is incomplete until it has been delivered so

as to give effect to it (s.21(1)). If there is any dispute about whether a bill has been validly delivered then it will be essential
to set out all of the material facts said to amount to delivery in full.
Likewise, it is not strictly necessary to allege consideration, which is presumed unless the contrary is shown under s.30(1) of
the Act. Again, it is suggested that it is preferable to state the consideration given where it is practicable to do so, and essential
so to do if it is known that this is disputed
By s.30(2) “every holder of a bill is prima facie deemed to be a holder in due course; but if in an action on a bill it is admitted
or proved that the acceptance, issue, or subsequent negotiation of the bill is affected with fraud, duress, or force and fear, or
illegality, the burden of proof is shifted, unless and until the holder proves that subsequent to the alleged fraud or illegality,
value has in good faith been given for the bill”. Nonetheless, the capacity in which the claimant sues, and whether or not as
holder in due course (or for value) should be set out. 64  If it is known that such matters are or will be alleged, it will be sensible
to set out the detailed facts by which it is alleged (if it is) that the claimant is, or derives his title from, a holder in due course.
Where the claim is based on dishonour by non-acceptance, the fact of the presentation for acceptance, or any circumstances
which excuse it (or any delay) must be set out. Likewise, in any claim against the drawer or indorser which is based upon non-
payment, the fact of presentation (or its dispensation) together with dishonour must be alleged. Though in some cases it might
not be strictly necessary, in claims against the acceptor it is obviously sensible to set out the presentment and dishonour, or the
circumstances in which presentment was not required.
That due proceedings on dishonour have been taken is a constituent of the liability of the drawer and any indorser. The particulars
of claim must therefore contain either the allegation that the requirements for notice of dishonour (and in the case of a foreign
bill protest) have been complied with, or set out the circumstances which are said to have dispensed with them. 65
Section 70 of the Act allows an action to be brought on a lost bill, subject to the provision of any indemnity required by the
Court in accordance with that section.
Relief claimed
10-24
Section 57 of the Bills of Exchange Act defines the damages which are payable upon dishonour of a bill and provides that they
are deemed to be a liquidated sum:
 
“(1)The holder may recover from any party liable on the bill, and the drawer who has been compelled to pay
the bill may recover from the acceptor, and an indorser who has been compelled to pay the bill may recover
from the acceptor, or from the drawer, or from a prior indorser—
 
(a)the amount of the bill;
 
(b)interest thereon from the time of presentment for payment if the bill is payable on demand, and from
the maturity of the bill in any other case;
 
(c)the expenses of noting, or when protest is necessary, and the protest has been extended, the expenses
of protest.
 
(2)Where by this Act interest may be recovered as damages, such interest may, if justice require it, be withheld
wholly or in part, and where a bill is expressed to be payable with interest at a given rate, interest as damages
may or may not be given at the same rate as interest proper.”
In Banque Cantonale de Geneve v Sanomi, 66  Blair J had no difficulty holding that a bank which took promissory notes from
an individual as security for company liabilities was “on ordinary principles” entitled to recover on them “only up to the extent
of the outstanding indebtedness” of the company.

Where a bill is expressed in a foreign currency, the amount of it may be claimed and awarded by the court in that currency. 67
By the terms of CPR PD40B para.10, a judgment should provide for payment of the foreign sum or in the alternative in sterling
equivalent (though it would appear that the court would retain a discretion to entertain the parties’ submissions upon this).
Section 57 of the Bills of Exchange Act will not apply to a bill or other instrument which is governed by foreign law. The
English courts may award damages in accordance with that proper law. In Karafarin Bank v Gholam Reza Mansoury-Dara 68
the subject cheques were governed by Iranian law. Iranian law prohibited the recovery of interest as a matter of public policy,
but allowed damages to be awarded for late payment by reference to the declared rate of inflation. The English court gave
judgment including such damages.
10-25
By s.9(3), “Where a bill is expressed to be payable with interest, unless the instrument otherwise provides, interest runs from
the date of the bill, and if the bill is undated from the issue thereof”; such interest will be recovered as part of the amount of
the bill. Where no interest is specified to run during the currency of the bill, it may nonetheless be recovered from the date
of maturity or presentation for payment (in the case of a demand bill) under s.57 and alternatively under s.35A of the Senior
Courts Act 1981 and it is prudent to rely upon both. Interest under either provision is discretionary and no rate is prescribed.
Interest may be prudently claimed at a rate of or just above base rate, but where a bill is payable in and/or judgment is sought
in a foreign currency it may be that the interest rate applicable to that currency will be more appropriate.
Under s.44A of the Administration of Justice Act 1970, inserted by s.1 of the Private International Law (Miscellaneous
Provisions) Act 1995 the court has power to direct that interest should run upon a judgment which is expressed in foreign
currency at a rate other than that of the rate specified under s.17 of the Judgments Act 1838. Generally, if interest is claimed
at a rate equal to or lower than the Judgment Act rate (both before and after judgment) the court will be prepared to enter a
judgment upon default for it; if a higher rate is claimed (or if a defendant applies, on the basis that a lower rate of interest is
appropriate in light of the currency of the obligation) then it will be necessary for interest to be assessed.
CPR r.16.4(2) requires a claimant who is seeking interest to set out the basis (whether under an enactment, contract or otherwise)
it is claimed, and if an amount is specified to identify the rate claimed, the date from which and to which it is claimed, the total
amount of the interest, and the daily rate at which it accrues after that date.
The expenses of noting and protesting a bill, where it is necessary to incur them, may be specifically recovered but should be
properly set out and limited to those specified by the Act, 69
Defences
10-26
A defence to an action on a bill of exchange must either deny one or more of the constituent elements of the claim, or set
up a positive defence either arising out of the operation of the Act, or the general law. The extent to which it is open to a
defendant to raise general contractual defences depends upon the status of the claimant; such matters will be no answer to an
action brought by a holder in due course. Where therefore such a defence is to be raised, it is necessary not merely to set out
those circumstances, but also to negative the status of the claimant (who is otherwise presumed to be a holder in due course).
There are a number of statutory presumptions contained in the Act, and other “statutory estoppels”. It is not, for example, open
to an indorsee to dispute the genuineness of the drawer’s signature (and see generally ss.54 and 55), and care must be taken
not to attempt to do so.
Under the CPR a defendant must state which of the allegations in the particulars of claim he admits, which he denies, and which
he is unable to admit or deny. Each fact appearing in the particulars of claim must therefore be dealt with in one of these ways,
though in the precedents which follow below only the “operative” part of the defence will be set out. Where a matter is denied
reasons must be given (CPR r.16.5) and care must be taken not to plead in a manner which is ambiguous or evasive. Where a
positive case is to be advanced it should be set out clearly and fully. So, for example, a defendant who wishes to contest the
claimant’s allegation that a bill was presented for payment must set out whether or not it was presented at all, and if it was the
particular respects in which the presentment was defective; it is not enough simply to deny (due) presentment.

Cross Claims
10-27
A liquidated cross-claim may be relied on by a defendant. Between immediate parties a liquidated and ascertained partial failure
of consideration may also be raised. However, an unliquidated cross-claim, even if it would otherwise be capable of giving
rise to an equitable set-off may not be raised as a defence to a claim for payment upon a bill of exchange. 70  Nor, generally,
will a stay of execution be granted on the ground of such a cross-claim, 71  though it can be raised as a “cross claim” for the
purpose of insolvency proceedings. 72  In Isovel Contracts Ltd (In Administration) v ASB Building Technologies Ltd 73  a stay of
execution was refused in respect of the judgment upon a cheque in favour of the company in administration where no statutory
set-off arose. For a fuller discussion see Byles at Ch.26. In Ahmed v Landstone Leisure Ltd 74  the court set aside a statutory
demand based upon a dishonoured cheque which had arguably been procured by misrepresentation. In Oxigen Environmental
Ltd v Mullan 75  the court gave judgment upon a promissory note, but ordered a stay of execution while requiring the amount
to be paid into court pending trial of a counterclaim.
The importance of the principle that bills and notes are to be treated as cash, and that summary judgment will normally be
given upon them without set-off or stay for any counterclaim, was emphasised by Blair J in Banque Cantonale de Geneve v
Sanomi. 76  Very clear statements of principle can be found in the decisions of Vinodh Coomaraswamy J in Cassa di Risparmio
di Parma e Piacenza SpA v Rals International Pte Ltd and Millenium Commodity Trading Ltd v BS Tech Pte Ltd. 77
Other relief
10-28
In an appropriate case (for example where a bill has been delivered in breach of a collateral condition, or there is a risk of
wrongful onward negotiation to a holder in due course) it may be sensible for a party to counterclaim for the delivery up of an
instrument, or for an injunction to restrain further indorsement or transfer.
Conditional Payment
10-29
Where a bill, note or cheque is given in respect of an underlying debt it operates as a conditional payment, 78  and this operates
as good defence to an action on the underlying debt before the security is due. 79  It will be no defence if the security has become
due but is unpaid (in which case the defendant may be liable upon it, as well as the underlying debt) unless it is also alleged
that it has been negotiated away by the claimant, or lost, so that it remains outstanding in the hands of third parties and the
defendant remains potentially liable upon it. 80
