Chapter 16 - Sale of Shares
Measure of damages
Against the vendor
16-03
Although shares are not within the definition of “goods” in the Sale of Goods Act 1979 s.62 (because they are “things in action”),
the measure of damages for non-delivery of goods has long been taken as the measure of damages for non-delivery of shares
by the vendor. 20  The measure of damages is the market value of the shares on the date they should have been delivered and
that value is the purchase price of the shares at that date. 21  Prices paid by a third party to the vendor or by the purchaser for
replacement shares may be evidence of the value if there is no market, but these prices are generally irrelevant to ascertaining
the market value.
 
(1)Where the market price rises between the date of breach and the date of judgment, Parke B in Shaw v Holland held that
the date of judgment was not relevant, since “the plaintiff has his money in his possession, and might have gone into the
market and bought other shares as soon as the contract was broken”. If the purchaser has already paid the price, this reason
falls away: moreover the purchaser of shares can sue for specific performance as soon as the date for delivery arrives. The
modern law is stated in Johnson v Agnew, 22  especially at 401, per Lord Wilberforce:
“The general principle for the assessment of damages is compensatory … Where the contract is one of sale
this principle normally leads to assessment at the date of the breach—a principle recognised and embodied in
s.51 of the Sale of Goods Act 1893. But this is not an absolute rule: if to follow it would give rise to injustice,
the court has power to fix such other date as may be appropriate in the circumstances.”
 
(2)Where the date for completion is postponed at the request of the vendor justice will normally require that the value of
the shares at the end of the period of postponement is the measure of loss, and it was so held prior to Johnson v Agnew:
Wilson v London & Globe Finance Corp. 23
 
(3)Where the sale is of shares not issued at the date of the contract, the subject-matter of the sale may be the letter of
allotment or other contractual document issued by the company rather than the shares. If so, it has been held that the
purchaser must measure his loss by the value of the letter of allotment at the date for delivery of it, and not the value of
the shares when issued 24 ; but if this decision is treated as establishing a general principle in such cases, it is submitted
that it cannot stand in the light of Johnson v Agnew.
A purchaser who successfully sues for specific performance and therefore takes delivery of shares later than the contractual
date will usually claim that remedy only if the market price has risen since the contract: the damages will then be nominal. If
the shares are shares in a private company of which the purchaser wishes to obtain control in order to benefit himself by earning
directors fees or remuneration, however, the damages may be substantial even if their value in the open market is less than the
contract price (as may well be the case if the shares are a minority shareholding in a private company); to recover such damages,
the purchaser would have to show that the vendor knew what losses were likely to accrue to the purchaser in consequence of

able to recover dividends paid to the vendor and interest for late payment to him. 26
If the shares delivered are not as represented, the vendor may be liable for damages for breach of (in effect) a warranty of
quality; so a purchaser recovered damages where shares were sold as fully paid but were in fact only partly paid. 27
The normal measure of damages where the shares delivered are not as represented is the difference between the value of the
shares as warranted and the value of the shares in fact. 28
Against the purchaser
16-04
The normal measure of damages is the contract price less the market value of the shares at the date for acceptance. 29  The
principles stated in Johnson v Agnew ought, it is submitted, to apply to breach by the purchaser. The damages ought not, however,
to be decreased if the market price of the shares has risen again after the date for acceptance; “if the seller retains the shares
after the breach, the speculation as to the way the market will go is the speculation of the seller, not of the buyer”. 30
Lending contracts
16-05
Stock lending has become, after a long period when the practice was uncommon, a recognised and frequent commercial practice.
However, this practice led to difficulties in establishing ownership of loan stock when the global investment bank, Lehman
Brothers, collapsed. 31
Lending without transfer of the title to the stock by transfer of possession to a borrower who has no notice of any defect in
title of the lender will confer a valid right to possession of the certificates, and the beneficial owner of the shares as against
the lender has no claim for conversion. 32  The measure of damages for failure to deliver the stock to be lent is the price of the
stock at the time for replacement or the price at the date of trial, at the election of the claimant. 33  A mortgagor is entitled to
set off the value of shares which the mortgagee is unable to return, by way of damages, ascertained at the date when the shares
should have been restored. 34
