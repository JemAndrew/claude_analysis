Note
In its original form FSMA provided in s.150(1) for there to be a right of action for breach of the FSA rules: “A contravention
by an authorised person of a rule is actionable at the suit of a private person who suffers loss as a result of the contravention,
subject to the defences and other incidents applying to actions for breach of statutory duty”. The problem was for the claimant
to prove that the breach of statutory duty had caused the financial loss complained of. In Saville v Central Capital Ltd ([2014]
EWCA Civ 337) the Court of Appeal held that the test to be applied to the issue of causation in an action for breach of statutory
duty was to ask whether, if the duty had not been breached, the damage would have occurred. In that case, if the credit-broker
had made a proper explanation of the PPI policy as required by the FSA’s Insurance Conduct of Business Rules (ICOB), the
claimants would not have bought the policy and could recover the premium as damages. Section 150 was re-enacted as s.138D
as from 1 April 2014 and the voluminous provisions of CONC provide a happy hunting ground for borrowers who want to
avoid or minimize their debts.
