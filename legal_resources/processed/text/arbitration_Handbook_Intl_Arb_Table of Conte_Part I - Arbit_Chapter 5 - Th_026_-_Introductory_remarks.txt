Introductory remarks
20-1
Article 20 deals with the language or languages to be used in an ICC arbitration. 82  Unlike with national courts, in arbitral
proceedings the parties may agree on the language of the arbitration, i.e. language in which the arbitral procedure will be
conducted and the award (and procedural orders preceding it) will be issued. This represents one of the major advantages
of international arbitration compared to local court proceedings for parties doing business all over the world. International
arbitration knows no language barriers for its users.
20-2
English has become increasingly important in international business and thus not surprisingly also in ICC arbitration. 80 per
cent of the ICC awards rendered in 2020 were rendered in English and approximately 35 awards rendered were in French and
24 in Spanish and 21 in Portuguese. 83  However, the role of English should be seen within the overall context with respect to
the parties, the arbitrators, the ICC Court and even the nature of the underlying contracts themselves.

20-4
These figures demonstrate the relevance of Spanish, Portuguese, German, French and Italian for ICC arbitration. In the recent
past, several ICC arbitrations have also taken place in Turkish. Therefore, although English is not the language of most parties
to ICC arbitration, it is the most important language used for international business and for ICC arbitration. 85
20-5
Nor is English the native language of most arbitrators in ICC arbitrations, as the origin of the arbitrators reveals. This being
noted, many arbitrators from non-English speaking countries speak excellent English and are of course very used to handling
arbitrations in English. However, for those arbitrators for whom English and legal English is a second language and no matter
how frequently they deal with English, they will be open to influences from the other languages that they speak and from the
civil law concepts with which they are familiar.
20-6
Finally, a review of the list of members of the ICC Court shows that the vast majority do not have English as their native
language, although most speak English fluently. 86  While the official working languages of the ICC Court are English and
French, English has been for many years the lingua franca of the ICC.
20-7
Therefore, while English has become the leading language in ICC arbitration, it is English that is frequently spoken and
understood by persons who are using it as a second or third language or who have been trained in a civil law vocabulary.
Moreover, there are considerable variances in the English used in various parts of the world. Therefore, in preparing an ICC
case, it is important to note this more complex linguistic background as compared to that in national court systems for example.
