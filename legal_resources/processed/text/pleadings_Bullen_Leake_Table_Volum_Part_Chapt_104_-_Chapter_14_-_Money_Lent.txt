Chapter 14 - Money Lent
14-01
This chapter sets out the fundamental legal principles pertaining to the lending of money. It does not deal with regulated
consumer credit agreements, although it should be noted that (a) since 2008 all credit agreements with individual borrowers
(without financial limit) are regulated agreements unless they fall within one of the exceptions specified under the Consumer
Credit Act 1974, (b) the provisions of ss.140A-140D of that Act (discussed later) apply to all credit agreements with individuals,
whether or not they are regulated consumer credit agreements (subject only to exempt agreements under s.16(6C) of that Act).
For a discussion of the law relating to the consumer credit agreements, see Ch.20.
The loan contract
14-02
A contract of loan of money is a contract whereby one person lends or agrees to lend a sum of money to another, in consideration
of an express or implied promise to repay that sum on demand, or at a fixed or determinable time, or conditionally upon an
event which is a certainty. 1  A claim for money lent is a claim on a debt. 2  Where it can be shown (by proof or admission) that
A has paid money to B, then in the absence of any presumption of advancement, there is prima facie an obligation to repay
upon B; the onus is upon B to establish that the money was intended as a gift. 3
Where money is lent without any stipulation as to the time of repayment, a present debt is created which is generally repayable
at once without prior demand. 4  Commonly, however, the parties will expressly agree a time for repayment, or that repayment
should be made on demand. In certain circumstances, for example in respect of money in a bank account, 5  there will be an
implied term that the loan is only repayable on demand. In order to constitute a valid demand:
“there must be a clear intimation that payment is required; nothing more is necessary, and the word ‘demand’
need not be used, neither is the validity of a demand lessened by its being clothed in the language of politeness;
it must be of a peremptory character and unconditional, but the nature of the language is immaterial provided it
has this effect.” 6
A term loan contract may specify certain “events of default”, the occurrence of one or more of which will give the lender the
right to “accelerate” repayment. Typical events of default include: the insolvency or winding-up of the borrower, execution of
any legal process on the borrower’s property, and (if the borrower is a partnership) the dissolution of the partnership. 7  Unless
otherwise stipulated, and except in the case of regulated consumer credit agreements, there is no obligation on the lender to
notify the borrower, and the lender’s right to immediate repayment may arise automatically upon the occurrence of an event
of default. 8
Where a loan is secured, the lender may nevertheless sue upon the borrower’s personal obligation to repay, which is unaffected
by the security. 9  Alternatively, the lender may realise the security, and, if it proves insufficient, sue for the balance. 10  Where,
however, the promise to repay is contained in a covenant in a deed, the action must be brought on the covenant. 11

Interest
14-03
Interest may be claimed in accordance with the applicable contractual term. Where there is no express agreement, there may
be an implied obligation to pay interest if, for example, there has been a course of dealing between the parties (such as where
interest has been charged and paid on other accounts without objection). 12  However, in the absence of an express or implied
agreement to pay interest, there is no obligation at common law to pay interest, although the practical effect of that rule has
now diminished as statutory interest can in all cases be claimed under s.35A of the Senior Courts Act 1981, s.69 of the County
Courts Act 1984 or other relevant provisions. 13
Compound interest is recoverable pursuant to an agreement or by virtue of an established custom binding on the parties, or at
common law (provided that it can be shown that this represents the loss suffered by the lender). 14  A bank has a right implied
by usage to charge compound interest on an overdrawn account, and that right survives a demand for repayment. 15
CPR r.16.4(2) provides that if the claimant is seeking interest he must:
 
“(a)state whether he is doing so—
 
(i)under the terms of a contract;
 
(ii)under an enactment and if so which; or
 
(iii)on some other basis and if so what that basis is; and
 
(b)if the claim is for a specified amount of money, state—
 
(i)the percentage rate at which interest is claimed;
 
(ii)the date from which it is claimed;
 
(iii)the date to which it is calculated, which must not be later than the date on which the claim form
is issued;
 
(iv)the total amount of interest claimed to the date of calculation; and
 
(v)the daily rate at which interest accrues after that date”.
The rule that there was no general common law obligation upon a borrower to pay interest for late payment was heavily criticised
and has now been departed from by the House of Lords in Sempra Metals Ltd v Commissioners of Inland Revenue, 16  which held
that a creditor receiving late payment may, in accordance with general principles of damages, recover lost interest (including
compound interest) provided that such loss is pleaded and proven.
Challenges to interest rates
14-04
In general, the contractual terms of a loan will provide for interest to be payable, and the provision determining the rate of that
interest will not be susceptible to attack as a penalty since the obligation to pay interest does not arise as a result of any breach
of contact. However, it is possible that a default rate of interest, i.e. a higher rate of interest arising as a result of the borrower
defaulting in his contractual obligations, may be regarded as being penal. The law on penalties was revised in ParkingEye Ltd v
Beavis 17 ; the modern test is whether the creditor had a legitimate interest in ensuring compliance with the terms of the loan and
the default rate payable is not extravagant or unconscionable in comparison to that legitimate interest. 18  In general, however,

Unfair relationships
14-05
A loan agreement with an individual borrower, even if not a regulated consumer credit agreement, may be susceptible to attack
as giving rise to an “unfair relationship” under ss. 140A–140D of the Consumer Credit Act 1974. Section 140A enables a
court to make an order under s.140B if it finds that the relationship between the creditor and the debtor arising out of a credit
agreement, or that agreement taken with any “related agreement”, is unfair to the debtor as a result of one or more of three
factors. Those factors are: (a) any of the terms of the agreement (or related agreement); (b) the way in which the creditor has
exercised or enforced any of his rights under the agreement (or any related agreement); or (c) “any other thing done (or not done)
by, or on behalf of, the creditor (whether occurring before or after the making of the agreement or any related agreement)”.
In determining whether one or more of the three factors in s.140A(1) give rise to an “unfair” credit relationship, s.140(A)(2)
requires the court to “have regard to all matters it thinks relevant”, including matters relating to the debtor as well as the creditor.
In the leading Supreme Court decision on these provisions, Plevin v Paragon Personal Finance Ltd, Lord Sumption gave the
following guidance 19 :
“Section 140A is deliberately framed in wide terms with very little in the way of guidance about the criteria
for its application. It is not possible to state a precise or universal test for its application, which must depend
on the court’s judgment of all the relevant facts. Some general points may, however, be made. First, what must
be unfair is the relationship between the debtor and the creditor. In a case like the present one, where the terms
themselves are not intrinsically unfair, this will often be because the relationship is so one-sided as substantially
to limit the debtor’s ability to choose. Secondly, although the court is concerned with hardship to the debtor,
subsection 140A(2) envisages that matters relating to the creditor may also be relevant. There may be features
of the transaction which operate harshly against the debtor but it does not necessarily follow that the relationship
is unfair. These features may be required in order to protect what the court regards as a legitimate interest of
the creditor. Thirdly, the alleged unfairness must arise from one of the three categories of cause listed at sub-
paras (a) to (c). Fourthly, the great majority of relationships between commercial lenders and private borrowers
are probably characterised by large differences of financial knowledge and expertise. It is an inherently unequal
relationship. But it cannot have been Parliament’s intention that the generality of such relationships should be
liable to be reopened for that reason alone.”
It has been suggested that, notwithstanding the width of this provision, the courts are unlikely to regard standard rates of interest
(including default interest) as giving rise to an unfair relationship provided that the rates are set for genuine commercial reasons,
i.e. the legislation is designed to protect consumers, but not to force creditors to act uncommercially. 20  This is in line with the
Court of Appeal’s approach under the former legislation. 21
Novation
14-06
Novation of a debt requires the consent of both contracting parties, as well as the third party. 22  Such a novation will arise, for
example, where the composition of partnership changes, 23  or upon the amalgamation of companies. The effect of a novation
is not to assign or transfer rights or liabilities, but rather to extinguish the original contract and to replace it by another. It is
therefore necessary for there to be consideration for the new contract. 24

Payment
14-07
Where the defendant admits that the moneys claimed are due, he may adopt the admissions procedure under CPR Pt 14 and
make a formal admission as to part of the claim on the Pt 14 practice form.
Where the defence of “tender before claim” is relied upon, the defendant must make a payment into court of the amount he says
was tendered 25  (and the usual restriction upon the disclosure of a Pt 36 payment does not apply 26 ).
