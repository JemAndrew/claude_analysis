Chapter 5 - The Arbitral Proceedings
Anti-suit injunctions issued by courts
22-37
Another issue relates to anti-arbitration or anti-suit injunctions issued by national courts. 215  There are two major issues in this
respect. The first issue is an injunction issued by a court ordering a party not to participate in the arbitration. The second issue
may be the issue of a court injunction in an attempt to support the arbitration. The latter is not a matter for the Tribunal in an
ICC arbitration but is briefly discussed below due to the related issue of anti-suit injunctions issued by Tribunals.
22-38
As regards anti-arbitration injunctions issued against parties, one may involve an injunction against a party participating in the
arbitration. This may raise issues as to due process as the parties may be concerned about breaching a court order by participating
in the arbitral proceedings. In such circumstances, the Tribunal must balance its duties in particular under art.22(1) with its
duties under art.22(4).
22-39
A complex issue arises with respect to state entities and the intervention of state courts. Again the causes of delay can be
diverse. If the place of arbitration is in a neutral country, then the courts of the place of arbitration will be expected to be neutral.
However, if the place of arbitration is in the respondent’s country, where there are doubts as to the independence or integrity of
the judiciary, then the intervention of the national courts may become a serious threat to a pending arbitration.
22-40
In a 2001 ICC case, one of the parties was a state entity from a country that was not a signatory to the New York Convention. 216
The place of arbitration was in that country and its courts issued an order that the proceedings should not proceed. The basis
was that the arbitrators had been challenged for deciding to hold evidentiary hearings at a place other than the capital of the
state. The Tribunal pointed out that the Terms of Reference had expressly provided that hearings could be held other than at the
place of arbitration; that the challenge had been rejected by the ICC Court; that the arbitration involved a state entity and that
there was a body of law precluding states from depriving arbitration clauses of their effect. The Tribunal decided to continue
the proceedings. 217
22-41
The Tribunal in that instance sought to avoid delay in the proceedings, while recognising the role of the courts of the place of
arbitration. The Tribunal’s approach was somewhat different from that in the Karaha Bodas case, 218  as in this case the Tribunal
did not attribute the court’s actions to those of the state. It is not clear that the Tribunal would have taken that decision if the
Terms of Reference had not expressly provided for hearings outside of state X.
22-42
In the Elektrim case discussed above, the English High Court refused to grant an anti-arbitration injunction with respect to the
LCIA arbitration pending in London due to the parallel ICC arbitration in Geneva. Although, as noted above, it held that the
courts should defer to the LCIA arbitral Tribunal as to the issue of the suspension of proceedings, the court also noted that the
issues were different in the two arbitrations. 219
22-43
In Albon v Naza Motor Trading Sdn BHD, the English Court of Appeal upheld an anti-arbitration injunction with respect to
an arbitration in Malaysia governed by Malaysian law. The injunction was issued at the request of the party who maintained

that the agreement in which the arbitration clause was found was a forgery and that the real agreement between the parties had
been an oral agreement in England. In this case, the party bringing the English injunctive proceedings did so to rely on them
as a valid reason for not attending the arbitration proceedings, although it is not clear whether the English court order would
be enforceable in Malaysia, which was the place of arbitration. 220  More recently, in the Enka case 221  discussed at para.6-8,
the UK Supreme Court upheld an injunction against Russian proceedings where the place of arbitration was London and the
court held that the arbitration agreement was subject to English law.
22-44
In the West Tankers case, the European Court of Justice clarified the intra-European Union position as regards anti-suit
injunctions in favour of arbitration in light of Council Regulation 44/2001. 222  Despite the express exclusion of arbitration from
the Regulation, the ECJ ruled that it is inconsistent with Regulation 44/2001 for an EU Member State Court to issue an anti-suit
injunction forbidding a lawsuit to proceed in another Member State, even if the anti-suit injunction is issued in support of an
arbitration agreement. The court found that Regulation 44/2001 applied because the subject matter of the dispute, i.e. the nature
of the rights to be protected between the parties, fell within the scope of the Regulation (in the instant case, the applicability of an
arbitration clause). The court then concluded that using an anti-suit injunction to prevent a court of a Member State from ruling,
amounts to stripping that court of the power to rule on its own jurisdiction under the Regulation. This in turn is contrary to the
mutual trust between legal systems and judicial institutions of the Union. Thus, anti-suit injunctions applicable to courts in the
EU are incompatible with Regulation 44/2001, although as discussed below, anti-suit arbitrations may be issued by Tribunals
in the EU with respect to EU courts. 223  Since the UK is no longer an EU state, it is not clear that this precedent is applicable
to UK courts at this time. 224
22-45
In the case of Sulamerica, 225  the English Court of Appeal upheld a lower court decision continuing an injunction against
Brazilian parties against bringing proceedings in Brazil with respect to an insurance policy governed by Brazilian law but
providing for arbitration in London. The Brazilian parties maintained that, under Brazilian law, which was the express governing
law for the policy, the arbitration clause was invalid. The Court of Appeal held that the arbitration clause was governed by
English law in part as the law of the place of arbitration.
22-46
However, the English courts will not necessarily enjoin proceedings with respect to interim measures in support of a London
arbitration. In U&M Mining Zambia Ltd v Konkola Copper Mines Plc 226  with respect to a dispute concerning the operation of
a mine located in Zambia, between Zambian parties, with an LCIA arbitration agreement, and London as place of arbitration. In
this case, Konkola Copper Mines Pls (KCM) sought and obtained an injunction from the Zambian courts, pending arbitration
proceedings. U&M obtained an anti-suit injunction before the English High Court to restrain KCM from taking steps in the
proceedings started in Zambia. The High Court lifted the English injunction noting that the issue of preliminary relief related
to proceedings between two Zambian companies relating to a Zambian mine. For the authors, this would have been a case for
the appointment of an Emergency Arbitrator if that had been possible.
22-47
The issues of jurisdiction and of injunctions not to bring or carry on proceedings in a forum outside the Brussels/Lugano
regime, whether or not the arbitration has started, were recently addressed by the English Supreme Court. In Ust-Kamenogorsk
Hydropower Plant JSC v AES Ust-Kamenogorsk Hydropower Plant LLP, 227  Ust-Kamenogorsk Hydropower Plant JSC (JSC)
brought proceedings against AES Ust-Kamenogorsk Hydropower Plant LLP (AESUK) before the Kazakhstan courts which
declared the ICC arbitration agreement as null and void and allowed matters to proceed. AESUK then brought proceedings
in England (English law being the law of the arbitration) seeking an anti-suit injunction although no arbitration was currently
taking place, and itself having no intention of starting proceedings. The UK Supreme Court upheld the injunction based on the
negative obligation of the arbitration agreement not to bring proceedings in breach of it. However, the nature of the negative
obligation depends on the terms of the arbitration agreement itself. In Anzen Ltd v Hermes One Ltd, 228  the Privy Council dealt
with an arbitration agreement providing that a party “may” submit a dispute to ICC arbitration. The Privy Council held that
this wording in and of itself did not disentitle a party from bringing court proceedings but that such proceedings should be
stayed if the other party makes an unequivocal request that the dispute be submitted to arbitration or if the party applies for
a stay in the court proceedings.
