Right to an independent and impartial Tribunal (arts 11, 14, 15, and 32)
22-75
Article 22(4) requires the Tribunal to act fairly and impartially. A Tribunal with members who are not independent is not in a
position to act fairly. A party has the right to have as members of the Tribunal persons for which there is no “likelihood that they
will be influenced by factors other than the merits of the case as presented by the parties” (IBA Conflict Guidelines, General
Principle 2(c)). In addition, a Tribunal must act in a collegial fashion if it is to act fairly.
22-76
The requirements of independence and impartiality are discussed under arts 11 and 14. As regards concrete examples, the Non-
Waivable Red List and Waivable Red List as well as the various decisions and cases are referred to beginning at para.14-60. In
addition, since art.22(4) focuses on the procedure some specific examples are provided below.
22-77
As discussed in ASM Shipping v TTMI Ltd, an arbitrator is not in a position to decide a case fairly if the arbitrator has, as
counsel, challenged the honesty of a key witness. 258  A Tribunal that is called upon to decide certain issues as a preliminary
matter should not decide other issues or express an opinion as to the other matters in deciding the preliminary matter. Therefore,
where a Tribunal decided preliminary issues in a partial award, an arbitrator who dissented and took the position that a party’s
claims on other issues were without foundation was removed. 259
22-78
A party has the right to have a co-arbitrator who participates in the proceedings and in the deliberations. In the Dutco case of the
French Supreme Court confirmed the importance of ensuring that each party had an equal opportunity to designate an arbitrator.
The obligations with respect to deliberation are closely related to that right. 260
22-79
In a number of French cases the Paris Court of Appeal has dealt in detail with the obligations of collegiality and the obligation
of arbitrators to participate in deliberations. In Malecki 261  a co-arbitrator resigned and the chair continued the proceedings in
his absence. The Paris Court of Appeals refused to permit enforcement of the award in France on the basis of a violation of
collegiality. In Comilog, 262  a co-arbitrator resigned during the deliberations and the award was annulled for failure to respect
the principle of collegiality. The Paris Court of Appeal distinguished two situations. The first is where the arbitrator failed to
participate in deliberations despite continuing as an arbitrator and having the possibility of participating. The second situation
is where the arbitrator had ceased to act. In the latter case, the Tribunal was improperly constituted.
22-80
The Milutinovic case 263  concerned a dispute between the members of a consortium for the construction of a power station
in Libya and the resignation of a co-arbitrator who resigned after he disagreed with a procedural decision. The other two
arbitrators rendered a partial award. They decided that since the resignation had not been accepted by the ICC, the arbitrator
was still considered to be a member of the Tribunal. 264  The Swiss Federal Supreme Court annulled the award on the basis that
it was a violation of the claimant’s basic rights that the two arbitrators continued the proceedings in the absence of the third
arbitrator. In another case, the Swiss Federal Supreme Court upheld an award issued after an arbitrator who failed to participate
in deliberations in an ICC arbitration. 265

22-81
In Republic of Colombia v Cauca Company, 266  Columbia had appointed a co-arbitrator who resigned after the proceedings
were closed and several days prior to the date limit for rendering an award. When Columbia failed to appoint a replacement
co-arbitrator, the remaining members of the Tribunal issued an award. The US Supreme Court upheld the award finding that
it was “sufficient and effective”.
22-82
The duty to act fairly applies with respect to procedure and deliberations. In Garoube, 267  a co-arbitrator supported a party’s
application to remove the president of the Tribunal attributing basically to the other party. The Paris Court of Appeal (upheld
by the French Supreme Court) annulled the award and related addendum issued with the participation of the co-arbitrator in
particular on the basis that the co-arbitrator’s letter of support evidenced bias. In fact, after participating in the annulled award
and addendum, the co-arbitrator had been removed by the ICC Court due to his relationship with the counsel. Subsequently,
in a domestic arbitration in the Tapie case, 268  the Paris Court of Appeal held that the same co-arbitrator had an improper
relationship with the same counsel and that the award had been obtained by fraud and should be annulled. The decision was
upheld by the French Supreme Court. 269
