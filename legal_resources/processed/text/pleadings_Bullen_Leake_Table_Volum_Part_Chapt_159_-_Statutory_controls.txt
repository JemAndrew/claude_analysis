Statutory controls
20-03
Statutory controls on certain forms of credit agreement have existed since at least the Great War (arguably in the case of “usury”
since the Middle Ages) and hire-purchase became subject to a complete legal regime in the inter-war period. No attempt was
made to create a universal system for credit and hiring until the passing of the Consumer Credit Act 1974 and even then it took
a decade to bring all its provisions into effect. In this section the Consumer Credit Act 1974 will be referred to as “the CCA”
and all reference to section numbers, unless otherwise stated, will be to sections of the CCA. The CCA was drastically amended
by the Consumer Credit Act 2006 and was further amended when the UK implemented the EU Consumer Credit Directive
2008 (Directive 2008/48/EC) 1  which aimed to harmonise the consumer credit law of all 27 EU Member States. In general the
changes brought about by the Directive came into force on 1 February 2011.
Until 2014 both the regulatory structure and the statutory rules for this sector were to be found in the CCA and its derivative
statutory instruments, which established the regulator (the Office of Fair Trading—OFT) and provided a prescriptive code
governing all aspects of credit and hire agreement. On 1 April 2014, however, the regulation of the industry was transferred
from the OFT to the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) and the OFT was abolished. The FCA’s governing statute was the
Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA) and the regulatory side of the credit industry was subsumed into the FCA’s
general remit over the financial services industry.
This led to a very uneasy situation where the main provisions of the CCA relating to the solicitation, negotiation, form, contents,
administration and enforcement of regulated agreements remained in force but were supplemented by a regulatory régime
which operated through mandatory codes of practice incorporated into FCA handbooks and sourcebooks. The FCA published
a specific sourcebook for consumer credit, known as CONC, whose provisions, though expressed in very general terms, will
sit alongside the very precise and prescriptive rules laid down by the CCA and its subordinate legislation. The litigator must,
therefore, bear in mind that it is no longer sufficient to consult the CCA and its regulations to see whether the agreement is
compliant and the conduct of the trader lawful: he must also be aware of the FCA material and, in particular, CONC.
The Consumer Rights Act 2015, which came into force on 1 October 2015, impinges on those credit agreements where the credit
finances the acquisition of goods, software or services but mainly in the field of providing redress for breaches of contractual
terms relating to title, quality and performance. The provisions of that Act will be discussed in Section 25: Supply of Goods,
Digital Content.
20-04
All the categories of contract with which this section is concerned were originally regulated by the CCA alone but are now
regulated both by CCA and FSMA. The definitions of the categories of agreement (hire-purchase, loan etc) were originally
contained in the CCA. The FCA régime proceeds on the basis of defining the activities for which authorisation by the FCA is
required by FSMA and those activities in turn are then defined. In the event, the definitions of the relevant agreements have
been retained but the source of the definitions is no longer the CCA but the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Regulated
Activities) Order 2001 (SI 2001/544) (the RAO). It should be noted that there is a small category of credit agreements, buy-
to-let finance, which are apparently outside the system. In 2015 the UK implemented the EU Mortgage Credit Directive 2014
(Directive 2014/17/EU) 2  by means of the Mortgage Credit Directive Order 2015 (SI 2015/910) (the 2015 Order). This brought
such agreements back into regulation (they had previously been exempt since 2008) but created a parallel regulatory regime
mirroring the control of other credit agreements.

It is important to note that neither the CCA nor FSMA regulates all agreements within each category of agreement. The word
“consumer” in the title of the CCA is misleading: the CCA regulates some agreements where the debtor or hirer is not a consumer
(principally business agreements below the £25,000 credit ceiling). Although, until 6 April 2008 regulation was principally
dependent on the financial amounts involved with (eventually) a ceiling of £25,000, all financial limits were abolished on that
date. Consequently all consumer credit and consumer hire agreements are regulated where:
 
(a)the debtor or hirer is an ‘individual’ or, under the RAO, “an individual or relevant recipient of credit”: in both cases
this is defined as being either a single natural person or a group of not more than three members where no member is a
body corporate;
 
(b)the agreement is not expressly exempted by the CCA or (from 1 April 2014) the RAO.
The CCA’s main provisions will be discussed below. In general, this explanatory section will stick to the CCA’s concepts of
‘creditor’ and ‘debtor’. It must be noted, however, that the nomenclature approved by FSMA and the RAO is ‘lender’ and
‘borrower’. In the precedents, therefore, the new terms will be used.
