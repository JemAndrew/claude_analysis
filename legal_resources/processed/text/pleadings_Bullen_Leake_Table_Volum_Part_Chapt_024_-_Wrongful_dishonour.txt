Chapter 9 - Banking
Wrongful dishonour
9-12
A failure by the bank to honour the payment order where there are sufficient funds (or agreed overdraft facilities) to do so may
result in the bank incurring liability to the customer (i) for breach of contract, and (ii) for defamation.
As to breach of contract, before the Court of Appeal decision in Kpohraror v Woolwich Building Society, 63  the law drew a clear
distinction between a “trader” and a “non-trader”. A customer who is a “trader” is entitled to recover substantial general damages
without proof of special damage. 64  A “non-trader” would have to establish a loss on a particular transaction, and moreover it
would have to be shown that the bank had sufficient knowledge of that transaction to be liable (under ordinary principles of
remoteness). 65  However, in Kpoharor, Evans LJ observed that in modern social conditions it was not only tradesmen for whom
the dishonour of a cheque might be obviously injurious. He said 66  that the credit rating of individuals is important for their
personal transactions, including mortgages, hire-purchases and banking facilities, and it is notorious that central registers are
kept containing information relevant to credit ratings. Hence, there is a factual presumption of some damage in every case. 67
The possibility of a claim for defamation arises out of the reasons for dishonour written by the bank on the cheque. It is customary
for banks to state the reason for the dishonour on the reverse of a cheque presented through general clearing. The words used, if
unjustified, may be defamatory: for example, “not sufficient”, 68  “refer to drawer”, 69  “present again” 70  or “account closed”. 71
