Chapter 3 - Appeals on Points of Law (Section 69)
Introduction
3-01
Appeals against arbitration awards can only be brought: (a) with the agreement of all the other parties to the arbitration; or
(b) with the leave of the court. 1  However, it is open to the parties to agree to exclude the rights of appeal conferred by the
Arbitration Act (the 1996 Act). 2  To that end, an agreement to dispense with the reasons for the award shall be considered an
agreement to exclude the jurisdiction of the courts to entertain any appeals from the award. Appeals can only be brought on
a question of law. 3
Applications for permission to appeal have to brought within 28 days of the date of the award or, if there has been any arbitral
process of appeal or review, of the date when the appellant was first notified of the result of that process. 4  However no appeals
may be brought unless the appellant has first exhausted any available arbitral process of appeal or review and any available
recourse in relation to the correction of awards and the making of additional awards pursuant to s.57. 5  Where there has been
a “material” application for a correction to an arbitral award pursuant to s.57, the 28-day period for making a s.69 appeal runs
from the date of the award as corrected. 6  Appeals have to be notified to the other parties to the arbitral proceedings and also
to the tribunal. 7
Leave to appeal
3-02
Leave to appeal shall only be given by the court if it is satisfied: (a) that the determination of the question will substantially affect
the rights of one or more of the parties 8 ; (b) that the question is one that the tribunal was asked to determine 9 ; (c) that, on the
basis of the findings of fact in the award – (i) the decision of the tribunal on the question of law is obviously wrong, 10  or (ii) the
question of law is one of general public importance and the decision of the tribunal is at least open to serious doubt 11 ; and (d) that
it is just and proper in all the circumstances for the court to determine the question. 12  Once a judge decides that these threshold
conditions are satisfied, then the court has jurisdiction to determine the appeal; issues about public importance, affecting the
rights of parties, and justice and convenience cannot be raised on appeal once leave has been granted. 13  An application for leave
to appeal must identify the question of law to be determined and state the grounds on which leave is sought. 14  When seeking
to establish what has been decided in an arbitral award, the court must read it in “a reasonable and commercial way, expecting,
as is usually the case, that there will be no substantial fault to find with it” and without approaching it “with a meticulous
legal eye endeavouring to pick holes, inconsistencies and faults in awards, and with the objective of frustrating the process of
arbitration”. 15  To the extent that the court cannot determine whether the Tribunal answered the question of law incorrectly on
the face of the Award, the court should remit the Award back to the Tribunal for reconsideration. 16

Procedure
3-03
Applications for permission to appeal against an award should be made by way of an arbitration claim form which: (a) identifies
the question of law; (b) states the grounds on which the award is being challenged; (c) is accompanied by a skeleton argument;
and (d) appends the award. 17  Written evidence in support of the application may be filed only if it is necessary to show that
the statutory requirements have been satisfied. 18  A respondent intending to oppose an application for permission to appeal
must file a respondent’s notice which: (a) sets out the grounds on which the application is opposed; and (b) states whether the
respondent contends that the award should be upheld for reasons not expressed (or not fully expressed) in the award and, if
so, state those reasons. 19  The respondent’s notice must be filed within 21 days after the date on which the respondent was
required to acknowledge service and must be accompanied by a skeleton argument. 20  Written evidence may be filed only if
it is necessary to show that the statutory requirements have not been satisfied. 21  The applicant may file and serve evidence
or argument in reply only if it is necessary to do so; any such evidence or argument must be as brief as possible and must be
filed and served within seven days after service of the respondent’s notice. 22  The “default position” is that applications for
permission to appeal under s.69 are to be dealt with on paper; if there is a parallel s.68 application, then the court may give
directions for oral argument at the hearing of the s.68 application. 23  If permission to appeal under s.69 has been refused, and
the applicant seeks leave to appeal that refusal to the Court of Appeal, the permission to appeal application may be dealt with
on paper by the judge who refused permission to appeal in the first place. 24  Appeals under s.69 are normally heard in public. 25
