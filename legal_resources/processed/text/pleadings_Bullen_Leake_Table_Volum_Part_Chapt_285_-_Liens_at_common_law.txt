Liens at common law
23-02
The person claiming to exercise a lien must be lawfully in possession of the property. Thus, if a buyer rejects goods for which
he has paid, he loses his right to possession of them and, therefore, a lien over them for the return of the price. 2  Certain types of
property are not susceptible to possession: for example, it has been held that an electronic database is not property susceptible
of possession or of being subject of tort of conversion. 3
Possession must be continuous so that if the owner has the right to call for the return of the goods, the lien is lost. 4
There are exceptional circumstances where possession is not necessary to give rise to a legal lien: for example, liens for seamen’s
wages and bottomry bonds.
General liens—examples
23-03
A banker, in the absence of an agreement to the contrary, has a general lien upon the securities of his customers, which are in
his hands as a banker, 5  but this lien may be subject to a trust in favour of a third party where the banker has knowledge of
circumstances giving rise to such a trust. 6
Solicitors have a general lien for their taxable costs, charges and expenses on the deeds and papers of their clients which have
come into their hands in the course of their professional employment. 7
Hotel proprietors, as innkeepers, enjoy a lien upon any property (subject to certain statutory exceptions) brought by a guest in
respect of the guest’s unpaid bill.
A factor has a general lien upon all goods consigned to him as factor for the balance due to him. 8  He does not lose his lien
because he has instructions to sell the goods at a particular price and in the principal’s name. 9
A packer has a general lien upon the goods of his customer which are in his hands. 10  Wharfingers and warehousemen have
similar liens, 11  but not where the transaction shows an intention on the part of both parties inconsistent with such a general
lien. 12  A warehouseman cannot assert a general lien against all goods deposited by a factor in his own name whether his goods
or not. 13
Stockbrokers have a general lien upon the securities of their customers. 14  A similar lien is available to insurance brokers. 15

Particular liens
23-04
Where a party is obliged by law to receive goods, the common law imposes the concomitant obligation to pay for the services
rendered and a lien upon the goods until payment is made. Thus, a common carrier, being bound to carry the goods, has a
particular lien on the goods carried until payment is made. Where there is no obligation to receive goods, there is lien for moneys
spent on improving the goods, but the lien does not extend to labour expended on mere maintenance. 16  To train a horse gives
rise to a lien, but to stable one does not. 17
Particular liens have arisen in the following cases, amongst many others: an accountant upon the books of account of his client;
an architect upon plans drawn by him; an auctioneer for his commission; a conveyancer upon documents prepared by him; a
garage proprietor for a car repaired by him; a printer upon copies of a book printed by him; and a tailor for the price of the suit.
Charterparties often create what is called a “lien” in favour of the shipowner over subfreights. This is not a lien properly called
and is subject to special rules. It is a product of maritime law and does not arise either at common law or by equity. 18
Equitable liens
23-05
An equitable lien arises by operation of law and is not dependent upon possession. It is not possible to state, by reference to a
general rule, the circumstances in which the law imposes an equitable lien. A vendor of land has a legal lien on the land sold
and on documents of title within his or her possession until completion of the transaction. The vendor also has an equitable lien
for the unpaid purchase price of any part of it. It ranks as an overriding interest for the purposes of the Land Registration Act
2002 in a claim for specific performance, the vendor may also expressly plead that he or she is entitled to a lien. 19
Conversely, a purchaser of land who has paid part of the purchase price not only has a lien on the title deeds in his or her
possession but also has an equitable lien on the vendor’s land. The effect is the same as if the vendor had executed a charge
upon the property.
Other examples of equitable liens are found as between partners in relation to the partnership estate and in the case of a
remainderman who has a lien upon profits receivable in the tenant’s hands against the tenant who has committed acts of waste. 20
A lien or charge in favour of a person who expends money for the benefit of or on the property of another may arise if the payer
is under an obligation to spend or is entitled to be indemnified for such expenditure. 21
A person entitled to trace a proprietary right into trust moneys or their proceeds may in appropriate circumstances be entitled
to an equitable lien over those moneys or proceeds. 22
