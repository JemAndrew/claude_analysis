General Note
12-01
This title covers proceedings under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 as amended (FSMA), the legislation which
governs the regulation of financial services. Detailed treatment of this legislation is outside the scope of this work (see Kirk &
Kingham, Encyclopedia of Financial Services Law). 1  FSMA received Royal Assent on 14 June 2000 and for the most part,
was brought into force on 1 December 2000. There have since been substantial additions and amendments.
FSMA set up a single regulatory system for financial sectors and activities (including banking, insurance and investment
business), under the supervision of a single regulator—the Financial Services Authority (FSA). The FSA replaced sector
regulators including the Securities and Investments Board, the Supervision and Surveillance Division of the Bank of England
and the Insurance Directorate of the Treasury.
The financial crisis of 2007–2008, involving the collapse of major banking and ancillary financial services companies,
together with the associated global recession, led to a major reform of the financial regulatory system in the UK. The reform
included the abolition of the FSA and its replacement with two regulators—the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) and the
Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA). This had the effect of separating the conduct (FCA) and prudential (PRA) regulation of
systemically important financial institutions. These changes were brought about by the Financial Services Act 2012 (the 2012
Act), which made substantial amendments to FSMA. In particular, ss.1 to 18 of FSMA were replaced with new ss.1A to 3S. The
2012 Act came into force on 24 January 2013 (but with limited effect in respect of the replacement of ss.1 to 18 of FSMA) and
on 19 February 2013 for the purpose of making appointments to new or existing bodies. 2  Sections 17 and 18 came into force
for the purpose of making rules on 19 March 2013. 3  Generally the balance of the 2012 Act came into force on 1 April 2013. 4
The scheme of the 2012 Act is to define the objectives and functions of the FCA (which was created by renaming the FSA), 5
and to allot certain objectives and functions to the PRA by the deletion of Pt I of FSMA and the insertion of a new Pt 1A. The
PRA was established as a subsidiary of the Bank of England but has since (from 1 March 2017) been absorbed into the Bank
of England. 6  The PRA is responsible for the prudential regulation and supervision of banks, building societies, credit unions,
insurers and major investment firms. Those functions which are allotted to the PRA are generally not relevant to this title.
12-02
The FCA is responsible for the conduct regulation of all financial services firms and the prudential regulation of those firms
not regulated by the PRA (including all other investment firms, insurance brokers and financial advisors). The FCA has the
“strategic objective” of “ensuring that the relevant markets function well” (FSMA s.1B(2)). The “relevant markets” are defined
in s.1F and include the financial markets and the markets for regulated financial services. 7  In addition to its strategic objective,
the FCA has the “operational objectives” of securing an appropriate degree of protection for consumers (FSMA s.1C), protecting
and enhancing the integrity of the UK financial system (FSMA s.1D) and promoting effective competition in the interests of
consumers in the markets for regulated financial services or certain services provided by recognised investment exchanges
(FSMA s.1E). The FCA’s “general functions” 8  include making rules, issuing codes and giving general guidance under FSMA. 9
The FCA’s rules and guidance are contained in its Handbook of Rules and Guidance (the FCA Handbook).
As regards the carrying on of financial services business in the UK, the primary enactment in FSMA is contained in s.19. It
provides that “no person may carry on a regulated activity in the United Kingdom, or purport to do so, unless he is (a) an
authorised person; or (b) an exempt person”. This is called “the general prohibition”. It came into force on 1 December 2001 10
