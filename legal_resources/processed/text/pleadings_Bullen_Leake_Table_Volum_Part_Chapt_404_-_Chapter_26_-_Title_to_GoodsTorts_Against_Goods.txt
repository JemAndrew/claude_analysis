Chapter 26 - Title to Goods—Torts Against Goods
Wrongful interference with goods
26-01
There is no single tort that deals with the several methods by which a person’s interest in his personal property may be damaged
or interfered with. The interference may operate to prejudice the owner’s title to his goods or his right to possession of them,
as well as their loss or destruction.
An attempt at rationalising some aspects of the law was made with the Torts (Interference with Goods) Act 1977 but it did not
seek a systematic reform. Section 1 of the Act coined a new description for torts affecting goods by introducing the expression
“wrongful interference with goods” but no new tort was created. Instead, the following existing torts were included:
 
(a)conversion;
 
(b)trespass to goods;
 
(c)negligence so far as it resulted in damage to goods or to an interest in goods;
 
(d)any other tort so far as it results in damage to goods or to an interest in goods.
One of the principal changes in the law was to abolish detinue as a separate tort and to incorporate it within the tort of conversion.
At the time of its demise, however, detinue had been restricted in its application to the case of a bailee in possession of goods
who permitted them to be lost or destroyed in breach of his duty of care over them.
