Chapter 4 - Early Neutral Evaluation
Overview
4-001
Early neutral evaluation (ENE) is a tool to help the parties resolve their disputes without having to move to a binding stage
(being litigation or arbitration). The purpose of ENE is to reduce litigation costs by addressing disputes early, focusing on
discovery and facilitating effective negotiations. Unlike traditional litigation, ENE is designed to save time and resources by
using a third-party to identify the core issues at stake, and assess the strengths and weaknesses of each party’s position, and
set the stage for potential resolution. In itself, it will not resolve a dispute but has the potential to help the parties adjust their
expectations and to narrow the gap between their positions. The main difference between ENE and expert determination (which
will be discussed in Chapter Five) is that, in ENE, the independent evaluator is not being asked to make a decision but to give
an opinion on the merits of a potential claim/dispute.
The parties ask an independent third-party evaluator to hear their competing positions and to provide an assessment of the
relative merits of each side’s case. Although an evaluator is not required to, it is possible for the parties to request that the
evaluator provides an evaluation of the likely outcome if the issue put before them went to a binding dispute resolution stage.
The development of ENE as an identifiable process stems from a judge in California who, in 1982, appointed a task force to
which he gave one objective: to determine if there are ways the court can help make litigation less expensive. 1  A committee
analysed the cost of litigation to identify where most money was spent and the underlying causes of the cost problem. This
committee reached the conclusion that the place where most money could be saved was in the early stages of litigation.
The committee found that it was in those stages that patterns and expectations were set and concluded that an “infusion of
intellectual discipline, common sense, and more direct communication” 2  might have the most beneficial effect. A programme
was put together and received positive feedback from various test cases. In this programme, the success of ENE hinged on
experienced evaluators providing unbiased assessments, focusing discovery on key issues, and fostering an environment that
is conducive to settlement.
Effectiveness and benefits
4-002
Whilst the benefits of ENE may seem obvious, either party’s lawyers may resist including an agreement for ENE in a contract
or, in the absence of a clause mandating ENE, may be sceptical about the value of ENE and the potential for evaluators to be
perceived as biased. A possible reason for a lawyer resisting the use of ENE include where they believe their client has a weak
case and, as a result, they fear that ENE could expose that weakness. In addition, if a lawyer believes that the other party may
not negotiate in good faith, they could conclude that ENE is a waste of time and resources. Unfortunately, if a party were to
receive advice from their lawyer that they should not engage in ENE, the practical effect would be that ENE is then unlikely
to be used. The effectiveness can also be reduced if the parties are not fully committed to the process or if the evaluator lacks
the necessary expertise in the relevant area of law.
The development of ENE showed that there was a positive impact on participants, including reductions in the time and cost
that were (or would have been) associated with litigation. Participants particularly appreciated the opportunity for early case

assessment, which, in turn, facilitated more informed decision making regarding settlement versus continued litigation. One of
the major benefits of ENE is that it can help clarify the issues whilst, at the same time, improving communication between the
parties. In themselves, these simple benefits often lead to early dispute resolution.
As with mediation, the biggest factors that can reduce the benefits of ENE are the lack of engagement of all parties involved,
as well as the expertise and neutrality of the evaluator.
In addition to significantly reducing the time and cost of commercial litigation by encouraging early settlement, ENE also
provides a confidential setting where parties can openly discuss their positions without the formalities and pressures of court
proceedings. This environment helps facilitate open communication and more realistic assessments. In turn, this can increase
the prospects for developing innovative solutions tailored to the specific needs of the disputing parties.
From its development in the 1980’s, ENE has received recognition for its efficiency and cost-effectiveness, embodying the
principle that a timely intervention can prevent more extensive and expensive litigation. This approach aligns with the adage
“a stitch in time saves nine”, suggesting that early expert input can lead to substantial time and money savings.
The process is designed to be flexible, allowing for its application at virtually any stage prior to the final hearing. It can be
effectively applied to commercial disputes where the complexities and stakes are high. However, whilst it can be used at any
time, it is most helpful when employed early in the dispute resolution process.
Given the nature of the role of the evaluator, even at its early stage of development, generative artificial intelligence (AI) has
enormous potential to enhance the concept of ENE. As is discussed in detail in Chapter Ten, this is because AI excels in learning
from large data sets and has the capacity to provide advice that, behind the scenes, is based on a statistical analysis of previous
published cases including reference to all relevant statutes, regulations and previous judgments. Having done that, AI has the
potential capacity to produce a list of the issues and identify what has to be proven/disproved by either party in order to win. As
such, it is highly likely that the future of ENE in dispute resolution will be enhanced by AI with both cost and time being reduced.
The use of generative AI tools also brings the potential for either party to run their own AI-based ENE without any engagement
with the other party (see Chapter Ten on AI). The use of AI in this way can help inform a party’s negotiation and/or litigation
strategy and, at the same time, give the party a sense check of their position and, in so doing, manage expectations.
When to apply?
4-003
Ideally, ENE should take place at an early stage of a dispute but, in principle, ENE is a tool that can be employed at any stage of
the dispute resolution process. Whenever it is applied, it is important for both parties to be clear (and agree) what the primary
issues are and whether there are any disputes of fact. As such, the benefits of ENE can be reduced if it is used at the very start
of a dispute before any real negotiation has begun or the real issues in dispute have been identified. This is because, without
identifying the issues, the expert might inadvertently be asked to address issues that turn out not to be contentious or, indeed,
change as the parties continue their efforts to resolve the dispute.
From a practical perspective, once negotiations have started and the parties have effectively declared their positions, it can be
highly beneficial to apply an ENE stage at any point before binding procedures start.
There is no automatic reason for ENE to be linked to mediation but, if mediation is to be attempted, ENE can be particularly
helpful prior to starting the mediation process. Conversely, if mediation was attempted before ENE but settlement is not
achieved, there is still a chance that mediation will have narrowed the issues or gap in the dispute. In this case, having helped
the parties to determine what it is they can agree on and what is disputed, a new understanding of the outstanding issues (that
can arise from the mediation) can still feed into a final ENE stage prior to moving to a binding stage if unresolved.

The process
4-004
Once the key issues have been identified, a neutral evaluator, being an expert in the subject matter of the dispute, needs to
be chosen by mutual agreement of the parties or through a process designated in the dispute resolution clause of a contract.
It is essential for the scope of the evaluator’s work to be defined and any questions worded carefully to ensure that, after the
evaluation has been provided, a party does not say “ah, but the evaluator did not consider x, y, or z”.
The parties need to be clear that the role of the evaluator is to provide a frank, non-binding assessment of the merits of the case,
potential outcomes and areas for settlement.
Having provided the evaluator with a case summary identifying the issues, each party then presents its case to the evaluator,
outlining the key facts, legal arguments and evidence supporting their position. This presentation can take various forms,
including written submissions, oral presentations, or a combination of both. The process is designed to be less formal and
adversarial than traditional litigation.
After consideration of the facts and any legal submissions, the evaluator then provides a non-binding opinion based on their
assessment of the strengths and weaknesses of each party’s case. This opinion may include insights into the likely outcome if
the dispute were to proceed to formal binding proceedings.
Once the evaluation is received, the parties need to consider how this impacts on their willingness to settle. There remains a real
risk that, if one party is deeply entrenched in their position, they might find it psychologically difficult to accept the conclusions.
In the extreme, an evaluation may be provided that effectively says to one party: “You are in the right and will win”, but for
the other party to still insist on going to court.
This unwillingness to change position is more likely to surface if there is an imbalance in power and especially where one
party has deeper pockets. This is an extension of the well-known strategy of a defendant/respondent delaying and extending
proceedings in the hope that the opposing party runs out of money, or some other critical time event happens.
The parties need to be aware that, even if ENE is in their favour, an opposing party may still not be willing to settle. As good
practice, similar to the requirements for a successful structured mediation, the parties should ideally confirm their willingness
to change position if an ENE highlights particular strengths or weaknesses on either side of the dispute.
By default, the discussions and opinions shared during the ENE process are confidential, encouraging open communication
between the parties. It is also normal to include an agreement that the report of the evaluator is without prejudice to any
subsequent binding proceedings and will not be seen by a judge or arbitrator. This does not stop the evaluation being used
to help frame the subsequent submissions of either party and it is normal for this to find its way into skeleton arguments or
position statements.
Implementing ENE requires careful planning and consideration of the specific dynamics of each case. Lawyers must prepare
by gathering and presenting evidence effectively, formulating clear arguments and being open to the evaluator’s feedback. The
choice of an evaluator is critical; their expertise and impartiality can significantly influence the process’s effectiveness.
The parties have flexibility about how to conduct the ENE process and, provided they stay focused on the overall reason for
running an ENE stage, can merge or skip some of the steps.
The key requirements for effective application of ENE are:
 
1.Initiate ENE at the earliest stage of the dispute to prevent the escalation of conflict and costs. Early involvement facilitates
a more open dialogue between the parties and sets a cooperative tone for resolution.
 
2.Choose an evaluator with extensive expertise in commercial law and specific knowledge relevant to the dispute. The
evaluator’s impartiality, experience and reputation are crucial for credibility and acceptance of their feedback.
 
3.Both parties submit briefs to the evaluator detailing their view of the case facts, legal issues and arguments. A challenge is
that these submissions need to be concise yet comprehensive, allowing the evaluator to understand the dispute’s core issues.

4.Conduct an initial meeting with the evaluator to outline the ENE process, discuss confidentiality agreements and set
expectations for the evaluation. Whilst the parties should ideally have already agreed the process and confidentiality
requirements, by sharing these in the presence of the evaluator, this step ensures all parties are aligned.
 
5.The next stage is the evaluation session which involves the parties presenting their case, including key evidence and
arguments. In order for the process to be effective, the parties should be prepared for the evaluator to ask questions of
clarification. At this stage, in order to maintain confidence in their independence, it is important that the evaluator takes
care in framing the questions to avoid creating a sense of bias.
 
6.The evaluator provides an unbiased assessment of the dispute, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of each party’s
position. If the parties have agreed and the evaluator is instructed, the assessment can also include predictions on likely
outcomes in litigation, potential areas for compromise and recommendations for settlement.
The formal part of ENE ends when the expert provides their evaluation. For the process to be of any benefit, after receiving
the evaluator’s assessment, the parties then normally engage (or re-engage) in negotiations to explore settlement options. If
requested, the evaluator can also facilitate these discussions, helping to identify mutually beneficial solutions and encourage
compromise. However, at this stage, the role of the evaluator effectively shifts to that of a mediator. Care needs to be taken in
deciding whether to ask the evaluator to do this as the skills required of an effective mediator are different. As the evaluator
will have been chosen for their knowledge of the area of dispute, they may not have the skills necessary to act as a mediator.
Depending on the outcome of the negotiations that follow the ENE, if there are still unresolved issues, a follow-up discussion
(or mediation) may be necessary. This can address the unresolved issues, refine settlement terms, and/or explore alternative
dispute resolution methods if a settlement is not reached.
If a settlement is achieved, the agreement needs to be recorded in a legally binding contract. As with negotiated or mediated
settlements, if proceedings have not yet started, this settlement contract is not the same as a court order or an arbitration consent
award but will be considered to be a stand-alone contract/settlement agreement. The fact that resolution has been facilitated by
ENE does not affect the requirement for a formal legally enforceable agreement.
As enforceability arises from the contractual nature of the settlement agreement, if required, enforcement would require a
separate claim. All is not lost if no settlement is reached with the help of ENE as the evaluator may provide a written summary
of the assessment and recommendations for future actions. A further potential benefit is that, even if not resolved, the evaluation
process may have led to the parties narrowing the issues that may ultimately need to be placed before a court or tribunal.
If an ENE process leads to a successful outcome for parties in an ongoing business relationship, they should consider reviewing
how it worked and considering options to improve its effectiveness and/or areas for improvement that may enhance the process
in the event of any future disputes.
If ENE does not lead directly to a successful outcome, it still has the potential to clarify each party’s position with regard to the
evaluation of issues put to the evaluator. Whether this leads to further non-binding dispute resolution steps will depend on the
circumstances but, if the decision to use ENE arose from a multi-tier escalation clause, the next step will have been determined
by the clause and is likely to include mediation and/or arbitration as the next or final steps.
Benefits
4-005
One of the primary benefits of ENE is that it can facilitate a quicker resolution compared to binding proceedings. It is important
to note that, whilst ENE is not an alternative to mediation, if the dispute lends itself to such an evaluation, it has the potential to
bring the parties closer without the cost of mediation. However, it should be noted that the costs of ENE are likely to be higher
than mediation and this needs to be balanced against the benefit of reducing the level of tension that can arise if parties need to
change their position or compromise without any factual justification. In any event, by narrowing the issues early on, there is
a good chance that the parties may save on the costs associated with lengthy litigation.

A significant benefit of ENE can be where one side is being unrealistic about its chances of success at trial. A neutral evaluator
can highlight weaknesses in a case that would be fully exposed if the matter were litigated. Having said that, if unrealistic
expectations are driven by emotions and/or beliefs, then it is possible that even a definitive evaluator’s report highlighting that
a party is likely to “lose”, may not affect that party’s willingness to settle.
Whatever the outcome, given the softer, less adversarial nature of ENE, a material secondary benefit is that it can help maintain
working relationships between parties, which is particularly important in commercial settings.
Issues
Voluntary participation
4-006
Given that ENE requires both parties to voluntarily participate, if the use of ENE is not prescribed (e.g. in a dispute resolution
clause) and either party is unwilling, the process may not be effective. However, the impact of unwillingness to participate
is not the same as in mediation. This is because, for a mediation to be successful, active engagement and concessions by
both parties is required. In contrast, in ENE, it is possible for an evaluation to take place based on documentary evidence
and limited submissions.
In the same way that a lawyer could be asked to provide a confidential opinion or advice to an instructing party without any
input from the opposing party, an ENE evaluator could provide their evaluation to both parties having received limited input
from one party. Whilst this is not likely to be accepted by the reluctant party, there is still a good chance that it will serve to
reset expectations and, as a result, bring a potential settlement closer.
Absence of full disclosure
4-007
As ENE is normally attempted early in the dispute resolution process and is not governed by formal regulations, compared
to litigation, ENE is likely to start without full disclosure and relatively limited discovery. This gives rise to the possibility
that an evaluator could be instructed to make their assessment without access to all the facts.
As a result, there is a risk that when the evaluation is provided, a party who feels that the evaluation is against them could
effectively refuse to accept it and thereby eliminate the primary benefit of ENE in closing the gap. If the parties genuinely
wish to settle without going through a binding process, in order to mitigate this potential risk, it is important that they agree
that all relevant issues have been identified and relevant information has been disclosed.
Perception of bias
4-008
As with any non-binding ADR process, the risk of a perception of bias affects the prospects of success. In the circumstances
of a binding proceedings, be it arbitration or litigation, this can cause parties to consider an appeal. In the case of ENE, it
will inevitably lead to the party who perceives bias to be tempted to ignore the evaluation and not to change their position.
In order to maximise the benefit of ENE, it is important that every effort is made to ensure that the evaluator is unbiased
and, equally importantly, is seen to be unbiased.

Apart from standard checks of previous connections between the evaluator and either of the parties, an important requirement

Types of disputes suitable for ENE
4-009
The typical application areas cover a range of disputes including supply contracts, construction, engineering, information
technology, insurance, rent reviews and valuation disputes. The common theme that makes these areas suitable is that they
can give rise to disputes where the matrix of facts is agreed and the barrier(s) to settlement include a specialised or technical
issue which the parties interpret differently. Similarly, any dispute where there is a disagreement over the application of the
law, lends itself to judicial ENE.
ENE can be particularly effective in circumstances where the parties have widely differing views of the merits of their case. In
these situations, ENE has the potential to provide a reality check and encourage more realistic settlement discussions.
Types of disputes less suitable for ENE
4-010
Independent of the commercial area, ENE is not suitable in circumstances where there are material disputes over facts that
would require the court or tribunal to conduct a fact-find in order to determine the outcome. Depending on the nature of the
disputed facts, it would be possible to ask an evaluator to make an assessment on the basis of different factual scenarios, but
this would provide no real benefit in bringing the parties closer to a resolution.
ENE is also not suitable for highly emotional disputes. Whilst the stereotypical view is that these off-limits cases are likely to
be areas similar to family law cases, it is remarkably common to find that commercial disputes are also filled with emotion,
with parties being driven by wanting their day in court and not being prepared to reach a pragmatic commercial solution.
It is also worth noting that positions can become polarised over time to the point that parties’ recollections of events evolve
in ways that reflect a repeatedly stated position rather than an accurate recollection of events. This important issue of memory
fallibility is usefully discussed in the English law case of Gestmin v Credit Suisse. 4
Drawing up an ENE agreement
4-011
An important characteristic that ENE has in common with other ADR processes is that, largely speaking, the parties have
complete flexibility over the process. However, if they cannot agree, this potential benefit can quickly turn it a problem. As
a result, it is important that, having appointed an evaluator, all the steps in the process are clear both to the parties and to the
evaluator.
An experienced evaluator should be able to provide a template confirming the process and the parties’ respective roles. The
process can also be agreed (or modified) through discussions involving both parties and the evaluator. To reduce the risk of
spin-off disagreements, the agreement should be as detailed as possible.
The non-contentious essential elements of such an agreement should include: 5
 
• the confidential and without prejudice nature of the process;

Incorporating an ENE clause into a contract
4-012
In principle, an ENE clause can be incorporated into a multi-tier dispute resolution clause in the same way as negotiation and
mediation. However, because it is flexible and because it can usefully fit anywhere from negotiation to the point where the
escalation clause reaches its final binding stage, it is very difficult to determine the trigger and where it should be placed.
As such, it is recommended not to include ENE as a required element in a contractual dispute resolution clause but for the clause
to refer to ENE more as a reminder to the parties to consider it if appropriate.
A simple standalone ENE clause that serves to remind parties could be worded like this:
“The parties agree that before initiating any formal dispute resolution process (such as litigation or
arbitration), they will consider participating in Early Neutral Evaluation (ENE) in a good faith attempt to
resolve the dispute. The ENE process will be conducted in accordance with [specify any relevant rules or
procedures].”
What is the outcome of using ENE?
4-013
Although the evaluator will assess the case and comment on the merits, they have no power to impose any settlement and cannot
force the parties to accept any proposed settlement terms.
The process is usually conducted without prejudice, meaning the outcome cannot be disclosed to the courts without the consent
of both parties. Despite this, the analysis made by the evaluator can be used by the parties to come to a settlement privately
or used in mediation following the evaluation. Although it cannot be shown to court or a tribunal without the parties’ consent,
as mediation is non-binding, there is no conceptual barrier to the parties showing the mediator a copy of the evaluation and
summarising their position regarding the points raised.
The key objective of the ENE process is to provide the parties with an impartial appraisal regarding the strengths and weaknesses
of their case in the hope that this will help the parties adjust their position and close the gap.

Risks v benefits
4-014
It is key for the parties to understand that the primary benefit of ENE is that it is a quick process that helps parties evaluate
the risks in pursuing a litigation or arbitration claim and, as a result of identifying weaknesses in their cases and gaps in their
evidence, it can motivate parties to enter into settlement discussions or, equally valuable, it can encourage parties to adjust
their starting points.
Having narrowed the gap, whilst the ENE is confidential, this also has the potential to narrow the issues that need to be considered
in a court or tribunal by identifying the central issues in the dispute.
The main risk of an ENE is that a clear-cut outcome might make one party less willing to compromise. In turn, this could reduce
the scope for settlement through negotiation. However, even if this risk exists, it is important to note that the evaluation might
accurately predict the outcome of a costly legal process. Understanding that risk could still motivate an out-of-court settlement,
saving everyone time and money spent on legal fees.
