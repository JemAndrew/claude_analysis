Introduction
9-01
The modern law of banking is the product of a plethora of interwoven sources. Ancient common law principles have been
supplemented by statutory interventions, from the Bills of Exchange Act 1882 to the modern-day Financial Services and
Markets Act 2000. There are in addition layers of EU directives, domestic regulatory rules and guidance (in particular those
promulgated by the Financial Conduct Authority), and voluntary codes of conduct. The landscape has been recently altered by
a wave of litigation in the aftermath of the 2008 financial crisis. The practical issues facing banking lawyers have also changed
significantly: the days where banking law was dominated by issues over cheques and other bills of exchange are gone, and
modern litigation now involves a wide spectrum of claims, from the mis-selling of derivatives and other financial products,
to claims involving fraud and money laundering. Nevertheless, it remains important to understand the fundamental nature of
the relationship between the banker and customer, the respective obligations between them, and the nature of various types
of bank accounts.

The narrative in the following paragraphs cannot be, and is not intended to comprise, an exposition of the law of banking.
Reference should be made the comprehensive texts available, including Paget’s Law of Banking (15th edn), the Encyclopaedia
of Banking Law (a looseleaf subscription which is regularly revised and updated), Brindle & Cox, Law of Bank Payments (5th
edition), Hewetson and Elliott, Banking Litigation (4th edition), and Ellinger’s Modern Banking Law (5th edition). 1
Reference should also be made to the further chapters in this banking and financial services section, dealing with bills of
exchange, bonds, the lending of money, guarantees and mortgages, financial services, and the stock exchange, as well as Ch.20
on credit, finance and leasing. It should be noted in particular that since 2008 there has been no financial limit on the definition
of a regulated consumer credit agreement, such that, subject to specified exceptions, all credit agreements involving money
advanced to individuals are now regulated agreements under the Consumer Credit Act 1974.
The relationship between banker and customer
9-03
The essential relationship between a banker and its customer is contractual, not fiduciary. 2  Obligations are owed on both sides
of the contractual arrangement, the fundamental obligations of the bank being to honour its customer’s instructions and to repay
on demand monies deposited in an account. Other obligations may be come into existence by virtue of specific arrangements.
For example, the parties may enter into a loan agreement or overdraft facility, or the bank may agree to provide certain specific

services with concomitant obligations, for example in relation to the provision of banker’s drafts, letters of credit or other
instruments, or the provision of foreign currency services, or advisory services.
Absent such special arrangements, terms are rarely to be implied into the underlying banker/customer contract. 3  So for example,
there is not normally an implied term in the contract between bank and customer obliging the bank to provide overdraft facilities
or other finances to its customer, 4  nor is there an implied obligation upon the bank to inform its customers of the introduction
of more advantageous forms of account 5  or to give a period of notice to the customer before withholding further finances. 6
Equally, there is in general no implied obligation upon the bank to advise its customer that particular borrowing is or may be
imprudent. 7  On the other, a bank does owe a statutory duty to perform such services as it is providing with skill and care,
by virtue of s.13 of the Supply of Goods and Services Act 1982. It is also under an implied obligation to keep its customer’s
affairs confidential. 8
Banks have been found to owe tortious duties of care to customers in a variety of factual circumstances, including the following:
 
(1)A duty not to honour a payment instruction where the bank is on notice of a potential fraud: see para.9-11.
 
(2)Where a bank gives financial advice to a customer, for example in relation to derivatives or security documents, it has
a duty to provide its advice with reasonable skill and care: see para.9-21.
 
(3)When providing a reference for a customer, the bank owes a duty of care and faces potential liability for negligent
misstatements. 9
In general, the bank does not owe a duty of care to third parties, such as the customer’s own clients. 10  It has been held that
a bank which fails to honour a payment instruction owes no duty of care to the intended beneficiary of the payment. 11  A
bank notified of a freezing order does not owe a duty in tort to the party that has obtained the order to take care to prevent the
dissipation of money in the frozen account. 12
