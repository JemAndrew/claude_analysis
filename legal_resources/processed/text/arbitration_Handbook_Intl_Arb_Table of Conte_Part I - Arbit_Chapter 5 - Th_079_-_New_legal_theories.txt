New legal theories
22-102
The basic principle is that the parties should have a reasonable opportunity to establish the factual and legal allegations on
which it bases its affirmative case and its defence. In approving the Rules, the Commission inserted reference to a “reasonable”
opportunity to underline the fact that the requirement is not absolute. This basic principle applies with respect to arguments
raised by the other party as well as legal reasoning of the Tribunal. For example, a Tribunal may raise legal issues of its own
initiative. However, before deciding such issues, in many legal systems it must provide the parties with an opportunity to
comment on those legal theories. 282
22-103
This has been upheld in various cases before the French courts. In one case under the CRCICA Rules, the Tribunal (which was
in fact a truncated Tribunal) issued an award based on provisions of the Egyptian Civil Code that had not been referred to by
the parties. The Paris Court of Appeal held that the award was not enforceable in France. 283  A similar approach is taken in
Switzerland (but only if the legal argument could not have reasonably been foreseen by the parties) 284  and Hong Kong. 285
22-104
In England, a party sought to annul an award on the basis that the other party had only very briefly mentioned rescission as one
of its claims and that therefore the other party did not have an adequate opportunity to deal with it. The English court rejected
the annulment application stating that the matter had been referred to in the submission and it was up to the other side to decide
how and how much detail to deal with it. 286
