Action for breach of condition or warranty
Title and quiet possession
25-23
The basic rule of the SGA is that there is an implied condition on the part of the seller that, in the case of a sale, he has the
right to sell the goods and, in the case of an agreement to sell, that he will have that right at the time when property is to pass
(SGA s.12(1)). There are warranties that the goods are free from all charges and encumbrances unless disclosed by the seller
or otherwise known to the buyer and that the buyer will enjoy quiet possession of the goods (SGA s.12(2)). There are similar
warranties applying to sales where the seller is transferring only such title as he or a third party may have (SGA s.12(3), (4)
and (5)).
The condition in SGA s.12(1) is regarded as fundamental by the courts. If it turns out, long after the goods have been delivered,
that the seller had no right to sell, the buyer may treat the contract as repudiated and recover back the price and he need give
no allowance for the value of any use of the goods he may have enjoyed in the meantime. 12
If between the sale and the buyer’s decision to avoid the contract under SGA s.12(1), a seller who had no title at the time of
sale acquires title, this will “feed title” to the buyer and preclude him from treating the contract as repudiated by the seller. 13
This is an area where the law for consumer sales has been replaced by the CRA. As the CRA is legislating for all contracts
where the ownership or possession of goods is transferred, it necessarily has to adopt a global approach. CRA s.17 compulsorily
imports into every supply contract a term that “the trader must have the right to sell or transfer the goods at the time when
ownership of the goods is to be transferred”—s.17(1)(b)—unless the circumstances show that the contract is to transfer the
limited title of the trader or of a third party—s.17(4). There is a term that the goods are free from any charge or encumbrance
not disclosed to the buyer and that the buyer will enjoy quiet possession—s.17(2). This term cannot be excluded—CRA s.31.
