International systems from a procedural point of view
18-2
ICC arbitration is a private form of justice, which is always linked to a national legal system, particularly as regards the conduct
of the proceedings and the enforcement of the arbitral award. 26  However, international arbitration is not necessarily linked to
a national legal system as regards the substantive law that the Tribunal applies to determine the merits of the case. 27
18-3
The main link between arbitral proceedings and a national legal system is through the place of arbitration. The courts of the
place of arbitration are the courts that deal with issues such as difficulties in the constitution of the Tribunal, challenges to
arbitrators, requirements of due process, the annulment and sometimes also enforcement of arbitral awards and remission of
the award to the Tribunal. 28  As expressly foreseen in French arbitration law, they are the supporting court (juge d’appui) for
international arbitrations taking place in France. Article V(1)(a) of the New York Convention requires that an award complies
with “the law of the country where the Award was made”, thus rendering the law of the place of arbitration relevant to the
enforcement proceedings in another country.
18-4
Under the New York Convention, it is important to determine the place of arbitration as art.I(1) of that Convention sets out
the basic rule that it applies to “arbitral awards made in the territory of a State other than the state where the recognition and
enforcement of such awards are sought” 29  and art.I(3) of the New York Convention provides that “any State may on the basis
of reciprocity declare that it will apply the Convention to the recognition and enforcement of awards made only in the territory
of another Contracting State”. 30
18-5
Although it is a main link between private ICC arbitration and a legal system, the role of the law of the place of arbitration has
evolved over time. In major centres of arbitration, the relevant arbitration law does not seek to prescribe or unduly influence
the method in which the proceedings are conducted. Instead, the law of the place of arbitration sets out minimum procedural
standards for the arbitrators and the procedure and certain principles that are applicable to the jurisdiction of the Tribunal.
18-6
However, as discussed beginning at para.6-6, the place of arbitration may have a role in determining the law applicable to the
arbitration agreement. Where the place of arbitration is in France, French courts will generally assume that it is the principles
applicable under French law that are applicable to the arbitration agreement, notwithstanding the fact that the contract of which
it is part is subject to another law. 31  Under Swiss law, an arbitration agreement is valid if valid under the governing law of the
contract or under Swiss law if the place of arbitration is in Switzerland. 32  Under English law, the situation is more complex as
the governing law of the contract will generally be considered applicable to the arbitration agreement but this can be overridden
for example where the place of arbitration is in another jurisdiction and that jurisdiction requires that the arbitration agreement
be subject to the law of that jurisdiction or if the arbitration agreement would be significantly undermined if governed by the
law governing the contract. 33
18-7
The place of arbitration or “where the Award was made” is a legal concept. It provides the seat of the Tribunal and, under art.32(3)
it is the place where the award was “deemed to be made”. The hearings, deliberations and the signing of the arbitral award

may all occur at the place of arbitration, in particular if all relevant participants reside there. However, these more frequently
take place in various jurisdictions. Regarding the conduct of physical hearings, the starting point for determining the place of
substantive hearings is the place of arbitration. With the surge of virtual hearings following the global outbreak of the Covid-19
pandemic in 2020, it became impossible or at least impractical to hold physical meetings or hearings at the place of arbitration.
Virtual hearing protocols established by Tribunals in agreement, or at least in consultation, with the parties, often mention that
the virtual hearing or meeting is deemed to take place at the place of arbitration, or that the fact that the hearing is taking place
virtually does not affect the seat of the arbitration.
18-8
The influence of the law of the place of arbitration is also felt indirectly as it may well affect the procedural approach chosen by
the arbitrators under art.19 even if it does not amount to a choice of that law as the procedural law. 34  However, as noted in the
discussion with respect to that article, the weight that the law of the place of arbitration should have is a matter of discussion.
18-9
The approach of national courts to ICC arbitration varies considerably. There has also been an evolution of the case law in many
national courts to further support arbitration. Therefore, in applying the ICC Rules, it is essential to keep in mind the principles
applicable under the law of the place of arbitration and the legal principles applicable at the place of enforcement. The most
striking aspect is the approach of national courts to awards that have been annulled at the place of arbitration. In France, for
example, annulment at the place of arbitration as such has no effect on the award and the French courts review whether, under
French legal principles, the award should be enforced or not notwithstanding the fact that it has been annulled. 35  The US courts
have tended in the same direction, although it now appears that they will only do so in exceptional cases. On 2 August 2016, the
United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit affirmed a lower court decision confirming an arbitral award rendered
in Mexico. The award was confirmed notwithstanding that it had been annulled by a Mexican court. The court stated that it:
“exercised its discretion in confirming the award because giving effect to the subsequent nullification of the award
in Mexico would run counter to United States public policy and would (in the operative phrasing) be ‘repugnant
to fundamental notions of what is decent and just’ in this country.” 36
The English courts have also rejected recently the existence of the “ex nihilo nil fit” principle. 37  In other countries, it is not
possible to enforce an award annulled at the place of arbitration. In Brazil, for instance, the Brazil Superior Tribunal of Justice
considered in a 2015 decision that “… due to the fact that the award was annulled in Argentina by a court decision issued in
that country, it is not possible to have it recognized in Brazil”. 38

In 2012, the major countries in ICC arbitration were France (101), Switzerland (122), the UK (71), the USA (41) and Singapore
(36). Singapore is a UNCITRAL Model Law jurisdiction as are other countries that have also been widely used for ICC
arbitration such as Germany (19/21). 40  In 2017 the major countries in ICC arbitration were France (121), Switzerland (90),
followed by the UK (73), the USA (51), Singapore (38), Brazil (28), and Germany (24). 41  In 2020, the order was slightly
changed with Switzerland (104) coming first, followed by France (88), the USA (88) and the United Kingdom (85); and other
countries have also been widely used for ICC arbitration such as Brazil (30), the United Arab Emirates (27), Singapore (26),
Qatar (22), the Netherlands (21), China-Hong-Kong (19), Mexico (18) and Germany (16). 42
18-12
Parties usually agree upon the place of arbitration. Pursuant to the ICC statistics, in 2012 and 2020, they did so in 90% of cases,
leaving the ICC Court to fix the place of arbitration for only 10% of the cases under art.18(1). 43  In 2020, the ICC Court decided
to fix the place of arbitration in the USA in 15 cases, in France in eight cases and in the United Arab Emerates in seven cases. 44

18-14
The list of places of arbitration is noteworthy with respect to various countries. The large number of arbitrations in France,
Switzerland and the UK reflects not only historic tradition and their reputations as centres of arbitration, but also the fact that
in many European countries the use of ICC arbitration clauses continues to be widespread. In the case of France, where Paris is
mostly chosen as place of arbitration, its choice can easily be explained by the presence of the ICC in Paris. In many contract
negotiations, Paris appears to be an easy compromise for the parties for that reason. As to Switzerland, its reputation of neutrality
and the large pool of experienced arbitration lawyers remain key factors in the choice of its cities as places of arbitration. As
to the UK and in particular London, one can think of many factors in addition to its role as major business and finance centre,
such as the excellency of its legal profession, its geographic location and simply the fact that English is spoken in that city.
The liberal arbitration laws and generally rather arbitration-friendly approach of the courts in these countries help parties in
making these choices.

law 46  and that may explain why the number of cases in which the parties have chosen Brazil have increased. 47
