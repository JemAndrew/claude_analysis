Consumer Credit
19-08
In the last edition we recorded that this area of the law had undergone a major upheaval with the transfer of regulation from
the Office of Fair Trading to the FCA and the incorporation of large areas of the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 into
consumer credit law. Happily the only change to report in this area is the implementation of the EU Mortgage Credit Directive
2014 (Directive 2014/17/EU) 1  by means of the Mortgage Credit Directive Order 2015 (SI 2015/910). This essentially takes
buy-to-let finance out of one set of regulation (the CCA) and gives it its own separate (but virtually identical) regulatory régime.
For details, reference should be made to Section 20: Credit, Finance and Leasing.
Changes to pre-action protocols
19-09
This section of the work was largely unaffected by the rules relating to pre-action protocols but on 1 October 2017, the ‘Pre-
Action Protocol for Debt Claims’ came into force (also known as Protocol XIII), which requires a highly elaborate pre-action
protocol to be sent in debt-collection cases. The protocol applies: “to any business (including sole traders and public bodies)
claiming payment of a debt from an individual (including a sole trader). … This Protocol does not apply to business-to-business
debts unless the debtor is a sole trader.” It is additional to any pre-action requirements of any other legislation (which are
already legion in the case of agreements regulated under the CCA) and affords the customer almost limitless opportunities for
procrastination. As the principal sanction for failure to comply would be in costs, it is improbable that any adverse costs order
would be made where a creditor had otherwise complied with all the requirements of the CCA and the FCA Handbook.
