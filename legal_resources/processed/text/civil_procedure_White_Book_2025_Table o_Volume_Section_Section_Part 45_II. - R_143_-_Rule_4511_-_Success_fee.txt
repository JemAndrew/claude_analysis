Rule 45_11
45.11— Success fee 1
7B-26+
(1) A claimant may recover a success fee if he has entered into a funding arrangement of a type specified in rule
43.2(k)(i).
(2) The amount of the success fee shall be 12.5% of the fixed recoverable costs calculated in accordance with rule
45.9(1), disregarding any additional amount which may be included in the fixed recoverable costs by virtue of rule
45.9(2).
(Rule 43.2(k)(i) defines as funding arrangement as including a conditional fee agreement or collective conditional fee
agreement which provides for a success fee.)

Where parties agree the amount of fixed costs, and the only dispute is as to the amount of a success fee, the proceedings should
be issued under r.44.12A in the normal way, and not by reference to Section II of Pt 45 (Costs Practice Direction para.25A.10).
A claimant who has entered into a funding arrangement of the type referred to in r.43.2(k)(i) may recover a “success fee” in
accordance with r.45.11(2). With effect from 1 March 2004, r.45.11(2) was substituted by the Civil Procedure (Amendment
No.5) Rules 2003 (SI 2003/3361), so as to provide that the amount of the success fee which a claimant may recover in
proceedings under Section II of Pt 45 if they have entered into a conditional fee agreement or a collective conditional fee
agreement which provides for a success fee was specified in the rule. Before then r.45.11(2) stated that, if the parties had not
agreed the amount of success fee, “it shall be assessed by the court”.
In Nizami v Butt [2006] EWHC 159 (QB), [2006] 1 W.L.R. 3307 (Simon J.) the defendant opposed the claimant’s claim for a
success fee under r.45.11 on the ground (1) that the CFA was unenforceable, (2) that the claimant therefore had no liability to
his solicitor for the fee, and (3) that therefore there was no liability for which the defendant should be required to indemnify
the claimant. The court rejected these submissions. The court explained that the function of the scheme in Section II of Pt 45

was to set fixed levels of remuneration which, though they might over-reward in some cases and under-reward in others, were
regarded as fair when take as a whole. The court held (amongst other things) that the intention underlying Section II of Pt 45 was
to provide a scheme under which parties could ascertain the appropriate costs payable (inevitably of a small amount) without
reference to the court, and in which there was no place for technical arguments as to whether the funding arrangement strictly
complied with relevant primary and secondary legislation.
The reasoning in Nizami v Butt was adopted by the Court of Appeal inKilby v Gawith [2008] EWCA Civ 812, [2009] 1 W.L.R.
853, CA, where the defendant resisted paying a success fee claimed under r.46.11 by a claimant who had before-the-event
insurance, on the ground that, in those circumstances, it was unreasonable for the claimant to enter a CFA. The Court held
that, although the word “may” is used in para.(1) of r.45.11, that rule confers on a claimant the right to recover the success fee,
provided that the condition precedent (i.e that he had entered into a specified type of funding arrangement) is satisfied. Further,
the amount of the success fee is as stipulated in para.(2) of the rule; the court has no discretion (either derived from r.44.3 or
from any other source) to vary the amount, either for the purpose of allowing more or less than the amount calculated.
